## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I had too much on my plate"
- "I was overcommitted"
- "I'd already said yes to too many things"
- "I was spread too thin"
- "I had too many obligations"
- "I was juggling too much"
- "I couldn't take on anything else"
- "Everyone needed something from me"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I have too much on my plate right now"
- "I'm overcommitted"
- "I just don't have the bandwidth"
- "I'm stretched thin"
- "I have a lot of obligations right now"
- "I can't add another thing"
- "I'm already drowning"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Identity built on being the capacity-machine
- People-pleasing patterns (saying yes to too many things)
- Inability to say no without guilt
- Treating overcommitment as a virtue (working hard, being needed)
- Avoiding the harder internal work by staying outwardly busy
- Genuinely overcommitted with no system for protecting personal growth time
- Fear that saying no will damage relationships or status
- Sometimes: legitimate calendar reality that needs structural fix

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — DISTINGUISHING FROM TIME

This excuse overlaps with TIME but is mechanically different.

**TIME** is "I didn't have the time" — usually involves vague busyness or specific calendar fullness.

**OVERCOMMITMENT** is "I had too many YESES already" — involves a pattern of saying yes to things and not having room for what matters.

The diagnosis matters because the reframes are different. TIME is about scoping the calendar. OVERCOMMITMENT is about the identity that makes them say yes to everything in the first place.

For your ICP specifically — high-earning entrepreneurs — overcommitment is almost always rooted in identity-as-capacity. They've built their self-worth on being the one who handles everything. Saying no feels like failure. Removing things feels like loss.

The work isn't time management. The work is examining the identity that's been saying yes.

**IMPORTANT — DON'T OVER-SOLVE ON THE CALL.** Earlier in this conversation we established the sales-vs-delivery principle: the scoping work itself happens in delivery, not on the sales call. On the call, you acknowledge the legitimacy of the bandwidth concern and defer the actual restructuring to session 1. Don't try to redesign their calendar mid-call.

## SCRIPT PATH

For OVERCOMMITMENT, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, R, and P**. Roots is unusually important here because the pattern is identity-rooted.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you were overcommitted — what specifically were you saying yes to that didn't actually deserve a yes?"

**Present:** "When you say you have too much on your plate — what specifically is on it that doesn't actually need to be there?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can identify lower-priority commitments. Most can. The fact that they can identify yeses-that-shouldn't-have-been-yeses but can't remove them is the pattern.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what you said yes to in the last 6 months that you wish you'd said no to."

**Present:** "Walk me through who's currently in line for your time — the commitments, the people, the obligations."

**What you're listening for:** Who they say yes to and why. Listen for: family obligations they could renegotiate, employee handholding they could delegate, social commitments they could decline, side projects they could pause.

### R — Roots (CRITICAL for this excuse)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn that you had to say yes to everything? When did 'being the one who handles it all' become part of who you are?"

**What you're listening for:** This excuse is almost always rooted in childhood. Listen for:

- Parent who modeled overcommitment (especially mothers — modeling for daughters)
- Being "the responsible one" or "the helpful one" young
- Cultural conditioning (specific ethnic/religious backgrounds value capacity highly)
- Early-career imprinting (hustle culture, "saying yes opens doors")
- Specific identity-shaping moments

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long have you been operating at this level of commitment? Has it always been this way?"

**Present:** "How long have you been this overcommitted? Is this a recent thing or chronic?"

**What you're listening for:** Chronic overcommitment that's been the default for years is identity-rooted. Recent overcommitment from a specific transition (new job, new kid, new business phase) is more situational.

### I — Involved (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Whose priorities are running your calendar? Who do you say yes to even when you don't want to?"

**What you're listening for:** Specific people or relationships where saying no feels impossible. Often spouse, family, key employees, board members, key clients.

### C — Compared to what (rarely needed)

Skip unless they're comparing their busyness to others as proof it's normal.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Identity-as-capacity (chronic, pervasive, rooted)

**Most common for your ICP.** The prospect's sense of self is built on capacity. Saying no feels like a violation of who they are. Removing things feels like failure. The overcommitment is identity-protective.

**How to recognize:**

- Long-standing pattern
- R question surfaces clear childhood origin
- Pervasive across life (not just business)
- Often guilt-laden about saying no
- Treats capacity as moral virtue
- Has tried to set limits and failed because limits felt like betrayal

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "What do you think would happen — to you, to your sense of self — if you actually said no to things and protected your own time?"
- "Who would you be if you weren't the one who handles everything?"
- "What did your parents model about handling things? Whose pattern are you carrying?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What you learned in childhood — that being the responsible one was how you stayed safe / got love / proved your worth — made sense for that situation. AND you're not a kid anymore. The conditions that required you to be the capacity-machine aren't the same. What if the pattern that built your worth then is limiting your growth now?"

- "If your worth was actually inherent — not contingent on what you produce or who you take care of — what would change about how you make commitments?"

- "Notice this: 'I have to say yes' and 'I have to be the one who does it' are very different from 'I choose to.' Where in your life are you actually choosing, and where are you being run by an old story about who you have to be?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Identity-vs-Service Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "There's a difference between someone who serves because they choose to, from overflow — and someone who serves because they have to, from a wound. Which version are you running on right now?"

After they answer:

> "What would it look like to serve from choice instead of from compulsion?"

Why this lands: Names the difference between healthy generosity and compulsive over-giving. Most overcommitted high-performers are the second version but think they're the first. Reveals the actual mechanism.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Yes That's Actually a No** — **Grade: A**

> "Every yes you say to one thing is a no you're saying to something else. When you said yes to [recent commitment that didn't deserve a yes], what did you say no to? What didn't get attention because of it?"

After they answer:

> "Was that trade worth it?"

Why this lands: Makes visible the cost-of-yes that prospects rarely calculate. They think of yes as adding; never as subtracting. Reveals that overcommitment is constant subtraction from what matters.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Modeling Reframe** — **Grade: A (especially for parents)**

> "If your daughter / son grows up and lives the life you're living — the same level of overcommitment, the same identity built on capacity, the same inability to say no — would you be proud, or would you grieve?"

After they answer:

> "What are you modeling for them right now?"

Why this lands: Activates parental concern for what they're passing down. Reveals that the pattern they're running creates the inheritance for their kids. Most parents will not want their children to inherit this. Often a somatic moment.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Operating System for Wrong Environment** — **Grade: A**

> "You learned to be the capacity-machine in a specific environment — childhood where being needed was how you stayed safe or got value. That OS worked for that environment. The environment has changed. You're an adult with resources, relationships, agency. But you're still running the kid's OS. Are you in the same environment? Or running outdated software?"

After they answer:

> "What would the updated OS look like?"

Why this lands: Reframes the identity as adaptive but outdated. Doesn't make the prospect wrong for developing the pattern — names that it served them then and doesn't serve them now. Activates updating as the move rather than blaming.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So the overcommitment isn't really about your calendar — it's about the identity that's been saying yes. We'll work on the actual restructuring in session 1, not now. But you see the pattern. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Important: explicitly defer the calendar work to delivery. Don't try to solve scoping on the sales call.

### PATH B — Acute overcommitment from a specific transition

**Less common but real.** The prospect is genuinely in a temporary high-demand period — new job, new business launch, recent move, new baby, sick family member. The overcommitment isn't identity — it's a moment.

**How to recognize:**

- Recent change that explains the demand
- They can articulate when the period will end
- Not their long-term pattern
- Less identity-charged when describing it
- Specific stressors with specific timelines

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "When does this period end? What's the realistic timeline?"
- "What was your bandwidth like before this transition? Will it return when this passes?"
- "Is this a phase or has it become the new normal?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What if this transition doesn't pass quickly — what if it lasts 6 months, 12 months? When does this work happen?"

- "Are you waiting for the busy to end? In your experience, do high-demand periods end and bandwidth return, or does the next thing fill the space?"

- "What if doing this work IS what handles the transition better, not what gets in the way of it?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Sharpening the Axe Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "There's a story about a woodsman cutting down trees. He's working harder and harder but cutting less. Someone asks 'why don't you sharpen your axe?' He says 'I don't have time — I have to cut these trees.' Are you that woodsman right now?"

After they answer:

> "What if this work is sharpening the axe? What if not doing it is why you're so overcommitted?"

Why this lands: Classic reframe. Reveals that overcommitment is often a symptom of operating with dull tools — the identity work would sharpen the operating capacity. The prospect can construct the logic themselves.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Compound Cost Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Every month you defer this because of the current overcommitment — what gets worse? Your bandwidth issues, your team issues, your stress? Or do they get better?"

After they answer (always: worse):

> "What does it mean to keep deferring the thing that would fix the overcommitment because you're too overcommitted?"

Why this lands: Reveals the recursive problem. They're using the symptom (overcommitment) as the reason to not address the cause (identity that creates overcommitment). The math compounds against them every month they wait.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "Okay, so the current overload is real AND it's a reason to do this work now, not later. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from moving?"

### PATH C — Genuine bandwidth constraint that requires honest scoping

**Less common.** The prospect is genuinely so overwhelmed that adding anything — even something that would ultimately help — is not realistic without removing something else.

**How to recognize:**

- Multiple genuine high-priority commitments
- No clear lower-priority items to remove
- Honest articulation of the real picture
- Not avoiding — actually constrained
- Open to the work but realistic about capacity

**This is sales-vs-delivery territory.** The honest move:

> "I hear that. Let me be direct. The bandwidth piece is real AND it's part of what we'd work on together. The first session, we look at your calendar, identify what comes off the plate to make room for this work, and protect the time. I'm not asking you to find time you don't have. I'm asking you to commit to the work, and we'll create the bandwidth together — but only if you're willing to remove things."

Then the filter question:

> "Are you willing to remove things from your plate to make this work? Because if you're not willing to remove anything, this isn't going to land — and I'd rather know now than discover it in week 4. What's your honest answer?"

**Transition for Path C:**

If yes: move to close with the commitment to scope in session 1.

If no: qualify out respectfully.

> "Then this isn't the right time. Come back to me when you're ready to actually protect the time. I'd rather have you in 6 months when you can commit fully than in 2 weeks when you can't."

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path:

> "Aside from the bandwidth piece, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the overcommitment — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as something we'll handle in delivery — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 OVERCOMMITMENT phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Distinguish from TIME** — if prospect uses phrases like "had too many yeses" or "stretched thin" or references identity-as-capacity, this is OVERCOMMITMENT. If it's more about "didn't have time" without the yes-pattern, route to TIME.
3. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, R highlighted
4. Deploy R early to surface identity rooting
5. Based on response, diagnose path (A identity-as-capacity, B acute transition, C genuine constraint)
6. Load matched reframe card
7. **Lock the sim from over-solving on the call** — defer calendar scoping to delivery
8. Check for concession
9. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm running a $5M business, sitting on two boards, mentoring two younger founders, and managing my family. There's genuinely no room."
- "I've structured my life for high output. Adding anything else isn't realistic without restructuring."
- "I'm efficient, but I'm at capacity."

Type A often presents identity-as-capacity dressed in operational language. Deploy R question to surface where the "I have to handle everything" identity installed. The Modeling Reframe and Identity-vs-Service work well.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Everyone needs something from me. I can't say no."
- "I'm just buried. Family, work, everything."
- "I want to but I literally don't have room."

Type B often presents more emotional overwhelm. Path A is common but presents differently — more victim-feeling than identity-pride. The Yes-That's-Actually-a-No reframe lands hard. Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't shame the overcommitment
- Honor it as a real pattern with real history
- After R question, slow down significantly — childhood material requires space
- For Path A, the Modeling Reframe often produces somatic aha — hold the silence
- Defer practical scoping to delivery without dismissing the bandwidth concern

### Friction budget notes

OVERCOMMITMENT can take significant budget if Path A surfaces deeply. That's often appropriate — it's identity work that may connect to Layer 3 fear. The fear underneath overcommitment is usually fear of being worthless if not productive/needed/capable. If the somatic aha emerges here, you may be in the fear extraction territory already.

If Path B or C, the handling is faster — name the pattern, defer scoping to delivery, sweep.

### Special: when overcommitment connects to martyrdom

Some prospects, especially women, present overcommitment as moral identity — "I'm a giver," "I take care of people." The move:

> "Let me name something carefully. There's a difference between generosity and martyrdom. Generosity comes from overflow — you have plenty and you give. Martyrdom comes from depletion — you give what you don't have and resent it underneath. Which version are you running right now?"

If they admit martyrdom, that's the work. The reframe is about restoring overflow, not stopping giving.

### Special: the "I'll get to it when things calm down" deflection

> "I just need to get through this quarter / year / project, then I can focus on this."

The move:

> "How many quarters / years / projects have you said that about? What's the actual data — do the things you defer until 'things calm down' ever actually happen, or do they get deferred indefinitely?"

Reveals the pattern. Most prospects realize their "wait until calm" has been operating for years and the calm never comes.
