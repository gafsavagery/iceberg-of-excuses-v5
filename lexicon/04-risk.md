## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I wasn't willing to take that kind of risk back then"
- "It felt too risky at the time"
- "I had too much on the line to bet on it"
- "I couldn't afford for it to not work"
- "I was being conservative"
- "I'd just taken a hit and couldn't risk another one"
- "I wasn't in a position to gamble"
- "I needed to protect what I had"

### Present-tense (active)

- "It feels really risky"
- "What if it doesn't work?"
- "I can't afford to lose another $46K on something that doesn't pan out"
- "I have too much on the line right now"
- "I need to be careful"
- "I'm trying to de-risk my life, not add more risk"
- "What's the guarantee?"
- "If this fails, I'm in real trouble"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Treating inaction as the safe option when it isn't
- Using "risk" as cover for fear of commitment
- Asymmetric attention to downside vs. upside
- Past loss that hasn't been processed and is dictating current decisions
- Conflating "risky" with "uncomfortable"
- Identity as "the cautious one" or "the responsible one"
- Avoiding the vulnerability of betting on themselves
- Sometimes: genuine real risk that should be honored

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This is the most asymmetric excuse in the lexicon. The prospect is treating one path (acting) as risky and the other path (not acting) as safe. The whole reframe rests on making both visible.

**Do NOT minimize the risk they're naming.** That breaks trust. The investment IS real money. The time IS real commitment. The outcome IS uncertain. Pretending otherwise loses the prospect immediately.

**The move is to make the unseen risk equally visible.** Not acting has its own cost, its own risk profile, its own outcome. The prospect has been treating it as zero. Your job is to make it real.

This is Type 4 reframe territory (Comparison Expansion from foundational principles).

## SCRIPT PATH

For RISK, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, C, and P**. Specifically and Compared-to-what are critical.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say it felt too risky — what specifically were you worried about losing?"

**Present:** "When you say risky — what specifically are you afraid would happen if you did this?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate the actual feared outcome or whether "risky" is vague. Most prospects can't get specific. They have a generalized risk feeling but no concrete scenario. That vagueness is the lever.

If they CAN get specific, listen for whether it's a real risk (financial ruin, business collapse) or a low-probability fear (it might not be a perfect fit).

### C — Compared to what (CRITICAL — deploy immediately after S)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what? Compared to the risk of staying exactly where you are for another two years?"

**What you're listening for:** Their willingness to consider that not acting is also a path with its own risks. Often they've never thought about it that way. The Compared-to-what question forces the comparison they've been avoiding.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through your thinking back then. What was the risk calculation that led you to not move forward?"

**Present:** "Walk me through your risk calculation. What's the actual math you're running in your head?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they did a real cost-benefit analysis or whether "risky" is shorthand for "scary." Real risk calculations include both paths. Avoidant risk thinking only weighs the action side.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long have you been making decisions based on 'too risky'? Has that always been your default?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether risk-aversion is situational or pattern. If they've been "too cautious" for a decade and have the data to show what that's cost them, the pattern itself becomes the lever.

### R — Roots (deploy when risk-aversion feels identity-coded)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to be this cautious with investments in yourself? Has that been a family pattern or did you develop it from experience?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who lost money in a bad investment, a religious or cultural background that emphasizes security over growth, or an early experience of loss that imprinted.

### I — Involved (rarely needed for RISK)

Skip unless the risk-framing is being attributed to someone else's influence ("my financial advisor wouldn't approve").

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Asymmetric risk thinking (acting is risky, not acting is safe)

**Most common case.** The prospect has been weighing the cost of action without weighing the cost of inaction. They've been treating the status quo as zero-risk when it isn't.

**How to recognize:**

- Can name what they'd lose by acting (money, time, comfort)
- Can't articulate what they're losing by NOT acting
- Treats "the same as now" as the alternative when it isn't
- Hasn't projected the current trajectory forward
- Talks about action-risk in specific terms, inaction-risk in vague terms

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "If you did nothing for the next two years and everything stayed the same — what would that cost you?"
- "Has 'staying the same' been working? What's the data on that path?"
- "When you imagine your life five years from now if you don't do this work — what does that look like?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You've described what acting would risk. Walk me through what NOT acting has risked over the last two years. What's that path actually cost you so far?"

- "If you'd done this work two years ago — even if it had been imperfect — where would you be today versus where you actually are?"

- "What if 'safe' has been the riskier path the whole time? What if the real risk has been continuing to bet on a strategy that's been losing for two years?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Status Quo Investment** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you're holding a stock. It's been declining 5% a year for the last three years. Holding it feels like the 'safe' choice because you haven't sold. But what's actually safer — holding the declining stock, or putting the money somewhere that might grow?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference between that situation and where you've been with this problem?"

Why this lands: Financial analogy works for analytical prospects. Reveals that "doing nothing" isn't neutral — it's an active choice with compounding cost. The pattern of treating decline as safety becomes visible.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Surgeon Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "If a surgeon told you 'you have a slow-growing condition. The surgery has a 90% success rate but it's invasive. Or we can do nothing and you'll definitely get worse over the next five years' — which is the risky path?"

After they answer:

> "What's different about how you've been framing this situation?"

Why this lands: Medical framing makes the asymmetry obvious. Premium prospects can immediately see that "doing nothing" with a known declining condition is more risky than the procedure. Reveals their current framing as incoherent.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Business Investment Comparison** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "In your business, you make investment decisions all the time — hires, marketing spend, capital investments. You never get 100% certainty on any of them, right? What's your actual decision framework when you're investing in growth?"

After they answer:

> "How is your decision framework here different from how you'd evaluate a business investment of the same size?"

Why this lands: Activates their professional decision-making frame. They'd never run their business with the risk-aversion they're applying to themselves. Reveals the double standard.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So the risk wasn't really action — it was the cost of continuing to wait. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — Past loss is driving current risk-aversion

**Common.** The prospect has a specific past loss (business failure, bad investment, painful financial event) that's making them gun-shy now. The aversion is rooted in real prior experience.

**How to recognize:**

- References a specific past loss
- The loss happened years ago but still drives behavior
- Defensive when discussing it
- Treats every investment now through the lens of "what if this is like that time"
- May say "I've lost enough money" or "I can't afford to be wrong again"

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Tell me about that past loss. What happened? What did you learn from it?"
- "What's been different about your decision-making since then?"
- "Has the caution been worth it? What's it cost you in opportunities?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You learned to be cautious after that loss. That makes sense. But what's the cost of being cautious in EVERY decision since then? Has the over-caution cost you more than the original loss?"

- "If past-you could see future-you still hesitating because of what happened back then — what would past-you say? Would past-you want the loss to define every future decision?"

- "There's a difference between learning FROM a loss and being CONTROLLED by it. Where would you place yourself on that spectrum?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Burned Investor** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine someone who lost money in a stock crash 10 years ago. They never invested again. They kept all their money in cash. Today, the cash is worth 30% less in real terms because of inflation. The 'safety' cost them more than the original loss would have. Does that pattern feel familiar?"

After they answer:

> "What's been the cumulative cost of your post-loss caution?"

Why this lands: Reveals that over-correction after loss often costs more than the original loss. The "safe" position erodes over time. The prospect has to construct that their caution has been compounding cost.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Trauma Response Reframe** — **Grade: B+**

> "After a car accident, some people stop driving. That's a trauma response, not a rational decision. They've taken a single bad event and let it permanently restrict their life. Is what happened with that past loss still teaching you, or is it just restricting you?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference between honoring what you learned and letting it run your life?"

Why this lands: Names the difference between trauma-driven caution and learned caution. Reveals that the prospect may be in a frozen response, not a wise one. Use carefully — don't pathologize.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "Okay. So the past loss is real, AND letting it drive every future decision has its own cost. What else has been in the way?"

### PATH C — Genuine real risk that should be honored

**Less common but real.** Some prospects genuinely cannot afford the investment without serious financial strain. The risk they're naming is real and severe.

**How to recognize:**

- Specific financial situation that makes this a real stretch (not "tight" — actually constrained)
- The investment would meaningfully affect their family's stability
- They're not avoiding — they're being honest about real limits
- The fear has specific, articulated content

**This is not a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation.** The honest move is to not sell into a real financial constraint.

**The move:**

> "I hear you. If this would put your family in a real bind, then this isn't the right move right now. Let me ask honestly — is it that you can't afford this, or that it would be a stretch but doable? Because those are different conversations."

If genuinely can't afford: 

> "Then let's not force this. I'd rather you come back to me in six months when the financial picture is different than have you stretch into something that creates real strain at home. Here's what I'd want you to think about between now and then..."

If it's a stretch but doable: that's Path A territory. They're using "risk" to mean "uncomfortable," and the reframe applies.

**Transition language for Path C:**

If qualified out: end the call respectfully with a future-pointing message.

If reframed to Path A: deploy Path A reframes.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe:

> "Aside from the risk piece, what else has been getting in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the worry about the investment — what else?"
- "Setting risk aside as not the real issue — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 RISK phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options card with S and C highlighted as primary
3. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path (A asymmetric thinking, B past loss, C genuine constraint)
4. Load matched reframe card
5. Closer deploys reframe
6. Check for concession
7. Sweep transition or loop

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm not opposed to investment, but I evaluate every major spend with rigor. The downside scenarios matter."
- "If this doesn't pan out, that's $46K I can't recover and three months of time I can't get back."
- "I've made bad bets before. I'm careful about which ones I make now."

Type A defends with rational-sounding analysis. Activate the asymmetric framing by surfacing what they've been NOT calculating (the cost of inaction). The business investment comparison (Analogy 3) works well.

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I just can't afford another mistake."
- "I'm scared of throwing more money at something that doesn't work."
- "I've already lost so much — I can't take another hit."

Type B defends with emotional risk-aversion. Acknowledge the prior pain first (warmth before challenge). Then deploy the burned investor or trauma response analogy. Move slowly with this one.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't minimize the risk they're naming
- Match their seriousness, then expand the frame
- After participatory analogies, sit in silence long enough for them to construct the comparison
- Don't argue with the risk — expand what's being compared

### Friction budget notes

RISK is often where the prospect's deepest defense lives because it sounds rational. They've been telling themselves "I'm being smart" for years. Reframing this directly is high-leverage but high-friction. Don't spend all your budget here — get the key reframe to land and lateral sweep. The fear underneath risk (usually fear of being wrong, fear of being seen failing) surfaces in the fear extraction phase.

### Special: when the prospect asks "what's the guarantee"

This is often a RISK objection wearing a different costume. The move:

> "Honest answer? There's no guarantee that you'll do the work. There IS a guarantee that the work works for people who do it. Same way there's no guarantee your gym membership makes you fit — the membership doesn't lift the weights. But the gym works for everyone who shows up consistently."

This honest framing converts the guarantee question from "promise me an outcome" to "what's required from me." Different conversation.

### Adjacent: "What if this isn't the right fit?"

Variant of RISK framed as fit concern. The move:

> "Fair question. The way you'll know if it's a fit is by doing the first two weeks of the work. If by week 2 you and I both feel this isn't right for you, we have a conversation about it. What I won't do is sell you into something that's not the right fit just because you're already in. But we won't know until you start, and you won't start if you stay in evaluation forever. So the actual question is whether you're willing to test it, or you're going to keep evaluating from the outside."
