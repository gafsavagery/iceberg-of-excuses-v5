## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (rarely surfaces in past tense)

- "Things were going pretty well so I didn't push for more"
- "I was satisfied with where I was"
- "Life felt good enough — I didn't want to upset the apple cart"

### Present-tense (DOMINANT)

- "Honestly, things are pretty good"
- "I'm doing fine"
- "I'm in a good spot"
- "I'm not unhappy or anything"
- "Life is actually pretty okay"
- "I don't really have problems"
- "I'm comfortable"
- "I've got nothing to complain about"
- "It's not bad enough to do something dramatic about"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Genuine contentment (rare for prospects on a coaching call)
- Sophisticated avoidance of confronting what isn't working
- Identity as "the grateful one" or "the not-needy one"
- Comparing down ("I have it better than most people") to avoid examining what's actually broken
- Numbing or dissociation from real dissatisfaction
- Fear of looking ungrateful or entitled
- Avoiding the vulnerability of admitting wanting more
- Past disappointment that taught them to lower expectations
- Sometimes: actual satisfaction (in which case they shouldn't be on a sales call)

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — THIS IS THE TRICKY ONE

For your ICP, "life is okay" is almost never true on a coaching call. Here's why:

Prospects don't book sales calls because they're satisfied. They book because something is pulling them toward more — or pushing them away from where they are. By the time they're on the call, there's a gap they're trying to close.

But "I'm content" is the most socially acceptable defense possible. It sounds mature, grateful, healthy. It deflects the entire conversation because you can't really argue with "I'm satisfied." Any pushback sounds like a coach trying to manufacture dissatisfaction to sell something.

**The handling requires extreme care.** You cannot tell a prospect they're not actually content. That's both invasive and impossible to prove. You have to make them surface their own dissatisfaction in their own words.

**The move:** Don't argue with the contentment. Ask careful questions that surface what they actually want that they don't have. The gap between current state and wanted state IS the dissatisfaction, named without using the word.

**Critical reminder:** If the prospect truly is content, this isn't a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation. Some people genuinely shouldn't buy. Sell into a real "content" and you create a refund or a bad outcome.

## SCRIPT PATH

For LIFE IS OKAY, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, and a unique gap-question**. The standard SCRIPT applies but with a twist — you're not interrogating the contentment, you're examining what's still unspoken.

### S — Specifically (deploy carefully — not aggressively)

**Present:** "When you say things are okay — what specifically is going well? Walk me through what's working."

**What you're listening for:** Their actual answer. Most prospects will list a few things and run out quickly. The thinness of the list is the lever. Also listen for hedges — "okay," "fine," "good enough" — these are not "great" or "thriving" words.

### T — Tell me the story

**Present:** "Walk me through a typical day for you. What does life actually look like?"

**What you're listening for:** Texture of their day. Are they describing energy, meaning, connection? Or are they describing routine, capability, getting-through? Most "content" prospects on coaching calls are describing functional but unalive routines.

### THE GAP QUESTION (unique to this excuse — deploy this above all)

**Present:** "If you couldn't say you're content — if you had to be honest about what you actually want that you don't have right now — what would you say?"

This is the move. It bypasses the social safety of "I'm content" and asks them to name what's missing without using the word "missing." Most prospects will pause here. The pause is the work.

**Variant:**

> "If a friend you really trusted asked you 'what's the thing you don't tell people about?' — what would the answer be?"

This converts contentment to confession territory. Most prospects have something they don't tell people. That something is the work.

### P — Point in time

**Present:** "How long have you been in this 'pretty okay' state? Is this new, or has it been like this for a while?"

**What you're listening for:** Long-term plateau is often the actual signal. The prospect has been in "fine" for years and the absence of dramatic dissatisfaction has been keeping them stuck.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Present:** "Where did you learn to settle for okay? Has 'good enough' been a pattern across your life?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who taught gratitude as virtue ("be thankful for what you have"), a religious background that pathologizes wanting more, or early disappointment that calibrated their expectations down.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip unless they reference others as the reason for contentment ("my family is happy so I should be happy too").

### C — Compared to what (CRITICAL for this excuse)

**Present:** "Compared to what? When you say you're content — content compared to what alternative? Compared to what you really want?"

**What you're listening for:** Most "content" framings depend on hidden downward comparisons. They're content compared to people who have it worse, not compared to what they actually want. Surfacing the comparison reveals the framing.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Sophisticated avoidance disguised as contentment

**Most common for your ICP on a coaching call.** The prospect has real dissatisfaction but has learned that naming it directly is uncomfortable or risks looking ungrateful. They've adopted "I'm content" as a polite shield.

**How to recognize:**

- Booked a call despite being "content" (the booking is the data)
- Uses hedge language ("pretty okay," "fine," "not bad")
- The list of "what's going well" is thin or generic
- The gap question produces a pause or shift in energy
- They booked the call for some reason they haven't named

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "You're on a call about working on your identity-level patterns. People who are truly content don't book those calls. What pulled you here?"
- "If everything was truly okay, what would have to be true that isn't true right now?"
- "What was happening in your head this morning when you decided to actually take this call?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "There's a version of you that booked this call for a reason. That version knew something. What did she know that the 'I'm content' version is trying to manage around?"

- "What does the most ambitious version of you — the version you don't usually let speak — want that the current version is pretending not to want?"

- "Where are you settling? Be specific. Where in your life are you accepting a 7 when a 10 is possible?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Two Voices** — **Grade: A**

> "There are two versions of you in this conversation. One is the polite, reasonable, 'I'm content' version that doesn't want to be needy or ungrateful. The other is the version that booked this call and is sitting somewhere in you wanting more. Which one is talking right now? And which one is more honest?"

After they answer:

> "What does the second voice actually want to say?"

Why this lands: Names the split between performed contentment and actual desire. Reveals that there are two versions of them in the conversation, and the louder one isn't necessarily the more honest one. Creates space for the suppressed voice to speak.

**ANALOGY 2 — The 7 Out of 10 Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "On a scale of 1-10, where is your life actually? Honest answer."

If they say 7 or 8 (most common):

> "Okay, 7. What's the difference between 7 and 10? What would have to be different?"

After they answer:

> "Are you willing to live the rest of your life at a 7 when you can see what 10 looks like? Or does that gap actually matter?"

Why this lands: Forces them to name a specific gap rather than abstractly defend contentment. Once the gap is named, the work is to address it. The framing reveals that "content" is often 7-out-of-10 dressed as enough.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Deathbed Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you're 80, looking back at the version of you sitting on this call right now. You're 80 and you've lived another 40 years exactly the way this version is living. Are you proud of those 40 years? Or do you wish you'd done something different at this exact moment?"

After they answer:

> "What is 80-year-old you trying to tell current you right now?"

Why this lands: Activates the long-arc perspective. Most prospects know intuitively that another 40 years of "okay" is regret material. The deathbed version of them is rarely satisfied with "content."

**ANALOGY 4 — The Suppressed Ambition Reframe** — **Grade: A for women / sometimes Type A men**

> "What's the thing you stopped letting yourself want because wanting it felt too risky, too greedy, or too ambitious? The thing you wouldn't say at a dinner party because it would sound too much?"

After they answer:

> "Why did that thing get put in a box? What were you protecting yourself from?"

Why this lands: Goes directly to suppressed desire. Many people, especially women, have learned to want less. The reframe gives them permission to want what they actually want.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So the 'content' framing is actually a way you've been managing the gap between where you are and where you want to be. Setting that framing aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — Lowered expectations from past disappointment

**Common.** The prospect was once ambitious or wanting more, got hurt or disappointed, and recalibrated expectations downward. Now operates from "expecting less means I won't be disappointed." Contentment is a defense mechanism, not actual satisfaction.

**How to recognize:**

- Reference to past disappointment, failure, or hurt
- "I learned to want less"
- "I used to be more ambitious"
- Bittersweet tone when describing what they have
- Resigned-feeling rather than energized

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Were you always this content, or did you used to want more? What changed?"
- "Is wanting less a value, or is it protection?"
- "When did you decide to recalibrate what you'd allow yourself to want?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What if 'lowering expectations' isn't wisdom — it's a wound? What if the version of you that wanted more was right, and the version that decided to want less is the injured one?"

- "Who would you be if you let yourself want what you actually want without protection? Not what's reasonable. Not what's grateful. What you actually want."

- "What's the cost of having lowered the ceiling on your own life?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Caged Animal Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Animals raised in captivity often won't leave the cage even when it's opened. They've adapted to the size of the cage. The cage feels safe. AND the cage is still a cage. Where in your life have you adapted to a cage that you could actually leave?"

After they answer:

> "Why is it still closed in your mind even if the door is open?"

Why this lands: Reveals that adaptation to limitation isn't the same as satisfaction. The prospect can construct that they've been operating in a smaller space than necessary because the space became familiar.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Dimmer Switch** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you have a dimmer switch on your life. After [past disappointment], you turned it down. Maybe down to 60%. It was protective. Less risk of disappointment at 60% than at 100%. AND you've been living at 60% ever since. What does turning it back up look like?"

After they answer:

> "What's been the cost of running your life at 60% all these years?"

Why this lands: Visualization of the deliberate dimming. Most prospects can locate the moment they turned down their own brightness. The cost of dimming becomes visible — they've been seeing less, feeling less, living less.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "So 'content' has actually been 'dimmed.' Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from turning the brightness back up?"

### PATH C — Actually content

**Genuinely rare on a coaching call.** Some prospects truly are satisfied with their lives. They've examined it honestly, they have what they want, they're in alignment. They booked the call for adjacent reasons (curiosity about the framework, recommended by a friend, looking for one specific thing).

**How to recognize:**

- Specific articulation of what's truly working
- No hedge language ("great," "thriving," not "okay")
- Body language is calm and grounded, not avoidant
- The gap question produces no significant shift
- They can articulate WHY they took the call — and it's not about dissatisfaction

**This is not a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation.**

**The honest move:**

> "Honest question. If you're truly content — and you might be — what brought you to this call? Because most of my clients come from a place of knowing something isn't right. If you don't have that pull, then this might not be the right work for you, and that's okay."

If they have a specific reason (curiosity, looking for one piece, recommended): explore whether that's enough to justify the engagement. Often it isn't.

If they don't have a specific reason: qualify out gracefully.

> "Sounds like you're in a really good place. Honor that. I'd rather have you come back if something shifts than pull you into work you don't actually need."

**You'll lose this sale. The integrity is more important. The prospect will remember.**

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe:

> "So the 'content' framing has been hiding what you actually want. Aside from that, what else has been keeping you from going after it?"

For Path C qualify-out: no sweep. End respectfully.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 LIFE IS OKAY phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Flag this as a high-care excuse** — needs careful tonal handling
3. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, and the gap question highlighted
4. **Prompt the closer to deploy the gap question** ("if you couldn't say you're content...")
5. Based on response, diagnose path (A avoidance, B dimmed, C actually content)
6. Load matched reframe card
7. **For Path C, lock the sim into qualification-out flow** — no reframe option
8. Closer deploys reframe
9. Check for concession or somatic aha
10. Sweep transition or qualify out

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Honestly, I've built a great life. The business is doing well, my family is good, I'm in a strong place."
- "I don't have anything to complain about. I'm grateful for where I am."
- "I'm not unhappy. I just want to see if there's more here."

Type A often uses sophisticated language to defend the contentment. Deploy the gap question with warmth. The 7-out-of-10 reframe works well — analytical types can engage with the specific gap. Also the deathbed reframe.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I have what I need. I shouldn't complain."
- "Other people have it way worse than me."
- "I'm just trying to be grateful."

Type B often uses comparison-down or gratitude framing. Path A or B is most common. The suppressed ambition reframe and the dimmer switch land hard. Lead with permission to want.

### Voice tone notes — CRITICAL

- Don't argue the contentment
- Don't manufacture dissatisfaction
- Curiosity-driven questions, not interrogation
- The gap question is delivered gently, almost as a wondering aloud
- After participatory analogies, hold significant silence
- Make space for them to admit what they couldn't admit before

### Friction budget notes

This excuse can take significant budget because the prospect's defense is socially-sanctioned. You can't push through it — you have to invite around it. The handling is slower than other excuses. But it's high-leverage because once the suppressed desire surfaces, the prospect is fully engaged.

### Special: the gratitude trap

Some prospects, especially those with spiritual or religious backgrounds, frame contentment as moral virtue ("I'm trying to be grateful"). The move:

> "Gratitude and ambition aren't opposites. You can be fully grateful for what you have AND fully committed to growing into more. They're not in conflict — unless gratitude has become a way to suppress wanting more. Which version are you running?"

This separates healthy gratitude from gratitude-as-suppression. Most prospects can see the difference once it's named.

### Special: when the prospect breaks down

The gap question or the deathbed reframe sometimes produces unexpected emotional response. The prospect realizes they've been suppressing something for years. If this happens, STOP. Don't push to close. Be present.

> "Take your time. There's something real here."

Let them sit with whatever surfaced. This is often the deepest material in the call. The relationship being built in that moment matters more than the next move.

### Special: when "content" connects to age

Older prospects (50+) sometimes use contentment to defend not changing at their age. "I'm content where I am, it's late to make changes." The move:

> "Late compared to what? Late compared to dying at the version of yourself you're not actually happy with? Or late compared to the average person who never reinvented at this age — and you're not the average person, you're someone who booked this call?"

Activates that "late" is a story. Reveals that some prospects have used their age as permission not to confront what isn't working.
