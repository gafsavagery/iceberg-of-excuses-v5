## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I couldn't afford it back then"
- "Money was tight at the time"
- "I didn't have the funds for it"
- "I was being financially conservative"
- "I wanted to but the budget wasn't there"
- "I had other financial priorities"
- "It was outside what I could spend at the time"
- "I would have if I'd had the money"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I can't afford it"
- "It's a lot of money"
- "That's outside my budget"
- "I'd need to look at the numbers"
- "It's not in my budget right now"
- "The investment is significant"
- "I'd have to figure out how to fund it"
- "It's more than I'd planned to spend"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Real financial constraint that limits options (rare for ICP)
- Using "money" as a feeling rather than a financial fact
- Avoiding the perceived value question by framing it as affordability
- Identity as "the financially careful one"
- Spending money on lower-value things while claiming inability to afford higher-value
- Asking permission to spend on themselves
- Past financial trauma affecting current discretionary decisions
- Conflating "expensive" with "not worth it"
- Sometimes: actual budget reality that should be honored

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — DISTINGUISHING FROM DEBT

This excuse overlaps with DEBT but is mechanically different.

**DEBT** is "I'm carrying obligations that prevent adding new commitments." It's about what they already owe.

**MONEY** is "the investment itself is too expensive / outside my budget / not affordable." It's about the cost of the offer relative to their resources.

The reframes differ. DEBT examines whether existing obligations actually prevent investment. MONEY examines whether the investment is genuinely beyond reach versus emotionally uncomfortable.

For high-earning entrepreneur ICP, "I can't afford $46K" is almost never literally true. They've spent more on cars, vacations, equipment, hires. The "can't afford" is usually "haven't decided it's worth it." Different conversation.

## SCRIPT PATH

For MONEY, the most leveraged letters are **S, C, T, and P**. Specifically and Compared-to-what are the critical openers.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you couldn't afford it — what specifically did the financial picture look like then?"

**Present:** "When you say you can't afford it — what specifically does 'can't afford' mean to you?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate the financial picture in concrete terms. Most high-earning prospects can't get specific because "can't afford" is emotional, not financial. The vagueness is the lever.

If they CAN get specific (income, cash flow, obligations), you have actual data to work with.

### C — Compared to what (CRITICAL — deploy immediately after S)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what? Compared to what you make? Compared to what you've spent on other things recently?"

**What you're listening for:** Anchoring the "a lot" or "can't afford" to specific comparisons. Most prospects can't honestly say the investment is "a lot" relative to their income. They mean it's "a lot" relative to comfort or relative to other unimportant spending.

Push specifically: "A lot compared to your monthly business revenue? A lot compared to what you spent on [recent discretionary expense]?"

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through your financial picture at that point. Income, cash flow, what you were spending on."

**Present:** "Walk me through how you'd evaluate this as a financial decision. What's the process?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have an actual investment-evaluation process or whether "can't afford" is a pre-decided conclusion. Most prospects haven't actually done the math — they have a feeling about the price.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long has money felt tight? Has it been a chronic state, or specific to a period?"

**What you're listening for:** Chronic "money is tight" framing usually reveals identity rather than current reality. If they've felt tight for 5+ years across growing income, it's a money story, not a financial constraint.

### R — Roots (deploy when money-anxiety feels identity-coded)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to think about money this way? Did you grow up around scarcity?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces childhood financial anxiety, parents who modeled scarcity even when affluent, religious or cultural conditioning around money as moral. The money story may be inherited and inappropriate to their current financial reality.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip unless they reference partner's financial views or an advisor's input.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Affordability framing that masks the actual question (perceived value)

**Most common case for ICP.** The prospect can afford the investment but hasn't decided it's worth the cost. They're framing it as "can't afford" when the real question is "I'm not sure this is worth $46K." Different conversation.

**How to recognize:**

- Income or business revenue substantially exceeds the investment
- Has made discretionary purchases recently that contradict "can't afford"
- Vague about the actual financial picture
- The question "compared to what" reveals there's no real budget constraint
- Tone shifts to defensive when pressed for specifics

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "What have you spent on in the last 6 months that wasn't strictly essential? Vacations, equipment, hires, other investments?"
- "If this work was guaranteed to 3x your business in 12 months, would the $46K feel like 'can't afford'?"
- "Honest question — is it that you can't afford it, or that you haven't decided it's worth it?"

The last question often produces the somatic shift. Most prospects will admit, sometimes reluctantly, that the second framing is more accurate.

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If I came to you and said 'I'll give you $100,000, but you have to give me $46,000 right now' — would you find the $46,000? Of course you would. So the money's not the constraint — it's whether you believe the value is there. Where's your actual hesitation?"

- "You've spent on [things they mentioned]. So money has been found and allocated. The question isn't whether you can find $46K. The question is whether you believe this is worth more than the next $46K you'd otherwise spend. What would have to be true for you to believe that?"

- "What's the cost of staying where you are for another two years versus the cost of investing in change? Which one's actually the expensive option?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The $10K for $5K Move (Tristan, verbatim core)** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you something. If I said 'here's $10,000 in my hand, take it — but you have to give me $5,000 right now to get it' — would you find the $5K?"

After they answer (yes):

> "So the money's not the constraint. The reason you'd hesitate on this isn't that you can't find $46K — it's that you're not yet certain you'll get back something worth more than $46K. That's a value question, not an affordability question. What does the value question actually look like for you?"

Why this lands: Devastatingly clean. Reveals that "can't afford" is almost always "don't yet believe in the return." The prospect can't argue with the math. Tristan deploys this constantly because it works across categories. Use this one first.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Outcome-of-the-Step Reframe (Tristan, verbatim)** — **Grade: A**

> "The reason you do this work is to break through the bottleneck that's been keeping your money — and your time, and your relationships — stuck. If we use 'money is tight' as the reason to not do the work that would change the money situation, we guarantee the money situation stays the same. Does that math work?"

After they answer:

> "What if this investment is the lever that changes the money situation, not something we wait until the money situation is different before doing?"

Why this lands: Reveals the recursive logic. They're using the symptom (tight money) as the reason not to address the cause (identity that produces tight money). The math becomes obvious when surfaced.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Business Investment Comparison** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "In your business, when you invest in growth — a key hire, a major piece of equipment, a marketing spend — what's your actual decision framework? Do you evaluate based on 'can I afford it' or based on 'will the return justify the cost'?"

After they answer:

> "Why are you applying a different framework to investing in yourself?"

Why this lands: Activates their CEO decision-making frame. They'd never run their business with the affordability-anxiety they're applying to themselves. Reveals the double standard.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Marriage / Reciprocity Reframe (Tristan, verbatim)** — **Grade: B+**

> "It's like saying you want to get into a marriage and you want love and respect and trust — but you're not willing to give those things yourself first. What kind of results would you get?"

After they answer:

> "What does that tell you about expecting transformation without commitment?"

Why this lands: Reframes the financial commitment as the give-side of a reciprocal exchange. The prospect can't get transformation without paying for transformation. Use selectively — works best for prospects who frame everything as "I need to know what I'll get first."

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So the money piece isn't actually the constraint — it's the value question. We'll keep working on that. Aside from the affordability framing, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — Money story inherited from childhood / outdated to current reality

**Common.** The prospect's current finances substantially exceed the investment but their money story is calibrated to a different era — childhood scarcity, early-career struggle, past financial trauma. They feel poor even when they're not.

**How to recognize:**

- R question surfaces childhood financial conditioning
- Current finances are objectively comfortable
- "Money is tight" feels like an ongoing identity, not a recent fact
- May reference parents' financial anxiety or family scarcity narratives
- Some shame or defensiveness around discussing money openly

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How does your current financial reality compare to what you grew up with?"
- "Has your money story updated as your income has grown, or are you still operating from the old framework?"
- "If a friend in your current financial situation came to you for advice about this investment, what would you tell them?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What you learned about money in childhood made sense for that situation. AND you're not in that situation anymore. The money story is from a different chapter. What's been the cost of carrying that old story forward into your current reality?"

- "If you were objectively looking at your current financial position — not through the childhood lens — what would a healthy financial decision-maker say about this?"

- "Whose voice is in your head when you say you can't afford this? Is it actually yours, or did you inherit it?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Outdated Wardrobe** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you went through a period of significant change — weight loss, new job, new life — and you're still wearing clothes from the old chapter that don't fit. Why are you still wearing them?"

After they answer:

> "Your money story might be like those clothes. It fit a different version of your finances. Why are you still wearing it?"

Why this lands: Visceral and immediate. Reveals that they're operating from an outdated framework. Makes the disconnect between past identity and current reality obvious.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Trust Fund Kid Mirror** — **Grade: B+**

> "Imagine someone who inherited wealth — they have $5 million in the bank and they still feel anxious about money, still won't invest in themselves, still treats every expense like it might break them. Would you say their problem is the money, or their relationship to money?"

After they answer:

> "How is your situation different?"

Why this lands: Activates them seeing the absurdity of money-anxiety in someone who clearly has enough. Most prospects will recognize the parallel in their own situation.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "So the 'can't afford' is more about the inherited money story than the actual finances. Setting that aside, what else has been in the way?"

### PATH C — Genuine financial constraint that should be honored

**Less common for ICP but happens.** The prospect genuinely cannot afford the investment without serious financial strain. The numbers don't work and stretching would cause real harm.

**How to recognize:**

- Income meaningfully below investment relative to obligations
- Can articulate specific numbers that don't pencil out
- Adding this expense would cause real strain (missed payments, family stress)
- Not avoiding — honest about real limits
- Cash flow is genuinely constrained

**This is not a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation.**

**The honest move:**

> "I appreciate you being straight with me. Let me ask — is it that you can't afford this, or that it would be a stretch but doable? Different conversations."

If genuinely can't afford:

> "Then this isn't the right move right now. I'd rather have you come back in 6-12 months when the financial picture is different than have you stretch into something that creates real strain. What would have to change financially for this to be possible?"

If they can articulate a path:

> "Okay, let's stay in touch. I'll check in with you in [reasonable timeframe]. When the financial picture supports it, we'll talk."

End the call with respect. Don't sell into real constraint.

If it's a stretch but doable: that's Path A territory. They're using "can't afford" to mean "uncomfortable," and the reframe applies.

**Transition language for Path C:**

If qualifying out: end the call gracefully.

If actually Path A: deploy Path A reframes.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe:

> "Aside from the money piece, what else has been in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the affordability question — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as not the real issue — what else?"

For Path C qualify-out: no sweep.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 MONEY phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Distinguish from DEBT** — if they reference existing obligations (mortgage, debt servicing, loans), route to DEBT. If they reference investment cost / affordability of THIS specifically, stay here.
3. Present SCRIPT options with S and C highlighted as primary
4. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path (A perceived value, B inherited money story, C genuine constraint)
5. Load matched reframe card
6. Closer deploys reframe
7. Check for concession
8. Sweep transition or qualify out

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "It's a significant investment. I'd want to understand the ROI clearly before committing capital at this level."
- "I'm fairly conservative about cash allocation. $46K is meaningful for me."
- "I've spent more on bad investments before. I want to make sure this isn't another one."

Type A defends with analytical framing — usually Path A (value question disguised as affordability). Deploy the $10K for $5K move first. Then the business investment comparison.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I just don't have that kind of money lying around."
- "Where would I even find $46K?"
- "I'm always tight on money — it's just how I am."

Type B defends with emotional scarcity framing — often Path A or Path B. The $10K for $5K still works. For Path B, the outdated wardrobe reframe lands well. Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't shame the money concern
- Don't get apologetic about the price
- Curious about the actual financial picture, not invasive
- After participatory analogies, hold silence
- The $10K for $5K move requires the closer to actually wait for the prospect to say "yes I'd find the $5K" — don't rush past their answer

### Friction budget notes

MONEY is one of the most common excuses raised, often surfaces after the price is mentioned. The handling is usually efficient because the $10K for $5K move lands fast. Don't overspend budget here — get the reframe to land and sweep.

### Special: when the prospect asks "is there a payment plan?"

This is often a buying signal disguised as price negotiation. The move:

> "There can be. Before we go there — are you sold on the work itself? Because if you're not sold on whether it's worth doing, a payment plan won't help. If you are sold, then we'll figure out the structure. Which is it?"

This separates "I want a discount" from "I'm sold and need a financing structure." Different conversations.

### Special: when they ask for a discount

> "Is there any flexibility on the price?"

The move (Tristan-style):

> "Honest answer — no. The price reflects the work. Lowering the price doesn't increase the value of the work, it just signals I'm not confident in what I'm offering. What's actually behind the discount question? Is it that you can't afford it, or that you haven't decided it's worth it?"

Returns the conversation to value, not price. Don't negotiate from a place of insecurity.

### Adjacent: "Can I pay over a longer period?"

If they're actually committed and need structure, offer payment plans within reason. But always after they've committed to the work, not before. Payment structure follows commitment, not the other way around.
