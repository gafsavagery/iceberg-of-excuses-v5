## 1. THE PHASE 2/3 REFRAME ENGINE

The full sequence Phase 2 into the first half of Phase 3:

**PHASE 2 — LATERAL SWEEP**

1. Prospect surfaces external excuse (Layer 1)
2. Closer deploys SCRIPT to surface specifics
3. Specifics reveal Layer 2 behavior pattern
4. Closer deploys matched reframe move
5. Prospect concedes the excuse isn't the real issue
6. Closer pivots laterally: "Aside from [X], what else has been in the way?"
7. Repeat steps 1–6 for 2–3 total excuses

**PHASE 3 FIRST HALF — THE SCOOP AND FEAR EXTRACTION**

8. After 2–3 externals cleared, closer scoops: "Aside from all of these, what's actually been in the way?"
9. Closer holds silence (5–10 seconds minimum)
10. Prospect names the fear in their own words
11. Closer detects whether the aha is somatic or intellectual
12. If intellectual only, closer deepens with follow-up
13. Closer reflects the fear language back to anchor it
14. Closer pins the fear in time

**PHASE 3 SECOND HALF — COST / CONSEQUENCE / VISUALIZATION**

15. Reframe the fear (with questions, not statements)
16. Past cost extraction (chronicity, "us" framing, multi-domain)
17. Future consequence (charged language, named others affected)
18. Standard-for-others move
19. The Kurt question (what do we need to let go of)
20. Visualization of 2.0 self

**Each excuse file in this lexicon covers steps 1–7 for that specific excuse. The scoop and Phase 3 second half are covered in their own files (99-scoop-and-fear-extraction.md and 99-phase-3-cost-consequence-visualization.md).**

## 2. SCRIPT — THE CLARITY QUESTION ACRONYM

**The principle:** Vague claims and buzzwords ("I was too busy," "I doubt myself," "money was tight") have no surface area for reframes. SCRIPT converts vague to specific so the reframe can land. **S leads always.** The other letters deploy based on what the specifics reveal.

**Mnemonic:** "When a buzzword drops, get the SCRIPT before you reframe. It's the script of excuses — the story they always tell themselves about why they're not where they want to be."

### The six letters

**S — Specifically**

"What do you mean specifically?"
"Be precise. What do you mean by that?"
"When you say [their word] — what does that actually look like?"

When to deploy: Always first. Every vague claim.

**C — Compared to what**

"Compared to what?"
"Compared to what alternative?"
"A lot compared to what specifically?"

When to deploy: When prospect uses comparative language without naming the comparison ("a lot," "too much," "not enough," "tight").

**R — Roots**

"Where did you learn that?"
"Whose voice was that originally?"
"Where did that belief come from?"

When to deploy: When the belief sounds inherited, parental, or external in origin.

**I — Involved**

"Who specifically?"
"Name the people."
"Who are the people in this for you?"

When to deploy: When prospect references a vague reference group ("people," "everyone," "they") or a specific person (partner, parent, boss, board).

**P — Point in time**

"When did this start?"
"When did you decide that's who you are?"
"How long have you been telling yourself that?"

When to deploy: When prospect makes permanent-feeling identity claims ("I always," "I've never," "I'm just") OR when you need to anchor the timeframe of the pattern for leverage.

**T — Tell me the story**

"Walk me through it."
"Tell me the most recent time."
"What did that actually look like?"

When to deploy: When prospect gives a general pattern claim without specifics ("I have a track record of," "I always end up").

### SCRIPT minimalism

SCRIPT is a **guideline, not a checklist**. Deploy the minimum number of clarity moves needed to create reframe surface area. Over-questioning breaks rapport and feels interrogative.

For most excuses, 1–3 SCRIPT letters surface what you need. Rarely will you deploy all six on a single excuse.

The skill is reading what's in front of you and choosing which letters apply, not running the full alphabet every time.

## 3. THE THREE LAYERS (EXTERNAL / BEHAVIOR / FEAR)

Itai's framework. Every excuse has three layers stacked.

**Layer 1 — External / Surface**

What the prospect says first. The cover story. The reasonable-sounding excuse.

Examples: "I was too busy." "Money was tight." "My partner wasn't on board." "The economy was bad."

**Layer 2 — Behavior**

What they were doing instead of solving this. The pattern underneath the excuse. The behavioral evidence of avoidance.

Examples: "I was running on email all day." "I was spending on other things." "I never actually had a real conversation with my partner about it." "I was waiting for permission from external conditions."

**Layer 3 — Fear**

The actual driver. The root fear that the Layer 1 excuse is covering for. What the prospect is protecting themselves from confronting.

Examples: "I'm afraid I'm not actually capable of this." "I'm afraid of being found out as a fraud." "I'm afraid if I commit fully and fail, I lose my identity." "I'm afraid of being seen failing by people whose respect matters."

**The work:** Move the prospect from Layer 1 to Layer 3 across the conversation. SCRIPT + reframe converts Layer 1 to Layer 2. The scoop converts Layer 2 to Layer 3.

## 4. SWEEP BEFORE DEEP — THE LATERAL SWEEP RULE

**Principle:** Even when a prospect hands you a juicy excuse or even a Layer 2 admission early, you do NOT dive vertical on the first one. You clear it laterally and move to the next one.

**The rule:** Run SCRIPT + reframe + concession + "aside from X, what else" through 2–3 excuses BEFORE scooping to Layer 3.

**Why:**

1. The sweep builds the stack. The prospect watches themselves produce 2–3 cover stories. The volume itself becomes evidence.
2. By scoop time, the prospect is already suspicious of their own script. Each subsequent excuse feels thinner because the pattern of excusing is visible.
3. The fear named after sweep has more somatic weight than fear named after a single excuse. The sweep earns the fear.
4. Without sweeping, the prospect can retreat to unswept externals as escape hatches when the fear gets uncomfortable. ("Yeah but realistically the money thing IS still hard.") Sweeping removes the escape hatches.

**The temptation to violate this rule:** Self-aware prospects will sometimes hand you Layer 2 or even Layer 3 admissions in their first turn. Don't take the bait. Stay in lateral sweep. The discipline is what makes the eventual fear extraction work.

**The transition language:** "Aside from [excuse 1], what else has been in the way?" Use this verbatim phrasing. The "aside from" presumes more exist. The "in the way" frames them as obstacles to be cleared, not legitimate reasons.

## 5. THE SCOOP MOVE

After 2–3 externals are cleared, the scoop converts Layer 2 into Layer 3.

**The structure:**

"So aside from [excuse 1], [excuse 2], and [excuse 3] — what do you feel has actually been in the way?"

**Key elements:**

- Three externals listed back-to-back-to-back. The plurality matters. The visual of you stacking them makes the cover-story pattern undeniable.
- The word "actually" does the heavy work. It implies the externals you listed weren't real.
- The question is open-ended. Don't constrain with binaries here — you want the prospect to volunteer the internal language themselves.

**What you do after the scoop:**

Hold silence for 5–10 seconds minimum. This is where the fear surfaces. The prospect fills the silence with the truth they've been protecting themselves from saying.

## 6. PARTICIPATORY ANALOGIES — CRITICAL PRINCIPLE

**The principle:** Analogies must be participatory, not lectured.

When you deliver an analogy as a statement, you're teaching them. They process it intellectually and nod. Nothing lands.

When you deliver an analogy as a setup that REQUIRES THEM to complete the logic, they have to construct the conclusion themselves. The conclusion landing in their own voice has somatic weight. THAT's the work.

### Non-participatory (bad — this is lecturing)

> "We all have 24 hours in a day. Elon Musk runs three companies and has more kids than I can count. So time isn't the issue — prioritization is."

What's wrong: You delivered the conclusion. They received it. They didn't construct it. It lands as a coaching point, not a realization.

### Participatory (good — this is coaching)

> "We all have 24 hours. Elon Musk runs three companies and has more kids than I can count. What do you think the difference is between you and Elon when it comes to time?"

What's right: You set up the comparison and asked them to complete it. They have to say "it's not the time, it's the priority" themselves. The realization is theirs. They own it.

### How to convert a lecture-style analogy into a participatory one

Take any analogy and ask: "Where does the conclusion live? In my mouth or in theirs?"

If you're saying the conclusion → you're lecturing. Convert it by removing your conclusion and replacing it with a question that forces them to say it.

### Examples of participatory framing

- "If you had to figure out the difference between [them] and [comparison], what would you say?"
- "What do you think that means for [their situation]?"
- "If you were watching someone else do this, what would you tell them?"
- "What does that tell you about [the underlying pattern]?"
- "How does that map to what you've been doing?"

### Critical rule for the simulator

**Every analogy in this lexicon is written in participatory form.** When the sim renders an analogy card, the question prompt is mandatory. The closer must ask the question — not deliver the conclusion.

## 7. PAST-TENSE VS. PRESENT-TENSE FRAMING

**The dominant frame is past tense** because most discovery questions are diagnostic about the gap between when they first knew this was a problem and now.

The closer's typical questions sound like:

- "How long have you known this was a problem?"
- "When did you first realize this was something you needed to fix?"
- "What have you tried in the past?"
- "Why didn't that work?"
- "What's stopped you from solving this already?"

The prospect's answers come back in past tense:

- "I was just so busy last year"
- "Money was tight at the time"
- "I tried hiring a coach but it didn't work"
- "My partner wasn't on board"

**Why past-tense is more powerful for discovery:**

1. Removes the hypothetical. Replaces it with concluded evidence.
2. Prospect can't argue with their own past inaction.
3. The leverage comes from time-cost already absorbed (years of being stuck), not from a hypothetical future cost.
4. The reframes hit harder because they attack concluded events, not active uncertainties.
5. The only honest answer to "why didn't you act when you had the chance" is some version of "I was afraid." Past-tense framing pulls fear to the surface faster.

**When present-tense applies:**

Some prospects are dealing with the excuse in the active present, not the past. Examples:

- "I'm running out of time right now" (active deadline pressure)
- "I literally don't have the money this month" (active cash crunch)
- "My partner just told me last week she's not on board" (active conflict)

In these cases, the SCRIPT questions and reframes shift to present tense.

**The rule for the simulator:**

Each excuse file provides BOTH past-tense AND present-tense phrasings of the Layer 1 statement and SCRIPT questions. The closer adapts based on which tense the prospect is using. The sim should display both options on the card and let the closer pick based on real-time read.

## 8. THE FOUR REFRAME MOVE TYPES

After SCRIPT surfaces specifics, you deploy a matched reframe. There are four primary types. Each excuse file maps which type applies for each diagnostic path.

### Type 1 — Variable Analysis

When the prospect's failure was caused by missing structural conditions, not by inherent incapability.

**The move:** Surface the variable that failed. Reframe the conclusion drawn from the failure.

**Example:** "When you've stuck with something hard, what conditions enabled it? When this failed, which of those conditions weren't present?"

**Use for:** Self-doubt (evidence-based), "I've tried before," "I couldn't figure it out," fear of falling off.

### Type 2 — Source Attack

When the belief is inherited from an external source (parent, teacher, early experience) and the prospect is operating on outdated authority.

**The move:** Surface the source. Question whether the source's life is one the prospect wants.

**Example:** "Where did you learn that you had to do everything yourself? And the person who taught you that — is their life where you want to be?"

**Use for:** Self-doubt (identity-based), "I'm not the kind of person who...", "I'm difficult to work with," people-pleasing patterns.

### Type 3 — Counter-Evidence Callout

When the prospect's evidence for the limiting belief is directly contradicted by other evidence they've given you in the same conversation.

**The move:** Use their own data against their claim.

**Example:** "You just told me you built a $2M company. So 'I'm a quitter' isn't true. What's actually true?"

**Use for:** Identity claims that contradict the prospect's actual track record.

### Type 4 — Comparison Expansion

When the prospect is treating one path as risky and the other as safe, when both have costs that should be weighed.

**The move:** Make the invisible cost visible. Force the prospect to weigh both paths honestly.

**Example:** "You've described what acting would cost. Now describe what NOT acting has cost over the last [timeframe]."

**Use for:** Risk/fear-based excuses, "I need to think about it," "what if it doesn't work," overcommitment.

## 9. SOMATIC AHA VS. INTELLECTUAL AHA

**The principle:** The fear named must land somatically, not just intellectually. If the prospect names the fear in normal conversational tone, you have an intellectual label — not the work. You haven't moved them. Don't advance to cost-of-fear yet.

### Signs of somatic aha (advance to cost extraction)

- Voice drops noticeably
- Long pause before answering (3+ seconds)
- Eyes drop or go distant
- Physical stillness or body shift
- Involuntary acknowledgments ("oh god," "huh," "shit," "fuck")
- The answer comes more slowly than other answers in the call
- Breath held or sigh after
- The phrase comes out smaller, quieter, less performed
- Sometimes tears, or close to them

### Signs of intellectual aha (deepen before advancing)

- Conversational tone unchanged
- Fast response, no pause
- Same energy as the rest of the call
- Words feel rehearsed or like therapy-talk
- The prospect names the fear like they've named it before to a therapist
- No physical shift

### What to do when aha is intellectual

Don't advance to cost-of-fear. Deepen with one of these moves:

- "When you say [their phrase] — what's underneath that?"
- "Stay with that for a second. What does that actually feel like in your body?"
- "If you didn't have words for it — what's the actual thing?"
- "Take your time with it. What comes up when you really look at it?"

These questions push past the verbal label into the somatic experience. Wait for the body shift. Then advance.

### Critical rule for the simulator

The sim should prompt the closer to identify which type of aha just occurred. If intellectual, the next card should be a deepening question, not cost-of-fear. If somatic, advance to the cost extraction sequence.

## 10. PROSPECT TYPES — TYPE A VS. TYPE B

The lexicon supports two prospect archetypes. The closer chooses which to drill against in the sim. Each excuse file should have notes on how the Type A vs. Type B prospect responds differently.

### Type A — High-performing operator

- Built or building a successful business
- Articulate, intelligent, analytical
- Defends excuses with logic and structure
- Most common defense: "my situation is unique"
- Hides fear behind sophisticated rationalization
- Often has clinical-feeling self-awareness ("I know I'm doing X but I can't stop")
- Responds to: rigor, directness, counter-evidence from their own data
- Breaks at: being shown their own contradictions in their own words

### Type B — Stuck or stalled prospect

- Earning less than they want / business not scaling / career plateaued
- Less articulate about patterns
- Defends excuses with emotion or external blame
- Most common defense: "you don't understand my situation"
- Hides fear behind victimhood or learned helplessness
- Less aware of their own patterns
- Responds to: warmth, validation of the difficulty, then challenge
- Breaks at: being shown that other people in their exact situation succeed

### How the sim uses these types

When the closer selects Type A, the sim's prospect responses use Type A language patterns (analytical, articulate, defends with logic).

When the closer selects Type B, the sim's prospect responses use Type B language patterns (emotional, less precise, defends with circumstance).

Same excuse, different texture. The closer practices adjusting their delivery for the prospect's archetype.

## 11. THE FRICTION BUDGET PRINCIPLE

Every sales call has a finite friction budget. You can break X number of beliefs and force Y number of decisions before the prospect's nervous system says "too much, I'm out."

**The implication for SCRIPT:** Deploy minimum letters needed. Over-questioning spends budget without adding leverage.

**The implication for reframes:** One strong reframe lands harder than three weak ones. Pick the one that fits the specifics; don't pile on.

**The implication for sweep:** 2–3 externals is the typical sweep budget. Beyond 3, you're exhausting the prospect and the scoop loses power.

**The implication for the scoop and fear extraction:** Most of the friction budget should be reserved for the fear work, not the sweep. The sweep is preparation. The fear is where leverage gets created.

**The closer's job is to monitor the budget in real-time.** Signs the budget is running low:

- Prospect getting defensive (porcupine)
- Shorter answers
- Visible fatigue or distraction
- Pushback on the questioning style itself ("why are you asking me this")
- Sudden tone shift toward transactional

When you see these signs, pull back. Acknowledge. Slow down. Don't keep pushing in the same direction. The work continues on the next call if needed.

## 12. WHAT THIS ENGINE IS NOT

A few things worth naming explicitly to avoid misuse:

**This is NOT a script.** You don't memorize sequences. You build instincts and deploy moves based on what the prospect gives you. The acronyms (SCRIPT, the layer framework) are scaffolding while the instincts form. Once fluent, the engine becomes invisible.

**This is NOT interrogation.** The questions are curiosity-driven. The closer is genuinely trying to understand the prospect's reality, not expose them as lying. Tone matters. Human first, framework underneath.

**This is NOT a system for selling people who shouldn't buy.** If after honest discovery and reframing, the prospect concludes this isn't right for them, that's a valid outcome. The engine surfaces truth. Sometimes the truth is they're not ready.

**This is NOT denial of real constraints.** Some excuses point at real things. A 40-year-old woman's biological window is real. A founder with genuine cash crunch has a real constraint. The reframe isn't to deny the constraint — it's to make the cost of inaction equally visible to the cost of action, and let the prospect choose with full data.

**This is NOT about being right.** It's about being useful. If the reframe doesn't land, don't double down. Move to a different angle. The prospect's truth is the goal, not the closer's intellectual victory.
