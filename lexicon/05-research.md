## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I wanted to research it more before committing"
- "I was trying to find the right person"
- "I kept reading and watching but never pulled the trigger"
- "I was comparing options"
- "I needed more data before deciding"
- "I was learning everything I could about this"
- "I was waiting until I knew enough to make the right choice"
- "I bought a bunch of courses to figure it out first"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I want to do some more research first"
- "Let me look into this more before I commit"
- "I need to compare with a few other options"
- "I'd like to read some reviews"
- "I want to find the right fit"
- "I'm still gathering information"
- "I need to understand more before I decide"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Research as procrastination disguised as diligence
- Information consumption replacing action
- Perfectionism about the "right" choice
- Identity as "the smart one who makes informed decisions"
- Avoiding the discomfort of commitment by staying in evaluation mode
- Genuine need for specific information (rare)
- Fear that there's a "better" option just around the corner
- Using research to avoid the actual work the solution would require

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse is uniquely dangerous because it sounds responsible. "I want to research" is socially acceptable. The prospect can use it indefinitely without ever being held to account. Friends, family, advisors all nod approvingly.

But for high-earning entrepreneur ICP specifically, "research" has often become a multi-year pattern. They've been "researching" the same problem for two, five, sometimes ten years. The research has produced more confusion, not more clarity. They've consumed enough information for a PhD on their own problem and still haven't acted.

The reframe is to make visible that research without action is a defense, not diligence. And that the actual question isn't "do I have enough information" — it's "what will I do with what I already know?"

## SCRIPT PATH

For RESEARCH, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, and P**. Specifically and Point-in-time are critical.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you wanted to research more — what specifically were you trying to learn?"

**Present:** "When you say you want to research — what specifically are you trying to figure out that you don't already know?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate concrete information gaps or whether "research" is vague. Most prospects can't name what they actually need to learn. They have a vague sense of "more information would help" without knowing what information.

If they can articulate specific gaps, those are addressable. If they can't, the research is procrastination.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what you actually did. What did you read, watch, listen to? What did you take away?"

**Present:** "Walk me through what you've researched so far. What have you consumed? What have you learned?"

**What you're listening for:** The volume of research already done. Most "I need to research" prospects have already done massive amounts of research. Listen for: courses bought, books read, podcasts consumed, communities joined, free webinars attended. Then ask what came of it.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long have you been researching this problem? Months? Years?"

**Present:** "How long have you been gathering information? When did the research start?"

**What you're listening for:** The duration of the research phase. Most prospects will reveal that they've been "researching" for years. The duration itself is the reframe — at some point research becomes avoidance.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn that you needed to be fully informed before deciding? Has that pattern shown up elsewhere?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who valued education over action, an academic background that prized analysis, or a profession that requires certainty before action (lawyer, doctor, engineer). The pattern that serves them professionally may be sabotaging them personally.

### C — Compared to what (deploy when comparing offers)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what other options? What are you actually comparing this to?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have specific alternatives or whether "comparing" is vague. Often they can't name specific alternatives — they just want to feel like they have them.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip unless they reference external advisors driving the research need.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Research as procrastination disguised as diligence

**Most common case.** The prospect has been researching for an extended period with no action. The research has become the work, replacing the actual work.

**How to recognize:**

- Multi-year research timeline
- Can name many things they've consumed (courses, books, podcasts)
- Can't name what they've implemented
- Vague about what specific information they still need
- Treats research as productivity
- Has "shelfware" — programs they bought but didn't complete

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Of everything you've researched, what have you actually implemented?"
- "What's been your pattern — research, implement, evaluate, then research again? Or research, research, research, and never implement?"
- "If you'd taken the time you spent researching and applied it to actually doing the work — where might you be?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What's the difference between research that leads to action and research that replaces action?"

- "If you spent the next year doing more research, would you actually be closer to solving this problem? Or would you have more information and the same problem?"

- "What's the actual question you're trying to answer? Because if you can name it specifically, we can probably address it in this conversation."

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Gym Researcher** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine someone who's been researching gyms for three years. They've read every review, compared every membership, watched workout videos on YouTube. They still aren't in shape. What's the actual problem — that they haven't found the right gym, or something else?"

After they answer:

> "How is that different from what you've been doing with this problem?"

Why this lands: Universally relatable. Reveals that the research has been the avoidance. Most prospects laugh uncomfortably when they see the pattern — they've been doing the equivalent of researching gyms while staying out of shape.

**ANALOGY 2 — The PhD Pattern** — **Grade: A for analytical ICP**

> "If I told you I was going to get a PhD before I started a business, you'd say 'just start the business.' Right? You learn more from doing six months of business than from reading six years of books about business. What's been different about how you've approached this?"

After they answer:

> "What would change if you treated this the same way you'd tell someone else to treat starting a business?"

Why this lands: Activates their identity as someone who has done hard things by doing them, not by studying them. They built businesses through action, not through perfect information. The double standard becomes visible.

**ANALOGY 3 — The MBA vs. Doer Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Think about the most successful entrepreneurs you know personally. Did they get there by becoming the most informed person on every topic? Or did they get there by acting on incomplete information and learning by doing?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've approached your own growth?"

Why this lands: Reference points for success are people who acted on incomplete information. The prospect has been holding themselves to a standard their heroes didn't meet. The contradiction is the lever.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So research has been the way you've avoided the actual work. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from moving on this?"

### PATH B — Genuine information gap that's addressable

**Less common but real.** The prospect has a specific information need that's reasonable. They want to understand the methodology, the format, the track record. They're not procrastinating — they're doing actual due diligence.

**How to recognize:**

- Can articulate specific information needs
- Hasn't been researching for years — this is recent
- Their questions are concrete and answerable
- No emotional charge to the questions
- They're treating this professionally

**This is not a reframe — this is a qualification situation.** Provide what they need.

**The move:**

> "Fair. What specifically would you need to understand for this to feel like the right decision?"

After they articulate:

Provide the information directly. Don't dance around it. Give them the methodology, the structure, the certainty stack from foundational principles. Then close with:

> "Okay, you've got what you need on the information side. What else?"

**Transition language for Path B:**

> "Now you have the data. Aside from making sure you had the information, what else has been in the way?"

### PATH C — Information-shopping for the "perfect" option

**Common.** The prospect is comparing multiple offers, looking for the "best" one, treating decision-making as optimization rather than commitment. Perfectionism dressed as research.

**How to recognize:**

- References multiple alternatives
- Can list options but can't choose
- Stuck in evaluation mode
- Talks about "the right fit" or "the perfect program"
- May say "I just need to make sure I'm making the best decision"

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How long have you been comparing options?"
- "What would 'the perfect choice' actually look like? Be specific."
- "Has there ever been a 'perfect choice' for anything significant in your life? Or do you make the best decision you can and then commit?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "There are 10 coaches who could probably help you. The difference between you finding the 'best' one and you finding a good one is probably 5% on the outcome. The difference between you starting and not starting is 100%. What's the actual lever here?"

- "When you've made other significant decisions in your life — your business, your marriage, where you live — did you find the 'perfect' option, or did you find a good option and then commit fully?"

- "What if the search for the perfect option is itself the avoidance? What if any of the legitimate options would work, and the decision is what you're actually afraid of?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Restaurant Paralysis** — **Grade: A**

> "You're hungry. You're looking at three restaurants. They all have good reviews, they're all in your budget, they all serve food you like. How long do you spend choosing? And what happens to the hunger while you're choosing?"

After they answer:

> "How does that map to what you've been doing?"

Why this lands: Simple, universal, exposes the disproportion between time spent choosing and time spent eating. Most prospects realize they've been "choosing" for years on a decision that should have taken weeks.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Spouse Comparison** — **Grade: A**

> "When you decided to marry your partner, did you have certainty that they were the 'best' option in the entire world? Or did you decide that they were good for you and then commit fully?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've approached this decision?"

Why this lands: Activates their experience of committing under uncertainty in the highest-stakes domain. They didn't optimize spouse selection — they chose and committed. Reveals that "optimal" isn't actually their decision pattern for important things.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Strategy Decision** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "In your business, when you make strategic decisions — pivot to a new market, hire a key person, sign a major contract — do you wait until you have perfect information? Or do you decide with incomplete information and then commit?"

After they answer:

> "Why are you holding this decision to a higher standard than the strategic decisions that built your business?"

Why this lands: They make significant business decisions on incomplete information constantly. They commit and adapt. Holding their own growth to a higher information bar than their business decisions is incoherent.

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "Okay. So 'finding the perfect option' has been a way to delay the commitment. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path:

> "Aside from the research piece, what else has been in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond gathering information — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as not the real issue — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 RESEARCH phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, P highlighted
3. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path (A procrastination, B genuine gap, C perfectionism)
4. Load matched reframe card
5. Closer deploys reframe
6. Check for concession
7. Sweep transition or loop

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I've been deep on this space for a while. I've read the major books, listened to the podcasts. I'm trying to identify the right framework."
- "I want to make an informed decision. I've been evaluating my options carefully."
- "I'm methodical. I do my homework before I commit."

Type A defends with intellectual rigor. The research IS extensive. Reframe by activating their decision-making pattern in their business (Analogy 2 and 3 work well). Path A is most common for Type A.

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I just want to make sure I'm not making another mistake."
- "I've bought so many courses already that didn't work."
- "I keep getting stuck on which coach to pick."

Type B defends with overwhelm and fear of being wrong again. Often has bought multiple programs that didn't deliver (or that they didn't apply). The PhD pattern analogy and the gym researcher both work well. Path A almost always.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't validate the research as a smart approach to this problem
- Treat the research pattern with curiosity, not approval
- After participatory analogies, sit in silence long enough for them to construct the parallel
- The realization "I've been researching to avoid acting" must come from them, not you

### Friction budget notes

RESEARCH is often handled relatively quickly because the participatory analogies are so revealing. Most prospects laugh nervously when they see the gym researcher or PhD pattern in themselves. Don't over-spend budget here — get the reframe to land and sweep.

### Special: when they ask "what should I research before deciding?"

This is a buying signal disguised as a research question. The move:

> "Honest answer? Nothing. Everything you'd find by researching, I can tell you in this conversation. The question isn't whether you have enough information — you do. The question is whether you're going to act on what you already know. That's a different question."

This converts the research stall into the actual decision moment. They either commit or reveal the deeper objection.

### Adjacent: "Can you send me information to review?"

Variant of RESEARCH framed as material request. The honest move:

> "I can, but let me ask first — what specific information do you not have right now that would change your decision? If you can name it, I can address it on the call. If you can't, sending materials usually means we don't end up working together. Materials don't move people. Decisions move people. So what's the specific thing you need?"

If they have a specific need, address it. If they're vague, surface the avoidance.
