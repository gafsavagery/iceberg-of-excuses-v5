## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I didn't believe in myself enough back then"
- "I doubted I could actually do it"
- "I wasn't sure I had what it takes"
- "I kept second-guessing myself"
- "I didn't trust myself to follow through"
- "I figured I'd just fail again"
- "I never thought I was the kind of person who could pull it off"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I don't really believe in myself"
- "I doubt I can actually do this"
- "I'm not sure I have what it takes"
- "I keep second-guessing everything"
- "I just have a lot of self-doubt"
- "I'm worried I'm not actually capable"
- "I'm not the kind of person who..."

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

Self-doubt is the most commonly mishandled excuse in coaching. Most coaches respond with either reassurance ("you ARE capable, I see it in you") or generic identity reframes ("when did you decide you were that person"). Both fail when the doubt has actual evidence behind it.

The key is that **self-doubt has four distinct sub-types**, and each requires a different reframe. Misdiagnosing the type and applying the wrong move is why most self-doubt handling falls flat.

The diagnostic question is the first move. Get the type right before deploying anything.

---

## THE DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION (deploy BEFORE SCRIPT)

**This is unique to self-doubt — the diagnostic precedes SCRIPT.**

> "When you say you doubt yourself — what specifically do you doubt? Your ability to do it? Your worthiness of having it? Your track record of following through? Or do you doubt yourself because of actual past attempts that didn't work?"

Their answer tells you which type:

- **Type 1 — Ability doubt:** "I don't think I'm capable" / "I'm not smart enough" / "I don't have what it takes"
- **Type 2 — Performed humility:** "I just generally doubt myself" / vague / can't get specific
- **Type 3 — Track-record/follow-through doubt:** "I always start things and don't finish" / "I don't trust my follow-through"
- **Type 4 — Evidence-based doubt:** "I've tried this before and it didn't work" / specific past failures

The wiggle answer ("I don't know, just generally") is itself the diagnostic — it points to Type 2.

---

## SCRIPT PATH (general for all types — deploys AFTER the type is identified)

### S — Specifically (deploys via the diagnostic question above)

Already covered by the type-diagnostic. Skip unless the prospect's answer was still vague after the diagnostic.

### P — Point in time (CRITICAL for Types 1 and 2)

**Past:** "When did you decide you were someone who doubts yourself? Five years ago? Ten? Were you always that way?"

**Present:** "How long have you been carrying this version of yourself? When did it install?"

**What you're listening for:** Most prospects with Type 1 or Type 2 doubt can identify a specific period or moment when the belief took root. Often childhood. Sometimes a specific failure event in adulthood. The timestamp is the lever.

### R — Roots (CRITICAL for Types 1 and 2)

**Either tense:** "Who taught you to doubt yourself? Whose voice was that originally?"

**What you're listening for:** Usually a parent, teacher, coach, or early relationship. Sometimes a specific painful moment (being labeled "not the smart one," being told they weren't good enough). The source attribution is what makes the reframe possible.

### T — Tell me the story (CRITICAL for Types 3 and 4)

**Past:** "Walk me through what you've actually tried. The specific times you didn't follow through, or the past attempts that didn't work — give me the actual stories."

**Present:** "Walk me through the evidence. What are the specific events that are feeding the doubt?"

**What you're listening for:** For Type 3, the pattern of incomplete commitments. For Type 4, the specifics of past failures (which usually reveal structural causes, not personal incapability).

### C — Compared to what (occasionally useful for Types 1 and 4)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what? Compared to people who've actually done what you want to do — what's different about you?"

**What you're listening for:** Often reveals they're comparing themselves to elite outliers (Bezos, Musk) and concluding they're inadequate, rather than to realistic peers who've solved their actual problem.

### I — Involved (occasionally useful for Type 1)

**Either tense:** "Whose opinion of you is loudest when you doubt yourself?"

**What you're listening for:** The internalized critic's source. Often a specific person (parent, ex-partner, sibling) whose voice has become the prospect's internal monologue.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS BY TYPE

### TYPE 1 — Identity-level ability doubt ("I'm not capable")

**Description:** Belief that they're inherently not the kind of person who can do this. No specific evidence — it's a felt identity, often installed in childhood.

**How to recognize:**

- Speaks in identity terms ("I'm just not someone who...")
- Can't point to specific failures that justify the doubt
- The doubt feels permanent, "that's just who I am"
- Often connects to a parent who labeled them ("you're the [creative/dreamy/scattered] one" while another sibling was "the smart one")

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "When did you first decide you weren't capable? Was it a moment, or did it slowly install?"

- "Who taught you to call yourself that? Whose voice was it?"

- "And the person whose voice that was — is their life where you want to be?"

- "If you took that voice out of your head — like surgically removed it — what would actually be left? Who would you be without it?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Outdated Operating System** — **Grade: A**

> "You've been running an operating system that was installed when you were [their age when belief installed]. Your iPhone OS gets updates constantly. When was the last time you updated the OS running on yourself?"

After they answer:

> "What's been the cost of running 25-year-old software on the version of you that exists now?"

Why this lands: Makes the belief feel updatable rather than permanent. The OS metaphor activates their tech-literate understanding that systems get refreshed. Reveals that the doubt isn't truth — it's an old install.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Inherited Identity** — **Grade: A**

> "If your dad / mom / [whoever installed it] had told you at age 8 that you were too short to ever be tall — and you grew to be 6'2" — would you still be walking around acting short? At what point does the data override the early labeling?"

After they answer:

> "How is what you've built — the business, the income, the life you've made — different from that?"

Why this lands: Forces them to see that the current evidence of their capability contradicts the early labeling. They've already proven the label wrong by what they've built. Yet they're still living down to the label.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Kid in the Mirror** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine your 8-year-old self is sitting across from you. Look at him. Now tell him 'I'm not capable.' Can you do it? With his face right in front of you?"

After they react (usually visibly uncomfortable):

> "Why does it feel different to say it to him than to say it to yourself?"

Why this lands: Reveals that the doubt is cruelty they wouldn't apply to a child. Activates the protective instinct. Makes visible that the prospect has been verbally abusing themselves in a way they wouldn't tolerate happening to another person.

### TYPE 2 — Performed humility ("I just doubt myself" — vague)

**Description:** Uses self-doubt as social armor. Naming it makes them seem self-aware and gets sympathy. Often hasn't actually examined whether the doubt is real or just a learned posture.

**How to recognize:**

- Can't get specific about WHAT they doubt
- Uses self-doubt as a "safe" answer in conversations
- Vague language ("I just have a lot of self-doubt")
- The doubt sounds rehearsed but shallow when probed
- May be intellectually sophisticated about it but emotionally surface

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Is the doubt actually there, or have you just learned that naming it gets you off the hook?"

- "If you didn't have permission to say 'I doubt myself' — if that wasn't an option — what would you actually have to say about why you haven't moved?"

- "What does 'I doubt myself' get you? What's it protect you from?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Stance vs. The Fact** — **Grade: A**

> "Doubt isn't something you have, like a fever. Doubt is something you do, like a posture. Every time you say 'I doubt myself,' you're not reporting a fact — you're taking a stance. Choosing it. What if you decided to stop taking the stance?"

After they react:

> "What would it feel like to just... not adopt that posture? Even for an hour?"

Why this lands: Reframes the doubt as active choice rather than passive condition. Anything that's a choice can be unchosen. The framing itself starts dissolving the pattern.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Lying Reframe** — **Grade: A (Tristan-style, blunt)**

> "Can I challenge you here? Can you stop lying to yourself about doubting yourself? You built a business. You don't actually doubt your ability — you doubt your willingness to be uncomfortable. There's a difference. Which one is it?"

After they react:

> "Be honest. Which is true — that you can't, or that you don't want to feel what you'd have to feel?"

Why this lands: Direct, confrontational, reveals that "I doubt myself" is sometimes cover for "I don't want to do the uncomfortable thing." Use when the prospect has clear evidence of capability that contradicts their stated doubt. Tristan voice.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Audit** — **Grade: B+**

> "If we did an audit of your life — not your feelings, just your data — and looked at what you've actually accomplished, what evidence would we find for 'I'm someone who doubts myself' versus 'I'm someone who does things despite the doubt'?"

After they answer:

> "Which one is closer to your actual track record?"

Why this lands: Forces them to look at evidence rather than feeling. Most high-performers who say "I doubt myself" have a massive track record of acting despite doubt. The audit reveals the identity is wrong.

### TYPE 3 — Follow-through doubt ("I don't trust my completion")

**Description:** Specific concern that they start things and don't finish. Has actual evidence of incomplete commitments. The doubt is about durability of commitment, not capability.

**How to recognize:**

- Can name multiple things they've started and not finished
- The pattern is real and visible to them
- Often has unfinished courses, gym memberships, books, projects
- The doubt is specifically about staying with something, not about capability to start

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Walk me through the things you've finished. Anything significant — relationships, business, kids you've raised. What enabled you to stay with those?"

- "What's different about the things you finish versus the things you don't?"

- "Is the issue that you don't follow through, or that you commit to things without the conditions that would enable follow-through?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Conditions Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "When you've stuck with something — like raising your kids, building your business — what conditions enabled that? Accountability, stakes, structure, public visibility?"

After they list:

> "Now look at the things you've fallen off — the courses, the gym, whatever. Which of those conditions weren't present?"

After they answer:

> "So the pattern isn't 'I don't follow through.' The pattern is 'I follow through when the conditions support it.' Different diagnosis. What does this engagement have built into it?"

Why this lands: Reveals that the falling-off isn't character — it's mismatched conditions. The prospect has actually finished hard things; they finished them under specific conditions. This engagement either has those conditions or it doesn't. Activates the diagnosis as solvable.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Selective-Finisher Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "You built a business. You didn't fall off that. You stayed in a marriage for X years. You didn't fall off that. So 'I don't follow through' isn't actually true. What's true is that you finish things you commit to fully. The things you fell off — were you ever fully committed at the start?"

After they answer:

> "What was different about how you committed to those versus how you'd commit to this?"

Why this lands: Uses their actual track record against the limiting identity. Reveals that "I'm a quitter" is a story, not a fact. The real pattern is selective commitment — and the question is whether they'll commit fully to this.

---

### TYPE 4 — Evidence-based doubt (specific past failures)

**Description:** The most sophisticated form of self-doubt. The prospect has actual data from past attempts and is using that data to predict future failure. Not irrational — but usually misdiagnosed. They're attributing structural failures to personal incapability.

**How to recognize:**

- Can articulate specific past attempts in detail
- The doubt is grounded in real events
- They've examined the failures rationally (or think they have)
- Pattern usually involves trying alone, without the right conditions
- Often the most articulate and analytical about their doubt

**Critical:** Do NOT use the abstract identity-belief reframes here (Type 1 and Type 2 moves). They will not land because the prospect has real evidence. Use evidence-examination moves instead.

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Walk me through the most recent attempt. Be specific. What did you try, what went wrong, what was the actual variable that failed?"

- "In each past attempt, what was the variable that broke? Was it your inherent capability, or was it something structural — environment, mentor, team, timing, support?"

- "Your doubt is rational. You have evidence. But what does the evidence actually say? Does it say 'you can't do this,' or does it say 'you couldn't do this under those specific conditions, alone'?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Variable Analysis** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine a plant that didn't grow in your kitchen. You concluded 'I can't grow plants.' But maybe the variable was that the kitchen didn't have enough light. Move the plant to a sunny window — different outcome. Was the plant the problem, or was it the conditions?"

After they answer:

> "What if the past failures weren't about you — they were about the conditions you tried to grow in?"

Why this lands: Externalizes the variable. The prospect isn't defective — the environment was wrong. Reframes failures as data about conditions, not data about identity. Doesn't deny the failures; redirects what they meant.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Success-Conditions Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "When you've succeeded at something — anything — what were the conditions? Right environment? Right mentor? Right pressure? Right accountability?"

After they list:

> "Now compare those conditions to the conditions during the past failures. What was different?"

After they answer:

> "So the past data isn't saying 'you can't.' It's saying 'you couldn't, under those specific conditions, alone.' Different conclusion. What if we engineered the success conditions instead?"

Why this lands: Mathematical. The data has two interpretations — incapability or wrong conditions. The success conditions almost always reveal that the prospect succeeds when supported and fails when isolated. The reframe is the offer.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Acknowledgment + Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Your doubt is rational. You're looking at data. Most people don't even do that — they hope this time will be different without examining why. So I'm not going to tell you your doubt is wrong. I'm going to tell you the conclusion you drew from the data is wrong. The data shows you couldn't do this alone, without the right conditions. That's not the same as 'you can't do this.'"

After they react:

> "So the real question is — are you willing to put yourself in different conditions and test the data again?"

Why this lands: This is the move that respects the prospect's rigor. Doesn't deny their analysis. Refines their conclusion. Most prospects with Type 4 doubt have never had someone honor the doubt while reframing what it means. The respect creates the space for the reframe to land.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any type's reframe:

> "Okay. So the doubt has a different diagnosis than 'I'm not capable.' Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the doubt — what else?"
- "If we set aside the self-doubt as not actually a permanent fact — what else is in the way?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 SELF-DOUBT phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Deploy the diagnostic question FIRST** — before any SCRIPT letter
3. Based on their answer, sim identifies the type
4. Type-specific SCRIPT letters then deploy (P+R for Types 1/2, T for Types 3/4)
5. Load the matched reframe card for that specific type
6. Closer deploys reframe
7. Check for concession or somatic aha
8. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm actually pretty confident in most domains. But in this specific area — building the next-level identity that would let me hire VPs and let go of operations — I have legitimate uncertainty."
- "I've tried executive coaching twice. Neither stuck. So when I look at this, I'm running probability against my history."

Type A often presents Type 4 (evidence-based) doubt. Deploy the variable analysis and success-conditions reframes. Respect the rigor.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, sim's responses sound like:

- "I just don't believe in myself, honestly."
- "I've never been the kind of person who can stick with things."
- "Deep down, I think I'm going to fail again."

Type B often presents Type 1 or Type 2 (identity-based or performed humility). Deploy the operating system or kid-in-the-mirror reframes. Lead with warmth, then challenge.

### Voice tone notes

- For Type 1: gentle but clear, especially with the kid-in-the-mirror analogy
- For Type 2: more direct, ready to challenge the performed humility
- For Type 3: respectful, analytical, work the conditions diagnosis with them
- For Type 4: most respectful of all — honor the rigor before redirecting

### Friction budget notes

Self-doubt is often the first OR second external surfaced. It often connects directly to Layer 3 fear (fear of not being enough). Don't try to fully resolve self-doubt in Phase 2 — surface the type, reframe to take the edge off, and lateral sweep. The deeper work happens in fear extraction.

For Type 4 specifically, the reframe is high-leverage because the prospect respects the move when you honor their evidence. Spend a bit more budget here — Type 4 prospects tend to be your best clients if you close them.

### Special: the "type-blend" prospect

Some prospects show multiple types simultaneously. The diagnostic question may produce a mixed answer. The move:

> "It sounds like there's both — there's some identity-level doubt that's been there a long time, AND there's specific past evidence. Let's work them separately. Which one feels more present right now?"

Take the dominant one first. Address the other on the sweep or in deeper fear work.
