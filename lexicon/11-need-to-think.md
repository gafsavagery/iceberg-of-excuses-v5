## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (rarely surfaces in past tense — usually present)

- "I kept telling myself I needed more time to think about it"
- "I was going to decide but I wanted to sit with it"
- "I told myself I'd think about it and never came back to it"

### Present-tense (DOMINANT — this is almost always a present-call objection)

- "I need to think about it"
- "Let me sit with this for a few days"
- "I want to sleep on it"
- "Let me process this and get back to you"
- "I need some time to consider"
- "I want to think through it carefully"
- "Can I have a few days?"
- "I don't want to make this decision in the heat of the moment"
- "Let me look at this with fresh eyes"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Deferral disguised as wisdom
- Avoiding the discomfort of committing
- Hoping the urgency will pass and the decision will go away
- Identity as "the thoughtful one who doesn't rush"
- Genuine need for processing (rare for high-performers in their domain)
- Wanting to find a reason to say no without saying no on the call
- Wanting to research/compare (see RESEARCH file)
- Wanting to consult partner/advisor (see WIFE/PARTNER file)
- Wanting to come down off the emotional high of the conversation
- Sometimes: legitimate need to do due diligence on contract details

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

**"I need to think about it" is almost never about thinking.**

The data is overwhelming: prospects who say "let me think about it" on coaching calls close at very low rates if you let them leave the call. The "thinking" doesn't actually happen — what happens is the emotional intensity dissipates, the fear voice grows, life intervenes, and the prospect ghosts or comes back at half-conviction.

**This means: if you accept "let me think about it" as a legitimate stall, you've lost the sale.**

However, "I need to think about it" CAN be legitimate. The closer's job is to distinguish:

1. **Stall to escape the call** (most common) — must be handled now
2. **Genuine need for specific information** (uncommon) — surface what they actually need
3. **Real need to process a major decision** (rare for high-stakes coaching) — different conversation

The move is to surface what they're actually trying to think ABOUT. Once that surfaces, it's almost always either a hidden objection (which can be handled) or a fear (which is the real work).

**Tonal warning:** Do not be pushy or salesy here. The closer who says "well I have other people interested, let me know by Friday" loses the sale AND damages reputation. The right move is curious and direct, not pressuring.

## SCRIPT PATH

For NEED TO THINK ABOUT IT, the most leveraged letters are **S, C, and T**. Specifically is critical.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST — and aggressively for this excuse)

**Present:** "Of course. What specifically do you need to think about?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate what they need to think about. Most prospects can't get more specific than "just think about it" — which IS the diagnosis. They don't have something specific they need to think through; they want to escape the decision moment.

If they CAN articulate (e.g., "I need to look at my Q4 cash flow" or "I need to check with my CFO"), that's a real consideration. Handle it as such.

### C — Compared to what (deploy when they can't get specific)

**Present:** "Compared to what you'd be thinking about — is there something specific that's unclear, or is it more of a general 'I want time'?"

**What you're listening for:** Forces them to admit whether there's a real question or whether they want time to escape. Most will admit "honestly, I just want some time."

### T — Tell me the story (deploy when relevant)

**Present:** "Walk me through what 'thinking about it' would actually look like for you. What's the process? Sit with it? Run numbers? Talk to someone?"

**What you're listening for:** Reveals whether they have an actual process or just a vague desire for time. Most don't have a process — which is the tell.

### P — Point in time (deploy when relevant)

**Present:** "How long do you need? What's the timeline you're thinking?"

**What you're listening for:** Vague answers ("a few days," "until next week") suggest no real process. Specific answers ("Tuesday after my Q4 board meeting") suggest legitimate consideration.

### R — Roots (rarely useful)

Skip unless the "need to think" pattern is clearly inherited (parent who taught them to never decide quickly).

### I — Involved (deploy when they reference others)

**Present:** "Who do you need to think about it with? Just yourself, or other people?"

**What you're listening for:** If they reference a partner or advisor, that's a different excuse (see WIFE/PARTNER or relevant files). If they're just thinking alone, it's about their internal process.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Stall to escape the call (most common)

**Description:** The prospect doesn't actually have something specific to think through. They're trying to escape the decision moment by deferring to a hypothetical future "thinking" that won't actually happen as productive deliberation.

**How to recognize:**

- Can't articulate WHAT they need to think about
- Vague timeline
- Comes after the price is mentioned
- Comes after a moment of acknowledgment that this is the right work
- Energy shift in the call — they were engaged, now they're pulling back
- "I just need time" without specifics

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Honest question — is there something specific that needs more clarity, or are you trying to give yourself space from the decision?"
- "If I told you you couldn't think about it — that you had to decide right now — what would your gut say?"
- "What do you think you'll know in three days that you don't know right now?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "In my experience, 'I need to think about it' almost always means one of two things — either there's a specific question I haven't answered, or there's a fear I haven't surfaced. Which one is it for you?"

- "What would change between now and a few days from now? What information would arrive? Because in my experience, the answer is usually 'nothing new' — and the only thing that happens in those days is the urgency fades and the decision gets harder."

- "If you imagine yourself three days from now, what does the decision look like? Are you more committed, less committed, or just further from the conversation we had?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Decision Doesn't Get Clearer** — **Grade: A**

> "Have you ever had a decision get easier by sitting on it longer? Or do decisions usually get harder as you sit with them?"

After they answer (almost always: harder):

> "What does that tell you about what's actually going to happen in the days you're asking for?"

Why this lands: Universal. Reveals that "sitting with it" doesn't actually make decisions easier — it makes them harder, because doubt grows in the absence of action. Most prospects know this from their own experience.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Surgeon's Recommendation** — **Grade: A**

> "If a surgeon told you 'you need this procedure or your condition gets worse,' would you say 'let me think about it for a week'? Or would you say 'when's the soonest we can do this'?"

After they answer:

> "What's different about this decision?"

Why this lands: Reveals that "let me think about it" is reserved for decisions where the prospect doesn't actually feel the urgency. If they felt the urgency of the condition you've spent the call surfacing, they wouldn't want to delay.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Real Question** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you directly. Three days from now, what would you have to know — that you don't know right now — to make the decision easier? Be specific."

If they can name it: address it now.

If they can't:

> "If you can't name what you'd know in three days that you don't know now — then 'thinking about it' isn't actually about thinking. It's about wanting to be away from this conversation. What's the actual reason you want to be away from it?"

Why this lands: Forces the prospect to either surface a real concern (which you can handle) or admit there isn't one. Either outcome moves the conversation forward.

**The "decision-deferral cost" move:**

> "Here's what I want to make sure you see. Every day you've been not deciding to fix this has been a day you've been actively choosing the current state. 'Let me think about it for a week' is 'let me keep losing time and money and momentum for another week.' That's not neutral — it's a vote for the path you've said isn't working. Is that what you want to vote for?"

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

If they commit: move to close.

If they identify a real concern: surface it and handle.

If they still want time: see the END-OF-CALL HANDLING section below.

### PATH B — Genuine need for specific information

**Less common but real.** The prospect has a specific information need they can articulate. Not stalling — actually missing something.

**How to recognize:**

- Can articulate a specific question ("I need to look at my Q4 numbers")
- The question is concrete and answerable
- Calm energy, not avoidant
- They're treating this like a business decision

**This is not a reframe — this is a handling situation. Address the specific need.**

**The move:**

> "Fair. What specifically do you need to confirm?"

If it's information you can provide on the call: provide it.

If it's something they need to look at offline (financial picture, calendar): set a specific follow-up.

> "Okay. When you look at [the specific thing], what would the answer need to be for you to move forward? And if it answers favorably, are you ready to commit?"

The last question is critical. It converts the deferral into a conditional commitment. If they say yes, you have a clear path. If they hedge, you're back in Path A.

**Transition for Path B:**

If addressed on call: close.

If genuinely needs offline check: set firm follow-up time.

> "I'll send you a calendar invite for [specific day/time]. By then you'll have looked at [the specific thing]. We'll either start, or you'll have clarity on why not. Sound fair?"

### PATH C — Real major-decision processing need

**Rare.** Genuine need to sit with a significant decision. More common for very high-ticket investments ($100K+) or when the prospect is in a specific decision-making style that genuinely requires processing time.

**How to recognize:**

- Specific request for time with a specific reason
- "I'm a slow processor on major decisions"
- They reference past decisions they made well by taking time
- Not avoidant — just operating in their decision style
- Often analytical, methodical types

**This is also not really a reframe. It's about setting clear next steps.**

**The move:**

> "I respect that. What I don't want to happen is for the time to extend indefinitely. Can we set a specific point — say, [3 business days] — where we have a follow-up call and you tell me yes, no, or what you need to decide? That way you get the processing time and we keep the decision moving."

After they agree:

> "And between now and then, what specifically are you going to process? What questions are you sitting with?"

This converts vague "thinking" into specific deliberation with a deadline. Most prospects who actually need processing time will agree because it honors their style.

**Transition for Path C:**

Set the follow-up firmly. End the call with care. Most Path C prospects who agree to a firm follow-up do close on the follow-up.

## END-OF-CALL HANDLING (CRITICAL)

If after handling the "think about it" objection the prospect still wants time, here are the moves in order of preference:

**Move 1: The honest direct ask**

> "Honest question — are you trying to give yourself space to find a reason to say no? Because I'd rather you tell me now what's actually stopping you than have you go think about it, find a reason, and ghost me. What's actually going on?"

This often surfaces the real objection. Some prospects will admit it.

**Move 2: The structured follow-up**

If they genuinely want time, set firm parameters:

> "Okay. Here's what I'll ask. I want a specific time — not 'next week,' but a specific date and time — where we get back on a call and you tell me yes, no, or what's still in the way. I'd rather hear no from you than have you drift. Can we set that?"

If they agree, set it before ending the call. If they hedge on the follow-up time, that's the answer — they don't want to commit.

**Move 3: The respectful no**

If they won't set a follow-up time, the truth is they're not going to do this. Respect it.

> "Okay. Sounds like you might not be in the right place for this. No harm in that. Let me know if anything changes."

End the call respectfully. Don't chase. Don't follow up endlessly. The prospects who matter come back.

**What you NEVER do:**

- Pretend other prospects are competing for your time ("I have other people interested")
- Create artificial scarcity ("the price goes up tomorrow")
- Get desperate or salesy
- Send a long follow-up email trying to close them after the call

These tactics damage your reputation and rarely close the actual sale. The honest, direct move closes more sales over time AND protects your brand.

## SWEEP TRANSITION

Note: "Need to think about it" usually surfaces near the END of the call, not in the middle of the lateral sweep. If it surfaces during sweep, treat it as a deflection and don't accept it:

> "Sure, we can come back to that. Right now I want to keep clearing what's in the way. Aside from time/money/whatever — what else?"

Don't let "let me think about it" pull you out of the sweep prematurely.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 NEED TO THINK ABOUT IT phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Check timing** — is this surfacing mid-call (sweep) or end-call (post-pitch)?
3. If mid-call: redirect back to sweep
4. If end-call: present SCRIPT options with S highlighted aggressively
5. After S, diagnose path (A stall, B specific info, C real processing)
6. Load matched handling card
7. Closer deploys handling
8. If commits: move to close
9. If still resistant: end-of-call handling sequence

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I make significant investments thoughtfully. I want a few days to sit with this and review the financial side."
- "I appreciate the offer. Let me think through it and circle back."
- "I never make decisions of this size on the call."

Type A often presents this as a methodical practice. May be Path B or Path C. Honor the rigor while setting firm next steps. The Real Question move (Analogy 3) works well.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I just need some time to process this."
- "This is a lot. Let me sit with it."
- "I want to be sure before I commit."

Type B often presents this from emotional overwhelm. Almost always Path A. Lead with the surgeon analogy or the decision-doesn't-get-clearer move. Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes

- Calm, not pushy
- Curious about what specifically needs thinking
- Direct but not aggressive
- After the participatory analogies, hold silence
- If they're going to leave without committing, leave cleanly — no desperation

### Friction budget notes

If this surfaces at end of call, you have most of your friction budget already spent. The handling should be efficient — one good participatory move, one direct ask, and a clear next step. Don't run multiple reframes here. The prospect's energy is already declining.

### Special: when the prospect tries to ghost after agreeing to follow-up

If you set a firm follow-up and the prospect ghosts, the move is one short, dignified message:

> "Hey [name] — we had [follow-up time] on the calendar. Want to confirm you're still on for it, or you've decided this isn't right? Either is fine. Just want to know which."

If they don't respond after one message, let it go. The decision has been made by their silence.

### Adjacent: "I need to sleep on it"

Variant. The move:

> "Of course. Before you do — what specifically do you think will be clearer in the morning?"

If they can name something specific: address it.

If they can't:

> "In my experience, decisions don't get clearer overnight — they get cloudier. The morning version of you will have less of the energy from this conversation and more of the doubt voices that show up at 6am. What would change tonight that would make tomorrow's version of you more committed to action?"

Often closes on the spot.

### Adjacent: "Can you send me the details and let me review?"

Variant disguised as a documentation request. The move:

> "I can. Before I do — what specifically would the documents change about your decision? Because in my experience, when I send documents to people who 'need to think,' the documents don't actually move the decision — the decision is being made now, whether you commit or defer. What are you actually deciding?"

Surfaces that documents aren't the issue.
