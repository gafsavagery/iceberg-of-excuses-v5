## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant — when examining why they haven't acted yet)

- "I was just so busy"
- "Last year was crazy at work"
- "I had too much on my plate at the time"
- "The business was taking all my attention"
- "I was working around the clock"
- "Between the kids and the company, there was no room"
- "Things kept coming up"
- "I never had the time to really focus on it"
- "I kept telling myself I'd get to it"
- "Every quarter felt like the wrong quarter"

### Present-tense (when prospect is currently paralyzed)

- "I'm just so busy right now"
- "I don't have the bandwidth"
- "Maybe in a few months when things calm down"
- "I'm slammed right now"
- "Now's not the right time"
- "Between everything I've got going on..."

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Calendar full of low-leverage reactive work
- Saying yes to other people's priorities
- Treating busyness as a virtue or identity marker
- Using "busy" as armor against the harder internal work
- Genuinely overwhelmed and unable to scope properly
- Avoiding the specific thing this would address

## SCRIPT PATH

For TIME, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, and P**. The others apply situationally.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you were too busy — what specifically was on your plate then?"

**Present:** "When you say busy — what specifically is on your plate right now?"

**What you're listening for:** How much of their plate is high-priority vs. low-priority. Most overcommitted prospects have lots of low-priority items they've said yes to. That's the lever. Listen for reactive work (email, meetings, fire-fighting) vs. proactive work (strategic, creative, hard decisions).

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what your year actually looked like back then. What were you working on day to day?"

**Present:** "Walk me through a typical week. What does your calendar actually look like?"

**What you're listening for:** Patterns — recurring meetings they don't need to be in, "yes" commitments to others, unprotected time blocks they fill with reactive work, lack of strategic time.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long were you in that busy season? Was it a few months, or did it stretch longer?"

**Present:** "How long have you been telling yourself you're too busy to deal with this?"

**What you're listening for:** Most prospects will say it lasted longer than they initially implied. "I was busy" usually means "I've been telling myself I'm busy for two years." When you anchor the timeframe, you reveal that "busy" has been a chronic state, not an acute one. Chronic states aren't real conditions — they're operating patterns.

### I — Involved (deploy when they reference obligations to others)

**Past:** "Who specifically was your time going to back then? Whose priorities were running your calendar?"

**Present:** "Who specifically is your time going to? Whose priorities are running your calendar right now?"

**What you're listening for:** People-pleasing patterns. Most overcommitted prospects are busy because they've said yes to other people. The Involved question makes the people visible. Listen for: spouse, family, employees, board, friends, peers.

### R — Roots (deploy when the busyness feels identity-coded)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn that being busy means you're succeeding? Whose voice was that originally?"

**What you're listening for:** Inherited belief that capacity equals worth. Often parental ("dad worked 80-hour weeks and that's what success looked like"). Often early-career imprinting ("hustle culture taught me to fill every hour").

### C — Compared to what (rarely used for TIME, more relevant for money)

Skip for most TIME conversations. Deploy only if prospect uses comparative language like "I have less time than most people."

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

After SCRIPT surfaces specifics, the prospect's answer falls into one of three paths.

### PATH A — They itemize and reveal LOW-LEVERAGE WORK is filling the time

**Most common case.** They were "busy" with email, meetings, reactive work, things they could have delegated. The reframe: being busy and being effective are different things.

**How to recognize:** Their itemized list is mostly tactical, mostly reactive, mostly things a competent employee could handle. They're spending time on inputs, not outputs.

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Of everything you just listed — how much of it was actually the highest-leverage use of your time?"
- "How much of that could someone else have handled if you'd been willing to let it go?"
- "If past-you had taken a step back two years ago and looked at this list — what would past-you have said?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You're a CEO. If two years ago, one of your employees came to you and said 'I haven't gotten to that critical project because I've been too busy with other things' — and they kept saying that for two years — would they still have a job?"

- "When you've made time for things in the past that mattered enough to you — what did that actually look like? How did you do it?"

- "If you'd told yourself two years ago that you'd still be saying 'I'm too busy' today — would past-you have made different choices? What would past-you have done differently?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Employee Reframe (Tristan, verbatim/adapted)** — **Grade: A**

> "You manage employees, right? If two years ago, one of your direct reports kept telling you 'I haven't gotten to the critical project because I've been too busy with other things' — for two years — what would you have done with that person?"

After they answer (they'll say "fired them" or "let them go"): 

> "So what's been different about how you've been talking to yourself about it?"

Why this lands for high-earning ICP: They manage people. They have zero tolerance for "I'm too busy" as a permanent excuse from their team. Making them see they've accepted that excuse from themselves is the lever. Don't rush past their answer — let them sit with the contradiction.

**ANALOGY 2 — The $10K Offer Adapted (Tristan, adapted)** — **Grade: B+**

> "If two years ago I'd called you and said 'I need 30 minutes of your time today — this is genuinely urgent and I'll pay you $10,000 for it' — would you have found the 30 minutes?"

After they say yes:

> "So time wasn't the constraint. What was?"

Why this lands: Forces them to acknowledge they CAN find time when the stakes are clear. The constraint isn't time — it's their assessment of importance. Don't accept "well that's different" — push: "What made $10K obviously worth 30 minutes, but a problem costing you millions wasn't?"

**ANALOGY 3 — The Elon Musk Comparison (Itai, original)** — **Grade: B-**

> "Elon Musk runs three companies, has more kids than I can count, and somehow finds time for everything. What do you think the difference is between him and you when it comes to time?"

After they answer:

> "So if everyone has the same 24 hours, and some people fit massive things into them — what does that tell you about the 'I'm too busy' story?"

Why the lower grade for your ICP: High-earning entrepreneurs in their 40s often have a sophisticated take on Musk (admiration AND skepticism). The comparison can feel forced or even backwards (they don't actually want to be Musk). Use only if Analogies 1 and 2 don't fit.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So 'busy' was the story you told yourself. What else was getting in the way back then?"

### PATH B — They reveal they were GENUINELY OVERWHELMED

**Less common but real.** Some prospects had actual chaos — major business growth, personal crisis, real emergency. In those cases, "I was busy" is true. The reframe shifts.

**How to recognize:** Their itemized list is heavy on legitimate high-stakes work, real crisis events, or genuine resource constraints (illness, divorce, child crisis, business survival mode).

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "So last year was genuinely intense. What's been true the last six months — and what'll be true six months from now?"
- "Has the busy actually ended, or has 'busy' become the default state?"
- "If the busy never fully ends — when does this problem actually get solved?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You've been waiting for the busy to end so you can deal with this. What if the busy never ends? When does this get fixed?"

- "If you're running a business that's growing, the demands don't decrease — they increase. So waiting for 'less busy' is actually waiting for [their business] to fail. Is that the plan?"

- "What if the reason you're so busy is downstream of the very thing this work would solve? What if the busyness is the symptom?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Tide Metaphor (extrapolated, fits Identity Architect frame)** — **Grade: A**

> "You've been waiting for the tide to go out so you can clean the beach. If you're running a company that's growing — does the tide ever go out?"

After they answer (no):

> "So if the tide never goes out, what does it mean to wait for it before doing the work?"

Why this lands: It validates the legitimacy of their overwhelm AND reframes the waiting as itself a failure mode. They have to construct the realization that the work has to happen IN the tide, not after.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Surgery Metaphor** — **Grade: A**

> "If a surgeon told you 'we need to operate but let's wait until you're not so stressed at work' — what would you say? When does a real condition wait for convenient timing?"

After they answer:

> "So why is this different?"

Why this lands: Frames the work as a real condition, not a luxury. Premium prospects understand medical metaphors immediately.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "So even though last year was real intensity — the waiting itself was part of the problem. What else has been keeping you from fixing this?"

### PATH C — They CONCEDE the busyness was an excuse

**The gold path.** The prospect admits, often quietly, that they could have made time but didn't. This is the Layer 2 admission and you do NOT dive vertical. You sweep.

**How to recognize:**

- Voice drops or pause
- "Honestly..." or "If I'm being real..."
- "I guess I could have made time"
- "I was probably just avoiding it"

**Acknowledgment + lateral sweep (don't dive yet):**

> "Okay. So if it wasn't really about time — there was something else going on. We'll come back to that. Aside from time, what else has been getting in the way?"

The phrase "we'll come back to that" is important. You're flagging the admission and parking it. The discipline is to clear the lateral sweep before going deep on the fear.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe is concluded:

> "Okay, so 'busy' wasn't actually the real issue — it was the story you'd been telling yourself. Aside from time, what else has been in the way?"

Variants:

- "If we set that aside as not the real issue — what's the next thing on the list?"
- "What else has been stopping you from fixing this?"
- "Beyond time — what else?"

Note: "Aside from" is the verbatim Itai/Tristan phrasing for the lateral sweep. The phrase presumes more exist. Don't soften it ("are there any other reasons?"). The presumption is the leverage.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 TIME phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as the active reframe context
2. Present the SCRIPT options card with S, T, P highlighted as primary (others available but greyed)
3. Wait for closer to select which SCRIPT letter to deploy
4. After closer asks the SCRIPT question, the prospect responds (Type A or B variation — see below)
5. Sim presents the path diagnosis card: "Path A, B, or C?" with the criteria for each
6. After closer diagnoses, sim loads the matched reframe card with follow-up questions and analogies
7. Closer deploys the reframe
8. Sim presents the concession-check card: did the prospect concede?
9. If yes → sweep transition card → load next excuse file
10. If no → loop back to additional reframe questions for the same path

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's response to TIME excuses sounds like:

- "Yeah, last year was particularly rough. We had a major product launch, two key team transitions, and I was personally running point on the new revenue stream. There genuinely wasn't bandwidth."
- "I'm running a $5M business with 12 employees. Every hour I'm not in execution is an hour the business doesn't grow."
- "I had a clear strategic justification for the time I was spending."

Type A defends with structure and rationalization. The reframe has to attack the rationalization with their own data (Type 1 or Type 3 reframes work best).

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's response to TIME excuses sounds like:

- "I just felt like I was drowning. Every day there was something."
- "It felt like I never had a chance to breathe."
- "I was so overwhelmed I couldn't even think about it."

Type B defends with emotion and overwhelm. The reframe has to acknowledge the emotional reality first, then redirect (Type 4 comparison expansion works well, with extra warmth).

### Voice tone notes for the closer (via ElevenLabs prompt if applicable)

- Steady, curious, not interrogative
- Use prospect's own language back at them
- After deploying the analogy question, **let silence sit** until they answer
- Don't fill the silence with restatements

### Friction budget notes

TIME is usually the FIRST excuse a prospect raises. Don't spend too much budget here. Goal: surface the specifics, reframe quickly, sweep to next. Save budget for deeper externals and the fear extraction.

If the prospect digs in hard on TIME and won't move, that's data — they're using TIME as armor against the deeper work. Note it and lateral sweep anyway. The accumulating sweep will eventually crack it open.

### When the reframe doesn't land

If the closer deploys the reframe and the prospect resists ("yeah but you don't understand my situation"), the sim should:

1. Flag the resistance as a porcupine moment
2. Offer the closer a permission preamble option: "Can I push on this a bit?"
3. Load a secondary reframe (different path or different analogy)
4. If two reframes don't land, lateral sweep anyway. The accumulation does the work.

## ADVANCED: WHEN TIME IS THE REAL CONSTRAINT (RARE)

In some real situations, time genuinely is the constraint:

- Prospect is in active medical crisis
- Prospect is in active family emergency
- Prospect's business is in acute survival mode

In these cases, the reframe is NOT to deny the constraint. The reframe is to surface that the work this offer addresses is downstream of (or causal to) the crisis they're in.

**Move:** "I hear that this is a real moment. Let me ask — the situation you're in right now, is it the kind of situation where solving the identity-level work would help you handle it better? Or do you genuinely need to deal with the crisis first and revisit this in 90 days?"

If they say "first," qualify them out gracefully. Don't sell into a real crisis. Future-them will remember the integrity.

If they say "this would help," you've just earned their commitment in the middle of their hardest moment.
