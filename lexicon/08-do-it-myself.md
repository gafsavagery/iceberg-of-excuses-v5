## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I thought I could figure it out on my own"
- "I was trying to do it myself"
- "I figured I'd just read the books and apply what I learned"
- "I've always been someone who figures things out"
- "I didn't want to rely on someone else"
- "I bought the courses and tried to implement on my own"
- "I thought I could DIY it"
- "I was working through it on my own"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I'd rather try to figure this out myself first"
- "I'm pretty good at self-directed learning"
- "I can probably figure this out on my own"
- "I usually do things myself"
- "Why would I pay someone when I could just read the right books?"
- "I'm self-taught in everything else"
- "I don't really need a coach for this"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Identity as "the smart one who figures everything out"
- Avoidance of vulnerability that comes with being coached
- Pride/ego as armor against being helped
- Genuine self-sufficiency from a lifetime of having to figure things out alone
- Fear of being dependent on someone
- Conflating "I CAN figure it out" with "I should"
- The specific avoidance of being SEEN needing help
- Past experience of help-as-control or help-as-debt
- Sometimes: rational assessment that the problem is genuinely solvable solo

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This is the high-performer's signature defense. Your ICP — high-earning entrepreneurs — almost all carry this pattern. They got where they are precisely BECAUSE they're capable of figuring things out. The pattern that built their success is now blocking the next level of their growth.

**The trap most coaches fall into:** Arguing that the prospect can't figure it out alone. This is false (they often technically could) and it triggers the prospect's "I'll prove you wrong" identity.

**The actual move:** Concede that they probably CAN figure it out alone. The question isn't capability — it's time, leverage, and the specific blind spots that prevent the solo approach from working. They've been "figuring it out" for X years and they're still stuck. That data is the lever.

**For high-performers, the reframe is about leverage, not capability.** Stop selling them on the idea that they need help. Sell them on the idea that doing it alone has been the slowest path.

## SCRIPT PATH

For DO IT MYSELF, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, P, and R**. All four are commonly needed.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you were trying to figure it out yourself — what specifically were you doing?"

**Present:** "When you say you can figure it out — what does figuring it out specifically look like?"

**What you're listening for:** The actual self-directed work they've done. Most "I'll figure it out" prospects have consumed massive amounts of content (books, podcasts, courses, frameworks) without successfully implementing. The volume of consumption vs. the lack of result is the lever.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what you've actually tried. What approaches did you use, and what came of them?"

**Present:** "Walk me through how you plan to figure this out. What's the approach?"

**What you're listening for:** The pattern of self-directed attempts. Listen for: book lists they've read, courses they've bought, frameworks they've studied, methodologies they've tried. Then ask what actually moved the needle.

### P — Point in time (CRITICAL — deploy this letter)

**Past:** "How long have you been trying to figure this out on your own? Months? Years?"

**Present:** "How long have you been working on this solo? When did you start trying to solve it yourself?"

**What you're listening for:** The duration of the self-directed phase. Most prospects will reveal that they've been "figuring it out" for years with limited progress. The duration combined with the lack of breakthrough is the entire reframe.

### R — Roots (CRITICAL for this excuse — almost always relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn that you had to figure everything out on your own? Whose voice was that originally?"

**What you're listening for:** This excuse is almost ALWAYS rooted in childhood. Listen for:

- Parent who didn't show up / wasn't available
- Sibling dynamics where they were "the responsible one"
- Early experiences of being let down by helpers
- Cultural conditioning around self-reliance as virtue
- Specific painful moment of asking for help and being denied or shamed

This is often the moment that connects to the deeper fear underneath. Don't rush past it.

### I — Involved (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Have you let other people in to help you with hard things in your life? Or has self-reliance been the pattern across the board?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether the DIY pattern is specific to this domain or pervasive across their life. If pervasive, this is identity work, not skill work.

### C — Compared to what (occasionally useful)

**Either tense:** "Compared to people who've solved this faster — with help — how does your timeline compare?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can acknowledge that people with mentors/coaches typically progress faster. Many high-performers can intellectually grant this but emotionally resist applying it to themselves.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — The high-performer who has been doing it alone for years

**Most common case for your ICP.** Successful person who's built their life through self-direction. The pattern that built their success is now slowing their growth. They have consumed massive amounts of self-help content with limited transformation.

**How to recognize:**

- Has built something significant (business, career, expertise)
- Can name many self-development inputs (books, courses, podcasts)
- Has been working on this specific problem for multiple years
- Articulate about the problem but stuck on the solution
- May reference "I've already read all the books on this"

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Of all the self-development work you've done over the last X years, how much of it has actually translated to changed behavior or changed identity?"
- "If 'figuring it out yourself' was working, would we be having this conversation?"
- "What's the difference between knowing something intellectually and actually living it differently?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You've been working on this on your own for [duration]. Let me ask honestly — is the gap closing? Are you measurably closer to the version of yourself you want to be than you were a year ago?"

- "You've consumed [their listed inputs]. If the information was the constraint, you'd be there already. So what's actually been the missing piece?"

- "Self-directed growth has gotten you to where you are. Has it been the fastest path? Or just the path you defaulted to because it didn't require trusting someone?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Athlete Coach Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Every Olympic athlete has a coach. They're already the best in the world. They could absolutely train alone — they have the discipline, the knowledge, the access to the same resources. Why do they all choose to work with a coach instead?"

After they answer (the coach sees what they can't see, accountability, faster optimization):

> "What's been different about how you've approached your own growth?"

Why this lands: Activates their respect for elite performance. The world's best in any domain have coaches not because they can't figure it out — because they can't see their own blind spots and they don't want to spend years finding what someone could show them in weeks. The prospect has been holding themselves to a standard that no elite performer holds themselves to.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Therapist Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "When you have a specific physical health issue, you see a specialist. You don't try to figure out your spinal injury by reading books. Why do you treat identity-level work differently?"

After they answer (sometimes: it's "soft" / not as concrete):

> "Or is it that the specialist requires you to be vulnerable in a way the books don't? Books don't see you. A coach does."

Why this lands: Names the actual reason DIY appeals — books don't witness you. The vulnerability of being seen is the friction. Reveals that the preference for self-directed isn't about efficiency — it's about avoidance of being witnessed.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Surgeon on Themselves** — **Grade: B+**

> "If a surgeon needed surgery, would they perform it on themselves? They have the knowledge. They have the skills. Why is that not the move?"

After they answer (can't get the right angle on themselves, can't be objective about their own situation):

> "How does that map to what you've been trying to do with your own identity work?"

Why this lands: Reveals that even with the technical skill, you can't operate on yourself. Some work fundamentally requires another person. Direct, memorable.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Mirror Limit** — **Grade: A**

> "You can look in a mirror and see your face. But can you see the back of your head without help? What can't you see about yourself that someone else could?"

After they answer:

> "What's been the cost of trying to see what you can't see, alone?"

Why this lands: Simple, physical, immediate. Makes the case for outside perspective without arguing against capability. The prospect can't deny that there are angles they literally can't see on themselves.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So 'I'll figure it out myself' has been the slowest path, not the smartest one. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — DIY rooted in childhood pattern of having no one to rely on

**Common for emotionally underbuilt high-performers.** The DIY pattern isn't really about preference — it's about survival training from childhood. They never had reliable help, so they learned to provide it for themselves. The pattern became identity.

**How to recognize:**

- R question surfaces a clear childhood origin
- Parent who was absent, addicted, ill, or emotionally unavailable
- They were "the responsible one" in their family young
- Pattern is pervasive across life (not just professional)
- Tone shifts when discussing the origin — gets quieter, more careful

**This is sacred material. Treat it as such.** This is often where the actual identity work begins surfacing.

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "When you were a kid, who was there when you needed help? Who held you?"
- "When did you realize you had to figure things out alone?"
- "What was the cost of being the kid who had to handle everything?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What you learned in childhood made sense for that situation. It was survival. AND you're not a kid anymore. The conditions that required total self-reliance aren't the same. What if the pattern that protected you then is limiting you now?"

- "Is it possible that 'I can figure it out myself' isn't about capability — it's about a wound that taught you not to need anyone? And what's that cost you in relationships, in growth, in being known?"

- "If you could go back and give that kid version of yourself permission to ask for help — would you? What would be different if he'd had that?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Old Operating System** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you learned in childhood that the only safe way to operate was alone, because nobody was reliable. That wasn't a bug — it was an adaptation that kept you safe. It worked. AND you're not in that environment anymore. Are you running an operating system that fit the old environment? What environment are you in now?"

After they answer:

> "What would it mean to update the OS to match the actual environment you're in now?"

Why this lands: Names the protective function of the pattern without pathologizing it. Honors that it was adaptive and reveals it's outdated. Doesn't make the prospect wrong — makes the pattern dated.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Kid Asking** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine the kid version of you needed help with something. He approached an adult and asked. What happened?"

Wait for their answer. Sit with it. Then:

> "And what did kid-you learn from that?"

Wait again.

> "And what's it cost adult-you to keep operating from that lesson?"

Why this lands: Goes directly to the source. Most prospects can locate the specific moment they learned not to ask. Naming it makes it visible as a learned response, not an inherent truth. This often produces the somatic aha — the moment when the prospect realizes they're still acting on a 7-year-old's conclusion.

**Important:** Don't rush this. The pauses between questions are the work. Let them sit in the memory.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "What you just shared is real. The pattern of not asking for help — it goes back further than this decision. We'll come back to that. Aside from the DIY piece, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Note: Path B often surfaces Layer 3 material directly. If the prospect has a somatic aha here, you may not even need to complete the full sweep — the fear has already surfaced. Use judgment.

### PATH C — Genuine self-sufficient learner who legitimately doesn't need a coach for this

**Rare but real.** Some prospects genuinely have the capability, the time, and the self-direction to solve the problem on their own. They're not avoiding — they're correctly assessing that solo work fits them.

**How to recognize:**

- Has actually applied self-development inputs with real results
- Can point to specific transformations from self-directed work
- Time and bandwidth are genuinely available
- The problem isn't urgent or compounding
- No emotional charge in the DIY preference

**This is not a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation.**

**The honest move:**

> "Honestly, some people do work better self-directed. Let me ask — what specifically about this problem makes you think solo work will solve it? And what's your honest timeline for seeing change without help?"

If they have a clear plan and timeline that's reasonable, the right answer might be: "Then go do that. Come back if you get stuck. I'm not going to push you into something you're not aligned with."

You may lose the sale. The prospect will respect you. They may come back in 6-12 months ready to commit when their solo approach hasn't moved them.

**Transition language for Path C:**

If qualified out: end the call with respect.

If actually Path A masquerading as Path C: deploy Path A reframes.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe:

> "Aside from the 'I'll figure it out' piece, what else has been getting in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond going solo — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as not the real issue — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 DIY phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, P, R all highlighted (R is unusually critical for this excuse)
3. **Prompt the closer to deploy R specifically** — this is where Path A vs. Path B is distinguished
4. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path
5. Load matched reframe card
6. Closer deploys reframe with appropriate tone
7. **Flag Path B as deepening into Layer 3** — sim should suggest checking for somatic aha
8. Check for concession
9. Sweep transition or hold for somatic moment

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I've built three companies. I've taught myself accounting, sales, marketing, hiring. I've never needed someone to walk me through a learning curve."
- "I'm honestly skeptical of coaching as a category. Most of what coaches do is repackage things you can find in books for free."
- "I learn fast. I don't need someone to spoon-feed me."

Type A defends with capability and history of self-direction. The Athlete Coach reframe (Analogy 1) is most effective — activates their respect for elite performance. Don't argue capability — concede it and shift to leverage.

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I've been reading self-help books for 10 years. I should be able to apply this stuff."
- "I don't want to be one of those people who needs hand-holding."
- "I'm too proud to ask for help."

Type B often more openly acknowledges the pattern. Path B (childhood origin) surfaces more easily. Deploy R early. The Kid Asking analogy (Path B Analogy 2) lands particularly hard.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't argue capability — concede it
- Use respectful, peer-level tone
- After R deploy, slow down significantly — childhood material requires space
- Hold silence after participatory analogies, especially Path B
- If somatic aha emerges during Path B, pause the sweep and stay in the moment

### Friction budget notes

DIY can take significant budget if Path B surfaces because it often connects to deep identity material. That's actually good — it's the work surfacing. Don't try to lateral sweep past a real somatic moment. The accumulated lexicon work means you have budget reserved for this.

If Path A only, the reframe can land quickly with the Athlete or Mirror analogy.

### Special: when prospect says "I should be able to figure this out"

The word "should" is the tell. The move:

> "Why 'should'? Where did that 'should' come from? Whose standard is that?"

Surfaces the inherited expectation that creates the DIY pressure. Often Path B material.

### Special: when prospect uses past success against you

> "I built a $5M business by myself. I'm not going to need help with this."

The move (without arguing):

> "You're absolutely capable. I'm not going to tell you you can't figure this out. Let me ask a different question — has 'figuring it out alone' been your fastest path, or just your default path? And is fastest what you want right now, or something else?"

This converts the conversation from capability (where they win) to optimization (where the reframe applies).

### Special: when DIY connects to fear of being controlled

Some prospects' DIY pattern is rooted in past experiences of being controlled or manipulated by someone who claimed to help. The pattern is:

> "I've been burned by people who said they wanted to help."

Validate without bypassing:

> "That's real. There's a difference between help that controls and help that empowers. Tell me — what would help that didn't try to control you look like?"

Then position your work as that — help that builds their autonomy, not dependence. Identity work specifically is designed to make them MORE autonomous, not more dependent.

## A NOTE ON THE DIY → REVENUE CONNECTION

For your specific ICP, "I'll figure it out myself" is the most common LEFT-ON-THE-TABLE excuse. High-performers buy this pattern from themselves indefinitely. They consume content, attend workshops, read books — and stay exactly where they are because the pattern that prevents them from being helped is the pattern that's keeping them stuck.

The closer who can handle this excuse with respect and skill captures a market segment that most coaches lose entirely. The prospects most worth working with are often the ones with the strongest DIY pattern. Building muscle on this specific reframe is high-leverage.
