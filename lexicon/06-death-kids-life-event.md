## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I lost my mom that year and it threw everything off"
- "My dad got sick and I was focused on caregiving"
- "We had a death in the family"
- "I was dealing with a divorce"
- "I had a kid that year — life got chaotic"
- "We just had our second/third baby and I was in survival mode"
- "I was raising young kids and there wasn't space for anything else"
- "There was a custody issue I was navigating"
- "I was going through cancer treatment"
- "We moved that year and everything was upheaval"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I'm dealing with my dad's illness right now"
- "I just had a baby"
- "We're in the middle of [major life transition]"
- "I'm a single parent and there's no bandwidth"
- "My partner is sick and I'm the primary caregiver"
- "I'm in a custody battle"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Real life event that legitimately consumed bandwidth (must be honored)
- Past life event that's become permanent permission not to act
- Life event being used as cover for fear that predates it
- Parenting/caregiving as identity that crowds out personal growth
- Conflating "this season is hard" with "I can't ever grow during a hard season"
- Sometimes: actively in crisis and shouldn't be selling to

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — READ THIS FIRST

**This is the most delicate excuse in the entire lexicon. Mishandling it does real harm.**

Death, illness, divorce, new babies, caregiving — these are real life events that legitimately affect bandwidth, attention, and capacity. Treating them as just another excuse to reframe is callous and breaks trust permanently.

**The closer's job is to:**

1. **Honor the event genuinely.** Don't perform sympathy. Don't move past it quickly. Sit with what they're describing.
2. **Distinguish acute from chronic.** Acute life events are real constraints. Chronic life-event narratives that have lasted years are different.
3. **Never qualify in someone who's actively in crisis.** If they're in the middle of grief, illness, or new-parent overwhelm, they shouldn't be on a sales call. The integrity move is to step back.
4. **Make space for what they actually need to say.** Sometimes the prospect needs to process the event with someone before they can think about anything else. Be that person, not the closer trying to push past it.

**You will lose some sales by handling this with integrity. You will also build trust that compounds across your career. The reputation cost of pushing someone in crisis is enormous and irreversible.**

## SCRIPT PATH

For DEATH/KIDS/LIFE EVENT, the most leveraged letters are **T and P**. Start with Tell-me-the-story, not Specifically. The latter can feel cold here.

### T — Tell me the story (DEPLOY FIRST — opens with warmth)

**Past:** "Tell me about that. What happened, and what was it like for you?"

**Present:** "Tell me what's going on. What are you in the middle of right now?"

**What you're listening for:**

- The actual event and its current state (active vs. resolved)
- Their emotional state when describing it
- Whether they want to talk about it or want to move past it
- How long ago it was and whether they're still in it
- Their support structure (alone with it or supported)

This is the most important moment for tone. Slow down. Don't run SCRIPT mechanically. Be human first.

### P — Point in time (deploy carefully, only after T)

**Past:** "How long ago was that? And what's been true for you since?"

**Present:** "Where are you in the timeline of this? Acute crisis, or further along?"

**What you're listening for:** The acute/chronic distinction. Recent and active = different conversation than years ago and being used as permanent permission.

### S — Specifically (deploy after T and P, only if needed)

**Either tense:** "What specifically has been hardest about navigating this?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have specific ongoing constraints or generalized "everything is hard." Specifics can sometimes be worked with; generalized overwhelm is different territory.

### I — Involved (deploy when appropriate)

**Either tense:** "Who's been there with you through this? What's your support system?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have support. If they're isolated, that's a different problem than constraint. Sometimes the offer of the coaching relationship itself is part of what they need.

### R — Roots (rarely used here)

Skip unless the pattern of using life events as permission has clear inherited origins.

### C — Compared to what (rarely used)

Skip in most cases.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Acute, active crisis — DO NOT SELL

**Critical case.** The prospect is currently in the middle of a major life event. New baby weeks old, parent in hospice, going through active divorce, etc.

**How to recognize:**

- Event is current or very recent
- Emotional charge in their voice
- They're describing logistics of survival, not life pattern
- "I literally can't think about anything else right now"
- They sound exhausted, not strategic

**This is not a reframe situation. This is a qualification-out situation.**

**The move:**

> "I hear what you're in the middle of. I want to be honest with you. This isn't the right time for this kind of work. You need bandwidth you don't have right now, and starting something significant in the middle of [their event] is going to either hurt the work or hurt you. Or both."

> "What I'd want you to do is take care of [the immediate priority]. Come back to me in three to six months when you have some space. If this is right for you then, it'll still be right for you then. Right now your job is to survive this season, not to take on a major new commitment."

**Why this works:**

- It's true. They shouldn't be buying.
- It builds enormous trust. Most coaches would push for the sale. You won't.
- They'll remember this. When they're ready, they'll come back.
- You protect your reputation and your clients' actual lives.

**What you absolutely don't do:** "But this work is exactly what would help you handle [the crisis] better." False. The crisis needs to be handled with normal life-support resources (therapy, family, time, medical care). Coaching isn't crisis management.

**Close the call with care:**

> "Take my contact info. When you're through the worst of this, reach back out. I'm not going anywhere."

End the call. Don't try to keep it open with "we could maybe..." moves. Clean exit.

### PATH B — Past event being used as ongoing permission

**Common.** Something real happened — death, divorce, illness, major life transition — but it was 1+ years ago. The event has become a permanent narrative for why they haven't acted, even though life has moved on.

**How to recognize:**

- Event was 1-3+ years ago
- They reference it as still affecting them currently
- Day-to-day life has actually stabilized but they haven't restarted growth
- Emotional charge is less acute, more rehearsed
- "I'm still recovering from..." or "Ever since..."

**Important:** The grief or impact may still be real. The narrative-as-permission is separate from the actual emotional impact. You can honor the latter while gently examining the former.

**Follow-up questions:**

- "What's life looked like since then? What's stabilized?"
- "How would you have described yourself before that happened?"
- "What did you used to be working toward before [the event]?"
- "If [the person who died / the event] could see you right now, what would they want for you?"

The last question is critical. It's the move that converts grief into motivation. The deceased almost always would want the prospect to thrive, not to stay in suspended animation.

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "What happened was real, and it changed you. AND you're still here. What do you owe to your continued life, even with the grief?"

- "Sometimes a life event becomes a reason to never act again. Sometimes it becomes the reason we finally act. Which version are you choosing?"

- "If [the deceased / the past version of you before the event] could see you stuck where you are — would they be proud of how you've honored what happened? Or would they want more for you?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Permission to Live** — **Grade: A**

> "When someone we love dies, we sometimes treat being happy as betrayal. Like if we move forward, we're forgetting them. But the people who love us don't want us frozen. What would [the person] actually want for you right now?"

After they answer (almost always: they'd want me to live, to thrive, etc.):

> "What's been different about how you've been honoring that?"

Why this lands: Reveals the gap between what the deceased actually would want and how the prospect has been treating the grief. Most people are accidentally treating frozenness as loyalty. The reframe gives them permission to grow as the honoring move, not the betrayal move. Use only when grief is genuinely involved, not for non-death life events.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Continuation Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "You went through something hard and you survived. The version of you that's emerged from that — what's she capable of that the pre-event version wasn't? What did surviving teach you?"

After they answer:

> "What if this work is for THAT version of you, not the pre-event version? What if the post-event version is actually ready for something the pre-event version couldn't have done?"

Why this lands: Reframes the life event as something that built capacity, not just took it. Many prospects haven't recognized the strength that survival created. Activates the post-event self as the buyer, not the wounded self.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Identity-Through-Crisis Move** — **Grade: B+ for Type A**

> "Some founders go through major life events and use them as the catalyst to redesign everything. Some get stuck. The difference isn't the event — it's what they do with what happened. Where are you on that spectrum?"

After they answer:

> "What would it mean to be on the redesign side rather than the stuck side?"

Why this lands: Activates the high-performer identity. Reveals there's a choice in how they relate to the event. Don't use this for prospects in active grief or recent loss — feels callous.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "Okay. What happened is real and you're still here. Setting aside the way that event has been the story — what else has been keeping you from moving forward?"

### PATH C — Parenting/caregiving as identity that crowds out growth

**Common, especially with women in their 30s-40s.** The prospect identifies as Mom or Caregiver to the exclusion of their own growth. Not in active crisis — just structurally giving everything to the role and treating personal growth as selfish.

**How to recognize:**

- References the kids/parent/spouse as the reason
- Often guilt-laden language
- Treats personal growth as taking from family
- "I just don't have anything left at the end of the day"
- Identity has narrowed to the caregiving role

**Follow-up questions:**

- "What do your kids see when they look at you?"
- "What would you want them to see?"
- "Who are you when you're not Mom/Dad/the caregiver?"
- "What's left of you when the role is set down?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If your daughter came to you in 20 years and said 'I've given everything to my kids and I have nothing left of myself,' what would you tell her? Would you want that for her?"

- "Kids learn what their parents model. What are you modeling right now about what it looks like to be a grown adult who's still growing?"

- "When you're constantly depleted, what version of yourself are you bringing to your kids? Is that the version they actually need from you?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Oxygen Mask** — **Grade: A**

> "On a plane, when the oxygen masks drop, what do they tell you to do? Put yours on first, then help your child. Why?"

After they answer (so you can actually help them):

> "How does that map to how you've been parenting? Have you been putting your mask on, or trying to help everyone else while suffocating?"

Why this lands: Universal, immediately relatable, makes the case that self-care isn't selfish — it's prerequisite to actually caring for others. Most caregivers see this instantly.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Modeling Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Your kids will become adults. What kind of adults do you want them to be? Specifically, what kind of relationship do you want them to have with their own growth, their own dreams, their own identity?"

After they answer (they'll say they want them to thrive, to pursue their dreams):

> "And what are you currently modeling for them about how an adult treats those things?"

Why this lands: Activates the parental concern for what kids will become. Reveals that self-sacrifice is teaching the kids to self-sacrifice. The behavior is producing the opposite of what they want their kids to inherit.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Resentment Trajectory** — **Grade: A**

> "When you've seen women who've given everything to motherhood and have nothing left — at 55, 60, 65 — how have they been? What's true about them?"

After they answer (resentful, depressed, lost identity):

> "Is that where you're headed? Is that what you want?"

Why this lands: Forces them to look at the long arc. Most have seen the older woman who gave everything and has nothing. Reveals the trajectory they're on without intervention.

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "So putting yourself completely last isn't actually serving the family — it's harming you and modeling the wrong thing for the kids. Setting that aside, what else has been in the way?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

For Path A: don't sweep. Close the call respectfully.

For Paths B and C:

> "Aside from [the life event / the caregiving role], what else has been keeping you stuck?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 DEATH/KIDS/LIFE EVENT phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Prompt the closer to use T first, not S** — soften the opening
3. After T deployed, sim responds with emotional context
4. Present path diagnosis with extra care on Path A
5. **If Path A is identified, lock the sim into the qualification-out flow** — no reframe option
6. For Paths B and C, load matched reframe card
7. Closer deploys reframe with attention to tone
8. Check for genuine concession (not just verbal agreement)
9. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "My mother passed away two years ago. It was sudden. I took six months to handle the estate and then I tried to get back to normal but everything felt different."
- "We had our third child eighteen months ago. The business has been on autopilot since."
- "My father's been declining for three years. I've been managing his care while running the company."

Type A tends toward Path B (past event still narrating). They can articulate the event and its aftermath. Reframe with the Continuation Reframe (Analogy 2) — activates the post-event capacity.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm just doing my best to keep everyone afloat right now."
- "The kids need me. I can't be selfish about my own stuff."
- "Every time I think about doing something for me, I feel guilty."

Type B tends toward Path C (caregiving as crowding-out). More emotional. Reframe with the Oxygen Mask (Analogy 1) and the Modeling Reframe (Analogy 2). Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes — CRITICAL

- Slow down for this entire excuse category
- Match their emotional register before reframing
- Use longer pauses than other excuses
- Validate the realness of the event before any reframe
- Never make them feel like the event is being treated as just another excuse
- After participatory analogies, let silence sit longer than usual — this material lands harder

### Friction budget notes

This excuse can consume significant friction budget if handled poorly, but very little if handled well. The key is:

- Open with warmth (T first, not S)
- Honor the event genuinely
- Diagnose path quickly (especially Path A — qualify out fast)
- Deploy ONE strong analogy for Paths B or C
- Sweep with care

Don't stack multiple reframes here. The emotional material is heavy. Get one to land and move on.

### Special: when the prospect breaks down

If they cry, get choked up, go quiet — STOP. Don't push. Be present.

> "Take your time. There's no rush here."

Wait until they're ready to continue. Don't fill the silence with coaching. This is the most human moment of the call. Treat it as such.

After they recover:

> "Thank you for trusting me with that. Whenever you're ready, we can keep going."

Let them decide whether to continue. If they want to stop the call and reschedule, honor it.

### Special: when the prospect uses life event to test you

Some prospects mention a major life event partly to see how you'll respond. If they're testing your humanity, the worst thing you can do is move past it quickly. The right move is to actually pause and engage.

> "I want to slow down here for a second. What you just said matters. How are you actually doing with all of that?"

This may or may not advance the sale. It always advances the relationship.

## A NOTE ABOUT MENTAL HEALTH

Some prospects will surface mental health crises in this excuse category — active depression, recent psychiatric hospitalization, thoughts of self-harm, etc.

**This is not coaching territory.** If a prospect surfaces a mental health crisis:

1. Acknowledge it directly
2. Validate that getting professional support is the right move
3. Provide resources if appropriate (988 in the US, etc.)
4. Do not sell into a mental health crisis
5. Offer to revisit when they're stable

The closer's job is to be a human, not a salesperson, in those moments. The sale is irrelevant compared to the person's wellbeing.
