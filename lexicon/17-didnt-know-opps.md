## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant — this excuse is almost always retrospective)

- "I had no idea opportunities like this even existed"
- "I didn't know this kind of work was a thing"
- "I had no idea people could fix this kind of pattern"
- "If I'd known this existed, I would have done it years ago"
- "I just didn't know there were options like this"
- "Nobody told me there were programs like this"
- "I didn't know coaches did this specific work"
- "I would have done something if I'd known what was out there"

### Present-tense (less common — surfaces during pitch)

- "I had no idea this kind of thing existed"
- "This is new to me — I didn't know about this category"
- "I've never heard of work like this"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Externalization — placing the responsibility for awareness on others
- Avoiding the work of being resourceful or curious
- Identity as "the one who didn't have access" or "the outsider"
- Genuine niche unawareness (rare in age of social media)
- Conflating "didn't know" with "no information was findable"
- Using lack of awareness as justification for past inaction
- Sometimes: legitimate not-knowing about a specific subcategory
- Passive consumer posture toward opportunity discovery

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — GROUNDED IN TRANSCRIPTS

This excuse appears explicitly in the Itai transcripts. From Itai's Sales Framework full transcript (at 20:19):

> "I didn't know what to do. These programs look like a scam. **I didn't know these opportunities exist.** These are external factors. Now, are they valid? No. Absolutely not."

Itai groups this with other externalization patterns — "I didn't have the time," "the economy is bad" — because the mechanism is the same: the prospect is placing the responsibility for awareness on outside forces.

The key distinction from "I didn't know what to do" (file 16):

- **"I didn't know what to do"** = passive waiting for someone to provide the answer
- **"I didn't know opportunities existed"** = passive waiting for the opportunity to come find them

Both are externalizations of resourcefulness. Both surface the same root pattern: the prospect hasn't been hunting. They've been waiting.

The reframe uses the same arm-around-the-shoulder positioning Tristan establishes in transcript 6 — nice versus kind. The challenge must come from care, not from shame.

## SCRIPT PATH

For I DIDN'T KNOW OPPS, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, P, and the responsibility/resourcefulness question**. Similar architecture to file 16 but the specific reframes differ.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you didn't know opportunities like this existed — what specifically did you not know?"

**Present:** "When you say you didn't know about this — what specifically is new to you?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate what specifically was unknown vs. just vague "didn't know about coaching like this." Specifics often reveal that they actually did know about adjacent things — they just hadn't connected the dots or made the time to look closer.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what you DID know was out there. What had you explored, considered, looked into?"

**Present:** "Walk me through what you've explored so far for this kind of problem."

**What you're listening for:** Most prospects have looked at adjacent options. They knew about therapy, executive coaching, business consulting, mentorship. They just hadn't found the specific category that fit. Surfaces that "I didn't know" is partially true but the framing is misleading.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long have you had this problem? And how long has the opportunity space been available to you?"

**What you're listening for:** Almost always reveals that the opportunity space has been available for years, sometimes decades. The "I didn't know" framing collapses against the timeline. Itai's lexicon line: the problem has existed longer than the prospect's claim of not-knowing can justify.

### THE RESOURCEFULNESS QUESTION (unique to this excuse — deploy directly)

**Either tense:** "If you'd been actively looking for the solution to [their problem] over the last [duration] — how would you have searched? What would you have done to find it?"

**What you're listening for:** Their answer reveals whether they were ever actually looking. Most weren't. They had a problem and waited for the solution to find them rather than hunting for it.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to wait for opportunities to come to you instead of going to find them? Has that been a pattern?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who taught "the right opportunity will come along" patience, an employment background where opportunities were assigned (promotions, projects), or a cultural framing of opportunity as something granted vs. created.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip in most cases.

### C — Compared to what (rarely needed)

Skip in most cases.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Passive opportunity-awareness (most common)

**Most common case.** The prospect has had a problem for years and never actively searched for solutions. They've been waiting for opportunities to surface in their feed, their network, their environment — rather than hunting for them.

**How to recognize:**

- Long-duration problem (multi-year)
- Vague about what they actually did to look for solutions
- References "if I'd known" or "nobody told me about this"
- Hasn't taken active steps to explore the solution space
- Often hasn't even Googled their specific problem in clear terms

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Have you actually Googled '[their specific problem] coaching' over the last few years? What came up?"
- "Have you asked anyone in your network how they've handled this kind of pattern?"
- "Of all the time you've spent on this problem, how much of it was spent actively seeking solutions versus thinking about the problem?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

Open with the nice-versus-kind framing from Tristan:

> "Can I be kind here, not nice? The kind thing is to point out a pattern I'm seeing — but only if I can do it with respect. Cool?"

After they agree:

> "What I'm hearing when you say 'I didn't know opportunities like this existed' is that you weren't actively looking. There's a difference between not knowing because information doesn't exist and not knowing because you weren't searching. We're in 2026 — this kind of work has been written about, talked about, podcast about, ad about for years. The opportunity didn't hide from you. So what's been the actual gap?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Google Reframe (adapted from Tristan)** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you something honestly. If you'd typed '[their specific problem] coaching' into Google any time in the last few years, would you have found this kind of work?"

After they answer (yes):

> "So the opportunity wasn't hidden. It was findable. What got in the way of you finding it?"

Why this lands: Reveals that "didn't know existed" is rarely literal. The information was abundant and findable. The gap wasn't availability — it was active searching. The prospect has to construct that.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Iceberg of Awareness** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine your awareness as an iceberg. Above the surface is what you know. Below is what you don't know that you don't know. Successful people are constantly exploring below the waterline — looking at what they might not be aware of. What have you been doing to explore below YOUR waterline on this specific problem?"

After they answer:

> "If you'd been actively expanding your awareness about how to solve this — would you have stayed in the dark about it for [duration]?"

Why this lands: Reframes opportunity-awareness as something the prospect is responsible for actively expanding. Reveals that they've been passive about discovery.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Successful Person's Habit** — **Grade: A**

> "Think about people you know who have transformed their lives — businesses, health, relationships. How did they find the solutions they used? Did opportunities come to them, or did they actively hunt?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've operated?"

Why this lands: Activates their reference points for transformation. Successful transformers hunt. The prospect has been waiting. The contrast is the lever.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Doctor Hunting Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "If you developed a specific medical condition tomorrow, would you wait for the right specialist to randomly come into your life? Or would you actively search for the best person for that specific condition?"

After they answer:

> "Why is that different from what you've been doing with this?"

Why this lands: Medical framing makes the disparity obvious. Nobody waits for the right doctor to find them. Yet for identity-level problems, people often do exactly that. Reveals the category error.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So 'didn't know opportunities existed' was really 'wasn't actively searching for them.' Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — Genuine specific niche that wasn't visible to them

**Rare but possible.** Some prospects have a specific, narrow problem and the solution category is genuinely niche enough that finding it required specific signals they didn't have. This is real.

**How to recognize:**

- Specific narrow problem
- The solution category is genuinely specialized (not just "coaching")
- They have some plausible reason they wouldn't have come across it
- They did actively look at adjacent options
- Not defensive — genuinely surprised the category exists

**This is closer to acknowledgment than reframe.** Honor the surprise.

**The move:**

> "Fair. Some specific niches are harder to find than others. Now that you know about it — what's drawing you to it? And what would have to be true for you to commit to it?"

Move forward from acknowledgment to commitment evaluation. The reframe isn't needed because the prospect's framing is essentially accurate.

### PATH C — "Didn't know" as cover for not-acting-when-knew

**Common variant.** The prospect actually DID know about this kind of work but didn't pursue it. They're using "didn't know" to cover for "knew but didn't act." More dishonest version of Path A.

**How to recognize:**

- When pressed, they admit they'd heard of similar work
- May reference podcasts, books, or social media that touched on the category
- Specific defensive about whether they "really" knew
- Often shows up after the closer asks about adjacent things they explored

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Had you ever come across this kind of work before — even tangentially? Podcasts, books, social media?"
- "What stopped you from looking deeper when you came across it?"
- "If you'd known then what you're learning now, would you have engaged? What was actually getting in the way?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "There's a difference between 'didn't know' and 'knew but didn't pursue.' Which one is actually true?"

- "If 'didn't know existed' isn't quite accurate — what's the more honest version? What was actually going on when you came across this kind of thing and didn't dig in?"

- "What if it wasn't lack of awareness — what if it was something else that was preventing you from engaging? What would that something else be?"

This is the move that pulls the cover off the cover. Most prospects, once honest, will admit they knew and didn't pursue — and the question becomes what was preventing them. That's where the actual fear surfaces.

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Honesty Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you something carefully. Is it more accurate to say 'I didn't know opportunities like this existed' — or 'I had some awareness but never engaged with it'? Be honest."

After they answer:

> "What was actually stopping you when you had some awareness but didn't dig in?"

Why this lands: Direct, respectful, refuses to accept the partially-true framing. Most prospects will admit when challenged that they had some awareness. The reframe surfaces what was actually blocking them.

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "Okay, so it wasn't really 'didn't know existed' — it was 'knew but didn't engage.' That's a different problem. Setting aside the awareness piece, what else has been in the way?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path:

> "Aside from the 'didn't know existed' framing, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the awareness piece — what else?"
- "Setting aside whether you knew opportunities like this existed — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 I DIDN'T KNOW OPPS phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Flag this as externalization-of-resourcefulness** — the reframe converts passive opportunity-awareness to active hunting
3. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, P, and the resourcefulness question highlighted
4. Deploy nice-versus-kind framing before the challenge
5. Based on response, diagnose path (A passive waiting, B genuine niche, C cover for not-acting)
6. Load matched reframe card
7. Closer deploys reframe with arm-around-shoulder tone
8. Check for concession
9. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm pretty well-read and well-connected, but I genuinely hadn't come across this specific kind of work."
- "I would have engaged if I'd known. I'm not sure how this slipped past my radar."
- "I keep up with the space — this is new to me."

Type A often defends with "I'm well-informed" framing. Usually Path A or C. The Google Reframe cracks Path A; the Honesty Reframe cracks Path C. Be respectful — Type A prospects don't tolerate being told they weren't paying attention.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Nobody in my life talks about this kind of work."
- "I'm not in the circles where you'd hear about coaching like this."
- "If I'd known, I would have done it earlier."

Type B presents more openly as Path A. Often genuine passive waiting. The Successful Person's Habit reframe works well. Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes — CRITICAL

- Deploy nice-versus-kind before the challenge
- Respect their actual position before naming the pattern
- Don't shame the not-knowing — name the passivity with care
- The arm-around-shoulder positioning is non-negotiable for this excuse
- After participatory analogies, let silence sit

### Friction budget notes

I DIDN'T KNOW OPPS often surfaces during or after the pitch when the prospect realizes the category exists. It can also surface in discovery as past-tense justification. Handling is usually efficient — the Google Reframe is fast and effective. Don't pile reframes.

### Special: when the prospect is genuinely awed by the work

Some prospects are honestly surprised this kind of work exists and their "didn't know" is genuine wonder, not externalization. The diagnostic:

- Tone is curious, not defensive
- They're asking questions about the work, not justifying past inaction
- They're moving forward, not retreating

If genuine wonder: don't deploy the reframe. The reframe addresses externalization. Wonder isn't externalization. Just engage with their curiosity and let them ask questions.

### Adjacent: "Where do I even find people like you?"

Variant of I DIDN'T KNOW OPPS framed as a current question. The move:

> "Honest answer — you find people like me by being someone who looks. The fact that you found this call means you've started looking. The question is whether you'll keep looking after this conversation, or whether you'll go back to waiting for the next opportunity to come to you. Which one is the actual pattern you want to build?"

Reveals that finding this conversation was active — and challenges whether they'll continue active engagement.

### Special: when this excuse connects to "burned by past coaches"

Sometimes the "didn't know opportunities like this" is partial — they knew about coaching generally but felt burned by what they tried. The two excuses braid. Route to file 10 (TRIED BEFORE) for the past-pain handling, then come back here for the awareness piece if needed.
