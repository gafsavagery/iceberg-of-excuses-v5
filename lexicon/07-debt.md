## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I had too much debt to take that on"
- "I was paying down credit cards and couldn't add another expense"
- "I was overleveraged at the time"
- "I had a business loan I was servicing"
- "I'd just refinanced and was cash-poor"
- "The mortgage was tight and I couldn't add anything"
- "I was carrying student loans / business debt / IRS debt"
- "I was trying to pay things off, not take on more"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I'm carrying a lot of debt right now"
- "I'd have to put this on a credit card"
- "I don't have liquid cash to invest in this"
- "I'm in debt repayment mode"
- "I can't afford to add another monthly expense"
- "My credit is maxed"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Real financial constraint that limits options (must be honored)
- Debt as identity ("I'm someone with debt") that prevents action even when affordable
- Conflating debt-discomfort with debt-impossibility
- Using debt as cover for fear of additional financial commitment
- "Paying off debt" as a permanent state that never resolves
- Avoiding investments in growth because of past financial pain
- Sometimes: genuine inability to invest without harm

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse requires careful handling because, like death/kids, some of it is genuinely real. People with crushing debt or severe financial pressure shouldn't be stretching for premium coaching investments.

**However**, for high-earning entrepreneur ICP, "debt" often means something different than it does for biz-op prospects:

- A $5M business owner with $200K in business debt is not in financial constraint
- A founder carrying a mortgage and modest credit card balance is not in financial constraint
- A high-income person with significant tax debt may have liquidity issues but not actual income issues

The closer's job is to distinguish:

1. **Severe constraint that genuinely prevents investment** (qualify out)
2. **Moderate debt that creates discomfort but allows investment** (reframe — most common)
3. **Debt as identity/narrative that's outlived the financial reality** (reframe)

## SCRIPT PATH

For DEBT, the most leveraged letters are **S, C, T, and P**. Specifically and Compared-to-what are critical.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you had too much debt — what specifically were you carrying then?"

**Present:** "When you say you're carrying a lot of debt — what specifically does that look like?"

**What you're listening for:**

Their willingness to be specific about the debt structure. Most prospects use "debt" vaguely. Push for numbers:

- Type of debt (mortgage, credit cards, business loan, tax, student)
- Current balances
- Monthly servicing cost
- Income relative to debt

The specifics reveal whether this is real constraint or generalized financial anxiety. A high-income founder with a $300K mortgage and $20K in credit cards is not financially constrained. A $50K-income person with $80K in credit card debt is.

### C — Compared to what (CRITICAL — deploy immediately after S)

**Past:** "Compared to what? Compared to what you make? Compared to what you spent that year on other things?"

**Present:** "Compared to what? Compared to your income? Compared to other investments you make?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether the debt is large relative to income/cash flow or whether "debt" is being used as a feeling rather than a financial fact. Often the prospect's debt is normal for their income bracket and not actually a constraint.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through your finances at that point. What was coming in, what was going out, what was the actual cash flow?"

**Present:** "Walk me through your current financial picture. Income, expenses, debt servicing — where does it actually sit?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate their financial picture in detail. Prospects in genuine constraint can; prospects using debt as cover are often fuzzy about specifics.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long has the debt been part of your story? Did it just happen or has it been ongoing?"

**Present:** "How long have you been in debt-repayment mode? When did that pattern start?"

**What you're listening for:** Chronicity. Some prospects have been "paying off debt" for years without actually reducing the balance. The pattern itself is the problem, not the debt amount.

### R — Roots (deploy when debt-anxiety feels identity-coded)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to be this anxious about debt? Was debt a thing in your family growing up?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces childhood financial anxiety (parents who fought about money, family bankruptcy, religious or cultural conditioning around debt as moral failure). The pattern may be inherited and not appropriate to their current financial reality.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip unless they reference partner's financial views or external advisors.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Debt-as-discomfort that's affordable when examined

**Most common for high-earning ICP.** The prospect has debt but it's not actually preventing investment. They have income, they have cash flow, they have available credit or savings. The "debt" framing is more emotional than financial.

**How to recognize:**

- High income relative to debt
- Debt is normal for their bracket (mortgage, business operations)
- They can articulate the debt but it's not actually crushing
- Cash flow is positive
- They've made discretionary purchases recently that contradict "I can't afford it"
- The debt has been mentioned multiple times but no specific plan to pay it off in a specific timeframe

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "What have you spent on in the last 6 months that wasn't essential? Vacations, equipment, other investments?"
- "If this was a business expense that would 5x your revenue in 12 months, would the debt feel like an obstacle?"
- "What's the difference between debt that's actually constraining and debt that's just feels uncomfortable?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You found money for [X discretionary expense]. So the money isn't actually the constraint — the allocation is. What does it mean that you've allocated to [those things] but not to this?"

- "If past-you had invested in this work two years ago and you'd been making 30% more by now — would the debt be the same problem? Or would the additional income have made the debt smaller?"

- "What's been the cost of NOT investing in growth because of the debt? Has the debt situation gotten better, worse, or about the same?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Bigger Income Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you make this investment, and over the next 18 months it drives your income up 40%. Would the debt that felt heavy at your current income still feel heavy at your future income? Or would it feel much smaller?"

After they answer:

> "What does that tell you about whether debt is the actual constraint?"

Why this lands: Reveals that "debt" is relative to income, and the investment is designed to grow income. The prospect has been comparing the investment to their current income, not their future income. The reframe reveals the framing error.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Asset vs. Liability Reframe** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "In your business, you take on debt to invest in things that grow revenue. Equipment loans, hiring, marketing spend. You don't avoid all debt — you avoid debt that doesn't produce returns. Why have you been treating this differently?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference between debt that produces income and debt that doesn't?"

Why this lands: Activates their CEO understanding of debt. They use debt strategically in business. Holding personal growth to a different standard reveals the inconsistency.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Education Comparison** — **Grade: A**

> "When people take on $200K in student loans for a graduate degree, are they making a bad decision? Why or why not?"

After they answer (depends on whether the degree produces income):

> "How is investing in identity-level work — which directly affects your earning capacity — different from that?"

Why this lands: Frames the investment as an education-equivalent that's actually MORE leveraged (no degree time, no opportunity cost of years out of work). The cultural acceptance of student loan debt highlights the inconsistency of treating coaching debt differently.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So the debt has been the story but not actually the constraint. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from moving on this?"

### PATH B — Debt-as-identity that's outlived the financial reality

**Common.** The prospect WAS in real financial constraint years ago and the identity stuck. They're no longer in constraint but they still talk about themselves as someone with debt issues.

**How to recognize:**

- Past financial trauma (bankruptcy, foreclosure, business failure)
- Current finances have substantially recovered
- Still describes self as "someone with debt"
- Treats every financial decision through the lens of the past constraint
- Hasn't recalibrated the framing as situation improved

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How are your finances now compared to when [past financial event] happened?"
- "If a friend in your current financial situation came to you for advice about investing in their growth, what would you tell them?"
- "Has your money story updated since [past event], or are you still operating from the framework you developed then?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You learned a money story during a hard time. That made sense then. What's been the cost of carrying that story forward into a different financial reality?"

- "What if the version of you that's been carrying the 'I have debt issues' identity is no longer accurate? Who would you be if you let that go?"

- "How would someone in your current actual financial position approach this decision if they weren't carrying the old story?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Outdated Wardrobe** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine you went through a period of weight loss and you're now wearing clothes that are three sizes too big. The clothes fit at some point but they don't fit now. Why haven't you bought new clothes?"

After they answer:

> "Your money story might be the equivalent. It fit a different version of your finances. Why are you still wearing it?"

Why this lands: Visceral and immediate. Reveals that they're operating from an outdated framework that no longer matches their actual situation. Makes the disconnection between past identity and current reality obvious.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Survivor Reframe** — **Grade: B+**

> "Someone who survives a major car accident often becomes a defensive driver. That's smart and adaptive. But if they're still terrified to drive 10 years later when there's been zero issue — what's that? Is that learning, or is that being stuck?"

After they answer:

> "Where are you in that pattern with money?"

Why this lands: Names the difference between adaptive caution and stuck caution. Use carefully — don't pathologize a recent financial trauma.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "So the debt identity is from a different chapter that's no longer the current reality. What else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH C — Genuine financial constraint that should be honored

**Less common for ICP but happens.** The prospect genuinely cannot afford the investment without serious financial strain. The numbers don't work.

**How to recognize:**

- Income is meaningfully below the investment size relative to obligations
- They can articulate specific numbers that don't pencil out
- Adding this expense would cause real strain (missed payments, family stress)
- Not avoiding — being honest about real limits

**This is not a reframe situation. This is a qualification situation.**

**The move:**

> "I appreciate you being honest about that. Let me ask honestly — is it that you can't afford this, or that it would be a stretch but doable? Because those are different conversations."

If genuinely can't afford:

> "Then this isn't the right move right now. Let me ask — what would have to change financially for this to be possible? And what's a realistic timeline for that?"

If they can articulate a timeline (e.g., "in 4-6 months when this contract closes"), the move is:

> "Okay, so the right play is to keep talking but not commit until your financial position supports it. I'll stay in touch. When you're ready, we'll move."

If they can't articulate any path forward financially, qualify out gracefully.

**Transition language for Path C:**

If qualifying out: end the call with respect.

If reframed to Path A (stretch but doable): deploy Path A reframes.

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe:

> "Aside from the debt piece, what else has been getting in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the debt situation — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as not the real constraint — what else?"

For Path C qualify-out: no sweep. End respectfully.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 DEBT phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options with S and C highlighted as primary
3. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path (A discomfort-as-narrative, B outdated identity, C genuine constraint)
4. Load matched reframe card
5. Closer deploys reframe
6. Check for concession
7. Sweep transition or qualify out

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm carrying about $400K in business debt — equipment loans, a working capital line, some SBA stuff. I'm conservative about adding new obligations."
- "I'm in a transition year financially. Cash flow is positive but I'm watching it carefully."
- "I made some aggressive moves last year and I'm consolidating now."

Type A defends with analytical framing. The debt is real but usually proportional to income/business size. Reframe with Asset vs. Liability (Analogy 2) — activates their strategic debt framework.

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm just trying to dig out from under credit card debt."
- "I've been paying it down for years and it never seems to go away."
- "Every time I get ahead, something else comes up."

Type B defends with chronic-debt narrative. Often Path B (debt-as-identity) when the actual numbers are smaller than the emotional weight suggests. Reframe with the Outdated Wardrobe (Analogy 1) — gentle but clear.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't shame the debt or imply they're financially irresponsible
- Treat money with neutral curiosity, not judgment
- Push for specifics without being intrusive
- After participatory analogies, let the realization land before moving on

### Friction budget notes

DEBT can be efficiently handled because the numbers usually reveal the truth quickly. If the prospect has the income and just resists the discretionary allocation, the reframe lands fast. If they have genuine constraint, qualify out fast. The middle case (Path B identity-debt) takes a bit more work but the wardrobe analogy is high-leverage.

### Special: when the prospect mentions specific debt numbers

If the prospect shares specific numbers, do quick math in your head about their actual capacity:

- Mortgage at 30-40% of income: normal
- Business debt servicing under 20% of revenue: normal
- Credit card debt under 10% of income: addressable
- Tax debt: serious but often has payment plan options

If their debt is in normal ranges for their income bracket, the "debt prevents this" framing is almost always Path A. Don't validate the debt as an obstacle if the math says otherwise.

### Special: the "I want to pay off debt first" deflection

> "I want to pay off my debt before I invest in anything else."

This sounds responsible but is often Path B (identity narrative). The move:

> "Honest question — how long have you been paying off debt? And how long would it take at your current pace to be debt-free? Five years? Ten? And during those years, you stay where you are with this problem? Is that the actual plan?"

This surfaces that "pay off debt first" often means "delay growth indefinitely." Sometimes the right move IS to invest in growth WHILE paying down debt because growth produces the income that accelerates debt payoff.
