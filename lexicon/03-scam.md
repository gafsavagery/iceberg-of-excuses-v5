## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I got burned by a coach a few years ago"
- "I bought a course that turned out to be garbage"
- "I've been scammed by this industry before"
- "The last guru I worked with was a fraud"
- "I spent $30K on a mastermind and got nothing"
- "I've seen too many people get taken advantage of"
- "I researched a lot of coaches and most of them are fake"
- "I didn't want to get scammed again"

### Present-tense (active)

- "How do I know this isn't a scam?"
- "There are so many fake coaches out there"
- "I need to be careful — I've seen people get taken advantage of"
- "What makes you different from the gurus on Instagram?"
- "I'm skeptical of this whole industry"
- "How do I know you'll deliver?"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Real prior experience of being burned (legitimate, requires careful handling)
- Fear of being judged for "falling for it" (the social cost of being wrong)
- Using skepticism as armor against the harder decision
- Generalized industry skepticism that's never been tested
- Identity as "the smart one who can't be fooled"
- Avoiding the vulnerability of trusting someone
- Confusing skepticism with discernment
- Sometimes: genuine due-diligence concern with no underlying fear

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse is uniquely tricky because the prospect's stated concern is partially legitimate. The coaching industry IS full of scams and fake gurus. Dismissing the concern outright loses trust immediately.

The closer's job is to:

1. Validate that the concern is reasonable
2. Differentiate between protective skepticism (useful) and avoidant skepticism (a defense)
3. Make the prospect surface what would actually constitute proof for them
4. Either provide that proof or make them realize no proof would satisfy them — which means it's not really about proof

**Never get defensive or list credentials as a counter-argument.** That activates their pattern-matching for "salespeople who name-drop." The move is curiosity and confidence, not defense.

## SCRIPT PATH

For SCAM/SKEPTICISM, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, and P**. Specifically and Tell-me-the-story are critical openers.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you got burned — what specifically happened? What was the actual experience?"

**Present:** "When you say you're worried about scams — what specifically would make this a scam in your eyes?"

**What you're listening for:**

For past-tense: The actual story. Was it a real scam (false promises, no delivery, fraud) or was it a program they didn't apply themselves to? Many "burned" stories are actually "I didn't do the work" stories that got narrated as "the program was bad."

For present-tense: What "scam" actually means to them. Is it about outcomes? About integrity? About price? About delivery? Each requires a different reframe.

### T — Tell me the story (deploy immediately after S)

**Past:** "Walk me through what happened with that coach. What did they promise, what did they deliver, what did you do, what was the gap?"

**Present:** "Walk me through how you'd evaluate whether someone is legit. What would you be looking for?"

**What you're listening for:**

For past-tense: The full sequence. The promise, their behavior, the delivery, the outcome. Often reveals the prospect's own role in the failure (didn't show up, didn't implement, expected magic).

For present-tense: Their actual evaluation criteria. If they can name them, you can address them. If they can't, the skepticism isn't really about evaluation — it's about avoidance.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long ago was that? And what have you tried in this space since then?"

**What you're listening for:** If it was years ago and they haven't tried anything since, the "burned" experience has become an identity rather than a data point. They're using it as permanent permission not to invest.

### I — Involved (deploy when they reference vague industry)

**Either tense:** "When you say 'this industry,' who specifically are you talking about? Are there specific people or programs that shaped this view?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have specific data or are operating on cultural narrative. Often it's the latter — they've absorbed "coaches are scammers" from social media without specific experience.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to be this skeptical of investing in yourself? Has that pattern shown up other places?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who was skeptical of "self-help stuff," a religious or cultural background that distrusts personal development, or an early experience of being mocked for vulnerability around growth.

### C — Compared to what (less common, but useful when prospect compares offers)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what other coaches or programs you've looked at — what's making this feel different or similar?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they've done actual comparison shopping or whether they're using "I haven't researched enough" as a stall.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Genuine prior bad experience that's become identity

**Most common case.** The prospect had a real bad experience (course that didn't deliver, coach who underperformed, mastermind that was hollow). The experience was real but it's been generalized into "the whole industry is fake."

**How to recognize:**

- Can name the specific bad experience
- The story is well-rehearsed (they've told it before)
- They generalize from one experience to all coaching
- Some defensiveness when you probe the details
- Often: their own role in the failure isn't named

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "What did you do with the program? How fully did you implement what they taught?"
- "Looking back, was the program itself the problem, or was something else going on?"
- "What did you take away from that experience — even if it was painful?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If you'd had the same experience with a restaurant — bad meal, overpriced, mediocre service — would you have stopped going to all restaurants forever? Or would you have learned what to look for next time?"

- "Looking back at that experience, what was actually true? Was the coach genuinely a scammer, or did they promise X and deliver X, and you needed Y? Or did you check out partway through?"

- "What would have to be different for you to trust an investment like this again? What would you need to see?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Restaurant Comparison** — **Grade: A**

> "You've had a bad meal at a restaurant before, right? Bad service, overpriced, the food was wrong. Did you swear off all restaurants forever, or did you learn what to look for and get better at picking them?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've responded to the bad coaching experience?"

Why this lands: The restaurant comparison is universally relatable and exposes the over-generalization. They can't honestly say they'd avoid all restaurants. The pattern of generalizing from one bad experience to an entire category becomes visible.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Bad Hire Reframe** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "You've made a bad hire before, right? Someone who looked great on paper, sold themselves well, and turned out not to deliver? Did you stop hiring people, or did you get better at evaluating?"

After they answer:

> "Same question applies here. Did you swear off coaching, or get better at picking coaches?"

Why this lands: Activates their CEO identity. They've all made hiring mistakes. They didn't quit hiring — they refined their process. The skepticism-as-permanent-armor pattern looks obviously wrong when reframed through hiring.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Burned Investor** — **Grade: B+**

> "You've made a bad investment before — a stock that crashed, a deal that went south. Did you stop investing entirely? Or did you learn what to look for and become a better investor?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've treated this?"

Why this lands: Financial analogy works for analytical prospects. Less emotional than the restaurant or hiring versions. Use when the prospect frames everything in financial terms.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So one bad experience doesn't mean the whole category is bad — it means you got better at evaluating. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from moving forward?"

### PATH B — Generalized industry skepticism with no specific experience

**Common case.** The prospect has absorbed "coaches are scammers" from social media, cultural narrative, or watching others. They have no specific bad experience but they've adopted the skepticism as protection.

**How to recognize:**

- Can't name specific bad experience
- References vague "I've seen people..." or "everyone knows..."
- Treats skepticism as sophistication
- May reference influencer takes or social media patterns
- Skepticism feels rehearsed but is actually shallow

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Have you actually invested in coaching before? What was that experience like?"
- "Where did you form this view of the industry? Specific people or just a general sense?"
- "If you set aside what you've heard about coaching and just looked at what we've actually discussed today — what do you think?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If you're being honest, is your skepticism based on actual experience, or have you adopted it as a way to protect yourself?"

- "What's it cost you to be skeptical of investing in yourself? Where would you be if you'd invested in growth five years ago without the skepticism?"

- "Is there a difference between healthy skepticism — which evaluates each opportunity on its merits — and blanket skepticism that protects you from making a decision either way?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Doctor Comparison** — **Grade: A**

> "There are bad doctors. There are doctors who misdiagnose, who push unnecessary procedures, who don't listen. Does that mean you avoid all medical care? Or do you find a doctor you trust and let them help you?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference between how you've approached this versus how you'd approach picking a doctor?"

Why this lands: Healthcare is something they've engaged with. The analogy exposes that they don't actually treat coaching with the same rationality as medical care. They've categorized it differently — as "self-help stuff that's all fake" — when it should be categorized as "professional service with quality variance."

**ANALOGY 2 — The Mentor Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Every successful person you respect — Branson, Bezos, the people you look up to — they all had mentors and coaches. They invested in growth from people who'd been where they wanted to go. If coaching was all a scam, how did they get there?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference between what they did and what you'd be doing?"

Why this lands: Activates their reference points for success. They respect mentorship in principle. Reveals that "all coaching is a scam" is incompatible with how successful people they admire actually got successful.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "So the skepticism is more of a generalized stance than something based on your actual experience. That's worth noticing. Aside from the industry concern, what else has been stopping you?"

### PATH C — Genuine due diligence concern (no fear, just thoroughness)

**Less common but real.** Some prospects are genuinely doing rational due diligence. No fear underneath, just appropriate professionalism about a significant investment.

**How to recognize:**

- They ask specific, professional questions
- They reference comparing multiple offers honestly
- They want to see specifics — methodology, track record, case studies
- No emotional charge to the questions
- They're treating this like a business decision, which it is

**This is not a reframe situation — this is a qualification situation.** The closer's job is to provide the proof, not to reframe the concern.

**Provide:**

- Specific methodology (the 90-day intensive structure)
- The mechanism (clinical training, identity-level work, specific framework)
- Case studies with specifics (not testimonials — concrete outcomes)
- The conditional certainty: "Here's what I can guarantee, here's what I can't, here's what you need to do for it to work"

**The move:**

> "Fair questions. Let me give you what I can. Here's the methodology, here's how it works, here's what's happened with people in similar situations, and here's what's required from you. After that, you can make a clean decision."

After providing: 

> "What else would you need to see to make an informed decision?"

**Transition language for Path C:**

> "Okay, so you've got what you need on the due diligence side. Aside from the evaluation question, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or B reframe is concluded:

> "Aside from the scam concern, what else has been getting in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the industry skepticism — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as not the real issue — what else?"

For Path C, after due diligence is satisfied:

> "Aside from the evaluation question, what else is in the way?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 SCAM phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as the active reframe context
2. Present the SCRIPT options card with S and T highlighted as primary
3. After SCRIPT deployed, the sim's response should determine which path
4. Present the path diagnosis card with criteria for each
5. Load matched reframe card
6. Closer deploys reframe
7. Check for concession
8. Sweep transition or loop

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses to scam concerns sound like:

- "I've worked with two coaches before. The first was excellent — strategic, hands-on, real ROI. The second was a complete waste of $40K. I learned from that one."
- "I evaluate every major investment with rigor. I want to understand the methodology and the track record."
- "I'm not opposed to coaching — I just don't tolerate vague offerings."

Type A defends with analytical framing. The "scam" concern is wrapped in due-diligence language. Often Path C, sometimes Path A. Reframe by providing rigor and specificity (Analogy 2, the bad hire reframe).

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Honestly, the whole industry feels shady to me. Everyone on Instagram claiming to be a coach."
- "I've seen people get taken advantage of."
- "I don't want to be that person who spends a bunch of money and gets nothing."

Type B defends with generalized cynicism. Usually Path B, sometimes Path A with vague specifics. Reframe with the doctor or mentor analogy and validate the concern before reframing.

### Voice tone notes

- Never get defensive
- Don't list credentials as a counter-argument
- Curiosity, not pitch
- Use questions, not declarations
- After deploying participatory analogies, let silence sit until they construct the answer

### Friction budget notes

SCAM concern usually surfaces midway through the conversation when the prospect is starting to feel real interest. It's a defense against the imminent decision. Handle it cleanly but don't spend too much budget — the underlying fear (being seen as gullible, being wrong, being taken advantage of) is what matters and surfaces in the fear extraction phase.

### Special: when the prospect tries to "verify your credentials"

> "Show me proof you're not a scam."

This often disguises a deeper fear. The move:

> "Fair. I can give you specifics on methodology, on my training, on what's happened with others. Before I do — what would actually constitute proof for you? Because I want to make sure whatever I share addresses what you're actually looking for. What's the test?"

If they can name a specific test, you can address it. If they can't, they're using "show me proof" as a stall. Surface that gently:

> "You're asking for proof but you don't quite know what proof would look like. That's information. It usually means the question isn't really about evidence — it's about something else. What's actually going on?"

### Adjacent: "How is this different from other coaches?"

Variant of SCAM. The reframe is similar but the answer is more about positioning than skepticism handling. The move:

> "What have you seen from other coaches that's made you want something different? What's been missing?"

Then position your offer against the specific gaps they name. Don't pitch differentiators generically — pitch them against the specific dissatisfaction they articulate.
