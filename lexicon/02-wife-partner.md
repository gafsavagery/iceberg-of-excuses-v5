## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "My wife wasn't on board"
- "My partner thought it was a bad idea"
- "She wasn't supportive of the investment"
- "He thought I was throwing money away"
- "We weren't aligned on it"
- "She had concerns and I didn't push it"
- "I would've done it but my partner wasn't ready"
- "I needed to wait until things were better at home"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I'd have to run this by my wife"
- "My partner would never go for this"
- "I need to talk to her first"
- "She'd kill me if I spent this without consulting her"
- "He has the final say on big financial decisions"
- "We make these decisions together"
- "I have to check with my partner before I commit to anything"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Using partner as cover for their own fear/hesitation
- Genuine lack of communication about the prospect's real desires
- Codependent decision-making patterns (no autonomy for either)
- Partner is actually genuinely against, but reasons haven't been examined
- Prospect hasn't actually had a real conversation with partner
- Prospect is projecting their own doubts onto partner ("she wouldn't approve" = "I don't approve")
- Power dynamic where one partner controls all financial decisions
- Prospect using partner to avoid making their own decision

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse has a unique trap. The closer must not:

1. Disparage the partner ("sounds like she doesn't support you")
2. Set the prospect against the partner ("you're a grown adult, make your own decision")
3. Coach around the partner ("don't tell her, just do it")

Any of these moves loses the sale AND damages a real relationship. The closer's job is to surface what's actually going on without making the partner an enemy.

The truth almost always is: the partner is being used as a proxy for the prospect's own fear OR the prospect hasn't actually had a real conversation about it. Both are surfaceable without partner-bashing.

## SCRIPT PATH

For WIFE/PARTNER, the most leveraged letters are **S, I, T, and P**. Specifically and Involved are the openers.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say your partner wasn't on board — what specifically was she not on board with?"

**Present:** "When you say she'd never go for it — what specifically do you think she'd object to?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether the prospect can actually name the partner's stated objections, or whether they're projecting. Most prospects can't articulate the partner's actual position with precision, which is the tell.

### I — Involved (deploy second, immediately after S)

**Either tense:** "Tell me about her. Who is she? What does she do? How long have you been together?"

**What you're listening for:** Context about the relationship. Is this a long marriage with established patterns? A newer relationship? Different income sources? Who handles money? Who makes big decisions? Is there a history of one partner deciding for both?

This isn't small talk. This is diagnostic. The relationship structure determines which path the reframe takes.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through the actual conversation you had with her. What did you say, what did she say?"

**Present:** "Walk me through how you'd actually have this conversation. What would you say? What do you think she'd say back?"

**What you're listening for:** Did the conversation actually happen? Most prospects who say "my partner wasn't on board" have not had the full conversation. They've had a brief mention or implied it sideways. The full conversation never happened because they never fully advocated for it.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long has it been since you and your partner have had a real conversation about you actually growing or changing in this way?"

**What you're listening for:** Often the answer is "years" or "we've never really talked about it like that." Reveals the relationship pattern, not just this decision.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn that big decisions require both partners' full approval before either of you can move? Whose voice was that originally?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces childhood patterns — parents who fought about money, parents where one partner made all decisions, religious or cultural patterns about marital decision-making.

### C — Compared to what (use selectively)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what other big decisions in your relationship — how does this one rank? Have you both supported each other through investments before?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether this is a uniquely high-stakes situation or whether the prospect treats every decision as needing total alignment, which is the actual pattern.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Prospect is using partner as proxy for OWN fear

**Most common case.** The prospect has internal doubts but they're harder to articulate than "my partner wouldn't approve." So they outsource the objection to the partner.

**How to recognize:**

- Can't articulate partner's specific objections clearly
- Their language about partner is vague ("she'd just have concerns")
- When pressed for the actual conversation, they admit it hasn't fully happened
- The objections they attribute to partner happen to match their own hesitations
- Tone is defensive rather than collaborative when talking about partner

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "If your partner was 100% supportive right now — what would YOU think about this?"
- "Is it that she wouldn't be on board, or that you're not sure you want to advocate for it?"
- "When you imagine her objections — are they actually hers, or are they the things YOU'RE not sure about?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Let me ask this honestly. If your partner wasn't a factor at all — if you were single, or making this decision independently — would you do this?"

- "When you've made decisions in your marriage that were really about your own growth — how have you usually approached those conversations? What's been different about this one?"

- "If she came to you and said 'I want to invest in something significant for myself, but I'm not sure how you'll feel about it' — what would you want her to do? Would you want her to skip the investment, or would you want her to come to you, advocate clearly for it, and let you weigh in for real?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Reversal Question** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine your partner came to you and said 'I've found something I want to invest $46K in for my own growth — it would mean weekly time commitments and a 90-day intensive. I haven't really talked to you about it because I'm not sure how you'd feel.' How would you want her to handle that?"

After they answer (they'll say something like "I'd want her to actually talk to me about it"):

> "So what does it look like to give your partner the respect of an actual conversation, instead of pre-deciding for her?"

Why this lands: It flips the dynamic. Most prospects realize they're not actually consulting their partner — they're pre-deciding the partner's objection and using it as their own protection. The reversal makes the inconsistency obvious.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Business Decision Reframe** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "You're running a business. If your COO came to you and said 'I think we should make this strategic investment, but I'm not going to push for it because I'm worried about how some of the team will react' — what would you tell them?"

After they answer (they'll say "advocate for it clearly, let me decide"):

> "What's different about how you're treating your own decision here?"

Why this lands: Activates their leadership identity. They expect their team to advocate clearly for ideas they believe in. Holding themselves to a different standard with their partner reveals the avoidance.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So this isn't really about your partner — it's about whether you're willing to fully advocate for what you actually want. We'll keep that in mind. Aside from the partner thing, what else has been keeping you from moving on this?"

### PATH B — Partner has genuine, articulated objections

**Less common but real.** Some prospects can clearly articulate their partner's actual stated objections, and the partner is genuinely against. This is a real relationship dynamic, not avoidance.

**How to recognize:**

- They can quote the partner's actual words
- They've had at least one real conversation about it
- The objections are specific (not vague concerns)
- The prospect's tone is more collaborative ("we disagree on this")
- They're not hiding behind the partner — they're navigating a real disagreement

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "What's HER actual concern? Not the surface version — what's underneath it?"
- "Has she ever supported you in something she had initial concerns about? What did that look like?"
- "What would have to be true for her to feel okay about this?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If your partner could see, six months from now, the exact result this work created for you — for the marriage, for the business, for your stress levels — would she still object?"

- "What's the actual cost to your marriage of you staying where you are? Not the cost of investing — the cost of you continuing to be the version of yourself that's been struggling with this?"

- "When you bring this to her, what's the case you're going to make? Not 'I want to do this coaching thing' — what's the real case?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Marriage Investment Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you this. If you stayed exactly where you are for the next 5 years — the same stress level, the same not-quite-getting-there, the same patterns at home — what does your marriage look like in 5 years?"

After they answer (usually some version of "worse" or "we wouldn't make it"):

> "And if you did this work and became the version of yourself you've described — what does the marriage look like then? Which version is your partner actually going to be in for the long haul with?"

Why this lands: Reframes the investment as FOR the marriage, not against it. The cost of not doing the work is the marriage. Partners who object to investment often haven't been shown that the alternative is the slow erosion they've been experiencing.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Joint Decision Process** — **Grade: A**

> "If your partner was looking at something for her own growth and you had concerns, what would actually move you from 'I have concerns' to 'I support this'? What would she need to bring to that conversation?"

After they answer:

> "What if you brought that exact thing to her about this?"

Why this lands: It treats the partner as a partner, not an obstacle. It gives the prospect a constructive framework for the conversation instead of dreading it.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "Okay, so there's a real conversation that needs to happen with her — not a pre-rejected one. Let's flag that as something you'll need to navigate. Aside from your partner's stated concerns, what else has been keeping you from moving on this?"

### PATH C — The relationship structure itself is the actual problem

**Surfaces less often but critical when it does.** The prospect reveals a relationship pattern where they have no autonomy for major decisions, or where the partner controls all finances, or where every decision requires complete alignment.

**How to recognize:**

- "She controls all the money decisions"
- "I don't make any financial decisions without her approval"
- "We've always done it this way"
- "She has to sign off on everything"
- The prospect sounds resigned, not collaborative
- The pattern suggests their adult autonomy has been ceded

**This is delicate. Do not move to attack the relationship structure.** This is the prospect's actual life. The reframe surfaces the pattern without pathologizing it.

**Follow-up questions:**

- "How long has it been that way?"
- "When was the last time you made a significant decision about your own growth without needing approval?"
- "How does that feel?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If this pattern of needing approval for every decision continues for the next 20 years — what does that look like for you, for her, and for the marriage?"

- "What would it look like to be an autonomous adult who still chooses to share life with someone, rather than someone who needs permission?"

- "Is it possible that the work this offer would do — the identity-level shift — is exactly the work that would make you a better partner, not a worse one?"

**Note for Path C:** This is heavy material and probably can't be fully resolved on a sales call. The honest move is often to flag it as part of the identity work itself.

> "Okay. So the bigger pattern here is that you've been operating without your own decision-making authority for a long time. That's actually one of the patterns this work addresses directly. Let's hold that and come back to it. Aside from your partner, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

**Transition language after Path C surface:**

> "What you just described — needing approval for your own growth — that's something we'd actually work on. We don't have to solve it today. Aside from that pattern, what else has been in the way?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path is concluded:

> "Aside from your partner, what else has been getting in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond the partner conversation — what else?"
- "Setting the relationship piece aside for now — what else has been stopping you?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 WIFE/PARTNER phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as the active reframe context
2. Present the SCRIPT options card with S, I, T highlighted as primary
3. After SCRIPT deployed, present the path diagnosis card with Path A, B, or C criteria
4. Load matched reframe card
5. Closer deploys reframe
6. Check for concession or resistance
7. Sweep transition or loop on additional reframe

### Type A prospect variation (high-performing operator)

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "My wife is a former lawyer. She's analytical, she looks at every major investment we make, and she'd want to understand the ROI and the time commitment in detail."
- "We have a shared decision framework for anything over $25K. It's not that she controls me — we just have a system."
- "She'd want to see the data."

Type A defends with structure. The partner is framed as analytical and reasonable. Reframe by activating their leadership identity (Analogy 2 for Path A) and the long-term marriage cost (Analogy 1 for Path B).

### Type B prospect variation (stuck/stalled)

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "She'd just freak out about the money."
- "She doesn't really get what I do."
- "We fight about money a lot."
- "I'd have to convince her and I just don't have the energy."

Type B defends with emotion and resignation. The partner is framed as obstacle or source of conflict. Reframe by validating the difficulty first, then redirecting (warmth-first approach, then Path A reversal question).

### Voice tone notes

- Never disparage the partner or align with prospect against partner
- Use partner-respecting language: "your partner" or "she" with neutral tone
- The reframe should make the prospect want to advocate for the work, not work around partner
- After deploying participatory analogies, hold silence long enough for prospect to actually construct their answer

### Friction budget notes

WIFE/PARTNER is usually one of the easier-to-surface excuses because it sounds reasonable. But the underlying patterns (Path A and Path C especially) connect to deep identity work. Don't try to resolve everything here. The job is to surface that "partner objections" isn't the real reason and lateral sweep.

### Special handling: female prospects with male partners

The dynamic flips. Female prospects in your ICP may say:

- "My husband doesn't really understand what I do"
- "He'd think I'm being self-indulgent"
- "He's the breadwinner so he has the final say"

The reframe paths are similar but the analogies should be re-genderd. The "imagine YOUR partner brought this to you" reversal works the same way.

### Pre-empting the "let me talk to her and get back to you" move

If the prospect tries to defer the close to "let me talk to my partner first," that's actually a buying signal disguised as a stall. The honest move is:

> "Of course. Two questions before you do. One: are you fully sold on this, or are you using her as cover for your own uncertainty? Two: if she's supportive, are you ready to start? Because if I let you off the call without those answers, you'll have a soft conversation with her instead of advocating for what you want, and we'll end up nowhere. So let's get clear first."

This isn't pushing past the conversation with the partner — it's making sure the prospect has done their own work before having that conversation. If the prospect is fully sold internally, the partner conversation goes differently than if they're hiding behind it.
