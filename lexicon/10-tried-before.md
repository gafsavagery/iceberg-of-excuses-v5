## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I tried something like this before and it didn't work"
- "I've done coaching / mentorship / mastermind before — got nothing out of it"
- "I bought programs in the past and they didn't deliver"
- "I worked with a coach for six months and it was a waste"
- "I've been here before"
- "I've tried everything and nothing's worked"
- "I burned a lot of money on this kind of thing"
- "I'm gun-shy from past attempts"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I've tried this and I'm skeptical it'll be different"
- "I've been burned by coaches before"
- "How is this different from the last thing I tried?"
- "I've already done the work and I'm still stuck — why would this be different?"
- "Nothing has worked for me so far"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Real prior attempt(s) that didn't produce results (must be honored)
- Failed attempts where the prospect didn't actually do the work (most common)
- Failed attempts under wrong conditions (no fit, wrong methodology, wrong timing)
- Using prior failures as permanent permission not to try again
- Identity as "the person who's tried everything"
- Treating one or two failures as exhaustive evidence
- Avoiding the vulnerability of trying again
- Sometimes: genuinely smart pattern-recognition about what hasn't worked

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse is adjacent to SCAM but mechanically different. SCAM is "I'm afraid of being taken advantage of." TRIED BEFORE is "I've been here, it didn't work, why should this time be different."

Most prospects who say "I've tried this before" have one of three things going on:

1. **They tried something superficially similar but mechanically different.** Bought a course (passive consumption) and now equate that with coaching (active relationship). Different categories of intervention.

2. **They tried the right kind of thing but didn't do the work.** Hired a coach, didn't implement, blamed the coach. The pattern was the prospect's, not the coach's.

3. **They tried legitimate things that genuinely weren't a fit.** Wrong methodology for their problem, wrong stage of their journey, wrong personality match.

The reframe depends on diagnosing which.

**Critical move:** Don't argue that the prior attempt wasn't legitimate. That makes the prospect defensive. Instead, examine WHAT was tried and WHAT the prospect did with it. Usually the diagnosis emerges from the examination.

## SCRIPT PATH

For TRIED BEFORE, the most leveraged letters are **T, S, and P**. Tell-me-the-story is the critical opener here.

### T — Tell me the story (DEPLOY FIRST for this excuse — not S)

**Past:** "Tell me about what you tried. What was it specifically — coach, program, course, mastermind? What was the actual experience?"

**Present:** "Tell me about what you've tried. The specific things — what were they, what happened?"

**What you're listening for:**

The actual experience. Listen for:

- Category of intervention (passive content vs. active coaching vs. peer group vs. self-study)
- Duration of engagement
- What was promised vs. what was delivered
- What the prospect actually did with it
- Why they think it didn't work

Most prospects will reveal the diagnosis themselves in the telling. Listen for "I bought it but never really finished it" or "I think it was just a fit issue" or "honestly looking back I wasn't ready" — these are gold.

### S — Specifically (deploy after T)

**Past:** "What specifically about it didn't work? Was it the methodology? The relationship? The format? Your own readiness?"

**Present:** "When you say it didn't work — what specifically didn't happen?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate WHAT failed. Often they can't get more specific than "it just didn't work" — which usually means the prospect doesn't actually know what went wrong, which means they couldn't have known what to do differently.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long ago was that? And what have you tried since?"

**Present:** "When did the past attempts happen? Recent or years ago?"

**What you're listening for:** If the failed attempt was years ago and they've done nothing since, they've turned one or two data points into permanent permission. The duration of inaction since the failure is the lever.

### I — Involved (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Who was the coach / program? What were their credentials and methodology?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether the prior attempt was legitimate work or low-quality. Many "I tried coaching before" stories involve programs that were genuinely poor quality (Instagram coaches, vague mindset stuff, no real methodology). Distinguishing legitimate failed attempts from low-quality ones matters.

### R — Roots (rarely needed here)

Skip unless the gun-shy pattern is clearly inherited from a parent who got burned and warned them off similar investments.

### C — Compared to what (occasionally useful)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what we've discussed today — methodology, format, the specific work — how similar or different does this feel from what you tried?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate the differences (suggests they see this as legitimately different) or whether they're lumping all coaching into one category (suggests overgeneralization).

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Tried something superficially similar but mechanically different

**Most common case.** The prospect bought a course, joined a free mastermind, did a weekend workshop — and is now treating that as equivalent to identity-level 1:1 coaching. The categories are different.

**How to recognize:**

- The prior attempt was passive (course, book, podcast) not active (coaching, mentorship)
- Or it was a group format with no individual attention
- Or it was short-duration (weekend workshop, 1-month program)
- They consumed content but had no real accountability
- They didn't have a relationship with a specific coach

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "Was it actual coaching — meaning weekly 1:1 sessions with a specific person who knew you — or was it more like content consumption?"
- "Did anyone actually see you, week to week, and push back when you weren't doing the work?"
- "How is what you tried mechanically similar or different from what we'd be doing?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You're a CEO. If I told you 'I tried hiring before — bought a job board subscription, posted a listing, didn't get anyone good, hiring doesn't work for me' — what would you say to me?"

- "What's the difference between consuming content about a thing and actually doing the thing with someone who's accountable for your outcome?"

- "Is it possible that you haven't actually tried this — you've tried adjacent things? And the thing itself is still untested?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Gym vs. Trainer Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine someone says 'I tried getting in shape — bought a gym membership for a year, didn't go much, didn't lose weight, so I know getting in shape doesn't work for me.' What's missing from that diagnosis?"

After they answer (they'd need a trainer, accountability, a program):

> "How is what you tried in the past more like the gym membership or more like the trainer?"

Why this lands: Universally relatable, immediately reveals the category error. The prospect almost always realizes their "I tried this" wasn't actually the equivalent of what's on offer now.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Self-Surgery Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "If you'd needed a back surgery and tried watching YouTube videos and doing stretches alone for six months — and then said 'I tried fixing my back, it didn't work, back surgery is BS' — what would the actual problem be?"

After they answer:

> "What's the equivalent of self-surgery versus actual surgery in what you tried?"

Why this lands: Makes the case that some interventions fundamentally require the right format. Self-help content is to coaching what YouTube videos are to surgery — adjacent but not equivalent.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Diet Books Stack** — **Grade: A**

> "If you have 10 unread diet books on your shelf and you're 30 pounds overweight, what's actually true? That diets don't work? Or that having information isn't the same as having a structured intervention with accountability?"

After they answer:

> "How does that map to what you've tried?"

Why this lands: Visceral and concrete. Reveals that information accumulation is not the same as transformation. Most prospects have an equivalent stack of unread or half-completed content.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So what you tried wasn't actually this — it was adjacent. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from real engagement?"

### PATH B — Tried the right thing but didn't do the work

**Common.** The prospect actually engaged real coaching but didn't implement, didn't show up consistently, didn't apply what was discussed. The prospect's pattern was the variable, not the coach's quality.

**How to recognize:**

- Was actual 1:1 coaching or substantive program
- Length of engagement was reasonable (3+ months)
- Coach had credentials
- But: prospect skipped sessions, didn't do exercises between sessions, didn't apply insights
- Often: "I just wasn't ready" or "I think I was sabotaging it"

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How fully did you show up? Did you do the work between sessions?"
- "Looking back honestly — was the program the problem, or was something else going on?"
- "What did the coach actually offer that you didn't take?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "If you were the coach in that situation — working with someone who was you back then — what would you have wanted from your client?"

- "What was going on with you that made you not do the work? What were you protecting yourself from?"

- "The reason the past coaching didn't 'work' — is it possible the work didn't happen because you didn't do it? Not as a judgment — as a diagnosis."

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Trainer Who Lifts For You** — **Grade: A**

> "If you'd hired the best trainer in the world, paid them $50K, and you showed up to half the sessions and didn't lift any weights between sessions — would the trainer be the problem, or would you?"

After they answer:

> "What's the equivalent of that in what happened with the coaching?"

Why this lands: Removes the option of blaming the coach without examining the prospect's own engagement. Most prospects will see the pattern immediately. Doesn't make them wrong — names the actual variable.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Honest Audit** — **Grade: A**

> "Let's do an honest audit. If we looked at the coaching engagement you did before — what percentage of the work was on the coach to deliver, and what percentage was on you to show up and apply? And which percentage was actually missing?"

After they answer:

> "What does that tell you about what 'didn't work'?"

Why this lands: Makes the math visible. Even excellent coaching requires the client to do their part. The honest audit usually reveals the gap was on the prospect's side, which they can fix THIS time.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Readiness Question** — **Grade: A**

> "What was true about you when you tried that coaching that's different about you now? What's changed in your readiness?"

After they answer:

> "What if the reason it didn't work then was that you weren't ready then — and the reason this is the right move now is that you're at a different stage?"

Why this lands: Honors the past attempt as appropriate-for-that-stage while opening up that current-self is different. Reframes the past failure as not-yet-ready rather than incapable.

**Transition language after Path B reframe:**

> "Okay. So the prior coaching didn't fail you — you didn't fully engage with it. That's worth knowing, because that's the variable you control this time. Setting that aside, what else has been getting in the way?"

### PATH C — Tried legitimate things that genuinely weren't a fit

**Less common but real.** The prospect did real coaching with a real coach, did the work, and it genuinely wasn't a fit — wrong methodology, wrong personality, wrong stage of their journey. Not their fault, not the coach's fault, just genuinely not the right pairing.

**How to recognize:**

- Was actual high-quality coaching
- Prospect did show up and engage
- Can articulate specific reasons it didn't fit (methodology, personality, focus area)
- Has done real reflection on why it didn't work
- Not defensive when discussing it — just honest

**Follow-up questions:**

- "What specifically wasn't a fit? Methodology? Style? Focus area?"
- "Knowing what you know now, what would have been a better fit?"
- "What you needed then — versus what you need now — is it the same, or has it evolved?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Based on what didn't work last time, what would you need to see in this engagement to know it's a better fit?"

- "How is what we're discussing actually different from what didn't work for you? Be specific."

- "What evidence would you need that this is the right fit before you'd commit?"

**Note:** This is closer to qualification than reframe. The prospect is being rational. Give them what they need to evaluate.

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The First Marriage Reframe** — **Grade: B+**

> "Some people get married, divorce, and decide they're just not made for marriage. Others get married, divorce, learn what didn't work, and find a better match next time. Which version are you?"

After they answer:

> "What did the first 'marriage' with the past coaching teach you about what would be a better fit?"

Why this lands: Reframes past failure as informative rather than disqualifying. Use carefully — don't use if prospect has actual relationship trauma.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Specific Specialist** — **Grade: A**

> "If you had a complex shoulder injury and saw a general practitioner who didn't fix it, you wouldn't conclude 'medicine doesn't work for me.' You'd find a shoulder specialist. What's the equivalent here? What specific specialist do you actually need?"

After they answer:

> "And how does what we've been discussing match or not match that?"

Why this lands: Activates rational fit-evaluation. Premium prospects respect the precision of finding the right specialist. Positions your offer to be evaluated on fit, not on generic "is coaching good."

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "Okay. So past attempts weren't a fit, and you're being thoughtful about what would be. Aside from that evaluation question, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path:

> "Aside from past attempts, what else has been in the way?"

Variants:

- "Beyond what didn't work before — what else?"
- "Setting aside the prior coaching question — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 TRIED BEFORE phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options with T highlighted as primary (deploys first for this excuse)
3. After T, sim's response reveals which path
4. Diagnose path A, B, or C
5. Load matched reframe card
6. Closer deploys reframe
7. Check for concession
8. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I worked with an executive coach for nine months in 2023. Solid credentials, good methodology. Didn't move the needle on what I needed."
- "I've been through three masterminds. Each was decent but none produced the specific shift I was looking for."

Type A is often Path C (genuine fit issue) or Path B (didn't engage fully). The honest audit and specific specialist analogies work well. Treat them as a thoughtful evaluator.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I've tried so many things. Books, courses, programs. Nothing sticks."
- "I always start strong and then fade out."
- "I'm convinced I'm just broken or something."

Type B is often Path A (tried adjacent things) or Path B (didn't engage). The gym vs trainer reframe and the diet books stack work well. Lead with warmth — Type B prospects often carry shame about prior incompletes.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't argue that the prior attempt was bad
- Curiosity about what specifically happened
- For Path B, the move requires honesty without shame — name the gap without making them wrong for it
- After participatory analogies, hold silence long enough for the realization

### Friction budget notes

TRIED BEFORE often appears mid-call after rapport is established. The handling is usually efficient if the path is diagnosed quickly. Don't spend too much budget — the underlying fear (usually fear of failing again) is what matters and surfaces in the fear extraction.

### Special: when the prospect lists multiple prior failures

> "I've tried coaches, masterminds, courses, books, a therapist — nothing works."

This is usually a tell that the variable is the prospect, not the interventions. The move:

> "That's a lot of attempts. Let me ask — what's the common variable across all of them?"

If they don't say "me," guide them:

> "You. You're the constant. Which means the variable that needs to change is something about you and how you engage with the work, not the work itself. What if the next intervention doesn't work — same as the others — because you're going to bring the same pattern to it? What would have to change in YOU for this to work?"

Direct but respectful. Reveals the actual variable. Most prospects who present this way are ready to hear it.

### Adjacent: "What makes you different from the other coaches I've worked with?"

Variant of TRIED BEFORE. The move:

> "Honest answer — depends on what didn't work with the others. Tell me what was missing, and I'll tell you whether what I do addresses that gap. If not, I'll tell you that too."

This converts the question from "sell me on you" to "match what you offer to my specific gap." Premium positioning.
