## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "It wasn't the right time"
- "The timing was off"
- "I was waiting for the right moment"
- "The market was bad"
- "It was during the recession"
- "The economy was uncertain"
- "Things were too volatile"
- "I wanted to wait until things stabilized"
- "It wasn't the right quarter / year for that kind of investment"

### Present-tense (active)

- "Now's not the right time"
- "Maybe in a few months"
- "I want to wait until things calm down"
- "The economy is shaky right now"
- "We might be heading into a recession"
- "I want to see how the next quarter plays out"
- "Things are too uncertain right now"
- "I'd rather wait for a clearer market"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Deferral disguised as strategic patience
- External conditions as cover for internal hesitation
- Waiting for a hypothetical "right time" that never arrives
- Identity as "the prudent one who times decisions well"
- Using macro uncertainty to defer micro decisions
- Conflating "uncertainty" with "risk that should prevent action"
- Genuine consideration of market conditions (rare in this context)
- Sometimes: legitimate seasonal/business-cycle reality

---

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE

This excuse is closely related to RISK and OVERCOMMITMENT but has a specific mechanism: **the prospect is waiting for external conditions to be right before acting on something internal.**

The fatal flaw in this logic: identity-level work has no relationship to market conditions. The economy doesn't determine whether you fix the pattern of being unable to hire VPs. The recession doesn't dictate whether your marriage improves. The macro environment is irrelevant to whether you do the internal work.

**The reframe surfaces this disconnect.** "You're waiting for conditions in [external thing] to be right before you fix something in [internal thing]. Those aren't related. The economy can't be the gate on whether you address your own patterns."

For prospects who genuinely operate in macro-sensitive businesses, there may be legitimate timing considerations for business decisions. But identity work is decoupled. The closer's job is to clarify that distinction.

## SCRIPT PATH

For TIMING, the most leveraged letters are **S, C, P, and T**. Specifically and Point-in-time are critical.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say it wasn't the right time — what specifically about the timing was wrong?"

**Present:** "When you say it's not the right time — what specifically needs to change for it to be the right time?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate the specific conditions that would make timing "right." Most can't. The vagueness reveals that "right time" is a feeling, not a measurable threshold.

If they CAN get specific (e.g., "after the Q4 close" or "once interest rates drop below X%"), that's real consideration and needs different handling.

### C — Compared to what (CRITICAL — deploy immediately after S)

**Either tense:** "Compared to what? Compared to the perfect timing that doesn't exist? Compared to next year's uncertainty?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can name a moment when timing WOULD be right. Most prospects who use "timing" as an excuse can't. There's no future moment that would feel like the right time — because the timing isn't actually the issue.

### P — Point in time (CRITICAL for this excuse)

**Past:** "How long have you been waiting for the right time? Months? Years?"

**Present:** "How long have you been telling yourself this isn't the right time?"

**What you're listening for:** The chronicity. Most "timing" prospects have been waiting for the right time for years. The duration itself is the reframe — chronic waiting means the timing concept is a defense, not a strategy.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through your timing thinking back then. What were you waiting for?"

**Present:** "Walk me through what 'right timing' would actually look like. What's the specific scenario?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they have a real timing strategy or just a vague preference for "later." Real strategies have specific milestones. Excuse-timing is open-ended deferral.

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to wait for the right time? Has timing always been your hesitation pattern?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces a parent who taught patience as wisdom, an early experience of acting too soon and being burned, or a cultural pattern of measured decision-making that's become avoidant.

### I — Involved (rarely needed)

Skip unless they reference advisors recommending the wait.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — "Right timing" as indefinite deferral (most common)

**Most common case.** The prospect has been waiting for the right time for an extended period with no specific milestone. "Right timing" is a feeling that never arrives because the actual hesitation is internal, not external.

**How to recognize:**

- Multi-year wait for "right timing"
- Can't name a specific scenario that would make timing right
- Each "wrong" timing moment is replaced by a new "wrong" timing reason
- Uses general macro conditions to defer specific decisions
- The timing concern doesn't match the type of decision being deferred (using economic uncertainty to defer identity work)

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "If the economy was booming right now and you had perfect macro conditions, would you do this? Honest answer."
- "What's been the pattern — does the 'right time' ever arrive, or does each year bring a new reason it's not the right time?"
- "When was the last time the timing felt right for a significant decision? What conditions made it feel right then?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Identity-level work and market conditions — what's the connection between them? Does the economy determine whether you address your own patterns?"

- "If you'd done this work two years ago — through whatever timing conditions existed then — where would you be today? Has the timing concern actually protected you, or has it cost you?"

- "What's the actual right time for fixing a problem that's been costing you for years? Now, or later?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Tree Planting Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "There's an old saying: the best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now. Why do you think people use that phrase?"

After they answer:

> "How does that apply to what you've been waiting on?"

Why this lands: Universal, immediately understood. Reveals that the cost of waiting compounds. The prospect can construct that they've already missed the "best" time and the question is whether to act now or miss it again.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Health Decision Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "If a doctor told you 'you have a slow-growing condition that will worsen over the next five years, the treatment is hard but works' — would you say 'let me wait for a better time in the economy to treat this'?"

After they answer:

> "What's the difference? Why is the timing logic different here?"

Why this lands: Reveals that "timing" makes sense for some decisions but not others. Health decisions get done when the condition exists, not when external conditions are favorable. Identity work follows the same logic.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Perfect Conditions Trap** — **Grade: A**

> "When was the last time conditions were objectively perfect? Looking back, the markets always had something to worry about — election uncertainty, recession fears, inflation, geopolitical tension. Was there ever a perfect moment that you waited for and acted on?"

After they answer (almost always no):

> "What does that tell you about waiting for perfect conditions?"

Why this lands: Forces them to acknowledge that "perfect timing" is a myth. Successful people don't wait for perfect — they act despite imperfect. Reveals the timing-wait as a category error.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Compound Cost Reframe** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "Every year you've waited for the right time, what's happened to the problem you're trying to solve? Has it gotten better while you've been waiting, or worse?"

After they answer:

> "What does that math actually look like over the next two years if the pattern continues?"

Why this lands: Activates their math brain. The cost of waiting isn't zero — it's compounding. Reveals that "waiting" is actually choosing the worsening trajectory.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "So 'right timing' has been the way you've deferred the decision. Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

### PATH B — Real macro / market consideration relevant to their specific business

**Less common but real.** Some prospects operate in genuinely macro-sensitive businesses where market timing affects their capacity to invest. Real estate, public markets, certain B2B sales cycles.

**How to recognize:**

- Specific business cycle that affects revenue
- Can articulate exact conditions that would make this a stretch vs. easy
- The timing concern is about specific cash flow timing, not generalized "economy"
- Calm energy, not avoidant
- They have an actual strategy, not a vague preference

**Follow-up questions:**

- "What specifically about your business cycle makes the timing tight right now?"
- "When does the cycle turn? What's the realistic window?"
- "If we did this work over the next 90 days, would the business cycle affect your ability to engage fully, or just your ability to pay the fee?"

**The move:**

This is genuine consideration. Honor it. Two options:

**Option 1 — Defer with a specific date:**

> "Okay. If [specific business event] happens in [specific timeframe], let's get back on a call then. I'll send you a calendar invite. Between now and then, here's what I'd want you to think about..."

**Option 2 — Restructure to fit the cycle:**

> "What if we structured the payment to align with [their cycle]? Half up front when we start, half when [specific event] hits?"

This honors the real timing without losing the prospect to indefinite deferral.

**Transition for Path B:**

If deferred: set firm follow-up, end call.

If restructured: proceed with adjusted close.

### PATH C — Economy / recession as generalized hesitation

**Common during economic uncertainty periods.** The prospect uses "the economy" or "the recession" as a reason without it actually affecting their personal situation. The macro narrative provides socially acceptable cover for internal hesitation.

**How to recognize:**

- References "the economy" or "the recession" generically
- Their personal financial situation isn't actually affected
- Can't articulate specific way the macro affects this decision
- Often references news cycles, economic forecasts
- Doesn't actually run their business based on macro projections

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How is your business actually affected by the current economic conditions?"
- "Has the economy changed your spending patterns on other things, or just on investing in yourself?"
- "If we set the economy aside as something that has always been uncertain and will always be uncertain — what's the real reason for the hesitation?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "Look at the last 20 years of headlines about the economy. Recession fears, election uncertainty, market crashes, recovery, more fears. Has there been a single year that didn't have major economic concerns somewhere?"

- "The successful people you know — do they wait for clean economic conditions before they invest in growth? Or do they invest specifically because growth in down cycles becomes a competitive advantage?"

- "What's the relationship between the macro economy and your specific identity work? Does the Fed's interest rate policy actually affect your VP hiring patterns?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Counter-Cyclical Investment** — **Grade: A**

> "Some of the most successful business moves happen in downturns — companies that double down on growth when competitors freeze. Bezos founded Amazon during a recession. Why do you think that's a pattern?"

After they answer:

> "What would it mean to apply that pattern to your own growth right now?"

Why this lands: Reframes "bad economy" from threat to opportunity. Activates the prospect's awareness that timing-based hesitation may actually be the wrong strategy.

**ANALOGY 2 — The News Cycle Question** — **Grade: A**

> "Imagine a wealthy investor who decides what to invest in based on what the news cycle says about the economy. Would you trust their judgment?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've been letting the news cycle determine your investments in yourself?"

Why this lands: Forces them to see that they're letting media-driven anxiety drive personal decisions. Reveals the absurdity when applied to financial decisions they'd never make that way.

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "Okay. So the economy hasn't actually been the issue — it's been a story you've been telling yourself. Setting that aside, what else has been in the way?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after Path A or C reframe:

> "Aside from the timing piece, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Variants:

- "Beyond waiting for the right moment — what else?"
- "Setting timing aside as not the real issue — what else?"

For Path B: defer or restructure, no sweep.

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 TIMING / ECONOMY / RECESSION phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. Present SCRIPT options with S, C, P highlighted
3. After SCRIPT, sim's response determines path (A indefinite deferral, B real cycle, C macro narrative)
4. Load matched reframe card
5. Closer deploys reframe
6. Check for concession
7. Sweep transition or defer

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm looking at the macro picture carefully. With current uncertainty, I'm being measured about new commitments."
- "The economic indicators suggest waiting is the prudent call."
- "I prefer to act with high conviction, and right now the timing signals are mixed."

Type A defends with analytical-sounding economic framing. Usually Path A or C. The Compound Cost Reframe and Perfect Conditions Trap work well — they activate the math brain.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Things just feel really uncertain right now."
- "With the recession everyone's talking about, I want to be careful."
- "Maybe in a few months when things calm down."

Type B defends with emotional uncertainty framing. Usually Path C. The News Cycle Question lands well. Lead with warmth.

### Voice tone notes

- Don't dismiss the economic concern as if it's not real
- Acknowledge macro uncertainty exists, then redirect to the specific question
- The Counter-Cyclical Investment reframe activates ambition — useful for high-performers
- After participatory analogies, hold silence

### Friction budget notes

TIMING / ECONOMY can take moderate budget because it sounds reasonable and prospects defend it as wisdom. Don't try to overpower with multiple reframes — the Compound Cost or Tree Planting moves are usually sufficient. Get the reframe to land and sweep.

### Special: when the prospect references specific economic data

> "Interest rates are at X. Inflation is at Y. Unemployment numbers are concerning."

This can be Path A in disguise (using data to defer) or genuine Path B. The diagnostic:

> "Specifically — how does [the data they cited] affect your decision about this identity work? What's the mechanism by which that data should determine whether you fix your own patterns?"

If they can articulate a real mechanism: Path B handling.
If they can't: Path A — the data is decoration on indefinite deferral.

### Adjacent: "I want to wait until after [specific event]"

Variants: "after the election," "after Q4," "after the holidays," "after Jan 1."

The move:

> "Okay. And after that event — what will be different that makes this the right time? Will the pattern you're trying to address be solved by then? Or will you have a new reason it's not the right time after that?"

Reveals whether the event is genuine milestone or just the next "not yet" goalpost.

### Special: when prospect uses age as timing

> "I'm 55, maybe it's too late for this kind of change."

Variant in Path A. The move:

> "Late compared to what? Late compared to the version of you at 65 who's still where you are now? Late compared to the average person who quit on themselves in their 50s? You're not the average person — you booked this call. What does 'late' actually mean for someone like you?"

Activates that "late" is a story. Successful 50+ year-olds who reinvent themselves are evidence the story is wrong.
