## LAYER 1 — HOW PROSPECT PHRASES IT

### Past-tense (dominant)

- "I didn't know what to do"
- "Nobody told me"
- "I didn't have anyone showing me the way"
- "I didn't know where to start"
- "I wasn't sure what the next step was"
- "I never had a mentor"
- "Nobody put a plan together for me"
- "I didn't know how to fix it"
- "I just didn't know"

### Present-tense (active)

- "I don't really know what to do"
- "Nobody's shown me a clear path"
- "I'm not sure what the next step is"
- "I need someone to lay it out for me"
- "I don't know where to start"

### What's underneath (Layer 2 patterns to listen for)

- Externalization — placing the responsibility for figuring it out on others
- Waiting for someone to come along and rescue them
- Conflating "I didn't know" with "no information was available"
- Avoiding the work of being resourceful
- Identity as "the one who needs to be shown"
- Genuine information gap (rare in the AI age)
- Passive learning posture inherited from school / employment
- Sometimes: actual specific information they couldn't have easily accessed

## CRITICAL FRAMING NOTE — GROUNDED IN TRANSCRIPTS

This excuse appears explicitly in the Itai/Tristan transcripts as a clearly-named external. From Itai's Sales Framework full transcript (at 20:14):

> "So they'll say something like, 'I didn't have the time. I didn't have the money. I was busy raising my kids. Life got in the way. The economy is bad. We're in a recession. **I didn't know what to do.** These programs look like a scam.'"

Tristan elaborates in transcript 6 (at 49:23-49:53):

> "What I really hear them saying is that **someone should have stopped what they were doing and told them**, which means they're putting the responsibility of getting into a better position in the hands of someone else."

The reframe Tristan uses (transcript 6 at 50:18) is the "nice versus kind" approach — putting an arm around the prospect's shoulder rather than shaming them. From Tristan in transcript 4 (at 33:50-34:25):

> "Saying they didn't take the responsibility can easily come off as like, 'Oh, I'm dumb. You think you're better than me.' [...] So the nice thing for me to do would be to just let you continue. [...] The kind thing for me to do is to pull you aside, tell you [...] because I care about you. [...] So do you want me to be nice or you want to be kind on this call?"

This framing matters: the closer must surface the externalization without making the prospect feel attacked or dumb. The arm-around-the-shoulder positioning is what makes the reframe land.

## SCRIPT PATH

For I DIDN'T KNOW, the most leveraged letters are **S, T, P, and a unique responsibility question**. The reframe here is specifically about converting external to internal responsibility.

### S — Specifically (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Past:** "When you say you didn't know what to do — what specifically didn't you know? Be precise."

**Present:** "When you say you don't know what to do — what specifically are you trying to figure out?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they can articulate concrete information gaps or whether "I didn't know" is generalized. Most can't get specific — which is the diagnostic. If they had been seeking, they'd know what they were seeking.

### T — Tell me the story

**Past:** "Walk me through what you actually did to try to figure it out. What did you read, watch, ask, search?"

**Present:** "Walk me through what you've done so far to figure this out. What's the process you've used?"

**What you're listening for:** Whether they actively sought information or passively waited. Often the answer reveals they did very little — they were waiting for the information to come to them rather than seeking it.

### P — Point in time

**Past:** "How long have you been not-knowing this? Months? Years?"

**What you're listening for:** The duration of the not-knowing reveals whether it was a brief gap (legitimate) or a chronic state (passive waiting). Most prospects have been "not knowing" for years while information was available to them.

### THE RESPONSIBILITY QUESTION (unique to this excuse — deploy directly)

**Either tense:** "When you say you didn't know what to do — whose responsibility was it to find out?"

**What you're listening for:** Their answer reveals the pattern. Most will pause. Some will say "mine" honestly. Some will try to dodge. The pause itself is the work.

Tristan's verbatim move (transcript 6, 49:31): "Internal responsibility or external responsibility?"

### R — Roots (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Where did you learn to wait for someone to show you the way? Has that been a pattern in your life — waiting to be told what to do?"

**What you're listening for:** Often surfaces school conditioning (waiting for the teacher to assign the work), employment conditioning (waiting for the boss to give direction), or family dynamic (parent who provided too much / not enough direction).

### I — Involved (deploy when relevant)

**Either tense:** "Who, specifically, were you waiting to tell you what to do?"

**What you're listening for:** Often reveals the prospect was waiting for some unspecified authority figure who was never going to materialize. The phantom-helper they've been waiting for.

### C — Compared to what (rarely needed)

Skip in most cases.

## DIAGNOSTIC PATHS

### PATH A — Passive waiting for someone to provide the answer

**Most common case.** The prospect has been waiting for someone — a mentor, an expert, a guide — to come along and lay out the plan. They've conflated "I didn't know" with "no one told me," which is externalization.

**How to recognize:**

- Vague about what they actually did to seek information
- References waiting for the right mentor or teacher
- Treats information as something delivered TO them, not sought BY them
- Often has shelfware (bought programs, didn't engage)
- Passive language ("nobody showed me," "I never had a guide")

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "If you had wanted to know — really wanted to know — what could you have done over the last [duration] to find out? Be specific."
- "What's the difference between not knowing because no information exists, and not knowing because you weren't looking?"
- "Whose responsibility is it to figure out how to fix your own life?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

The Tristan-style "nice versus kind" entry:

> "Hey, can I challenge you on this — but kindly? The nice thing for me to do is to just nod and let you continue. But the kind thing — the thing someone who actually cares about you would do — is to pull you aside and tell you the truth. Can I be kind right now?"

After they agree:

> "What I'm hearing when you say 'I didn't know what to do' is that you were waiting for someone to stop what they were doing and tell you. Which means you've been putting the responsibility for fixing your own life in someone else's hands. Does that land?"

**Participatory analogies (graded for high-earning ICP):**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Google Reframe (Tristan, verbatim from transcript)** — **Grade: A**

> "Let me ask you something. We're in 2026. We have AI. We have Google. We have podcasts. We have unlimited free information. If you'd really wanted to know how to solve this problem over the last [duration] — what could you have actually done?"

After they answer:

> "So the information was available. What got in the way of you finding it?"

Why this lands: Tristan deploys this verbatim because it works. Reveals that "I didn't know" hasn't been a real condition since the 90s. Information is abundant. The prospect's failure to find it wasn't a lack of availability — it was a lack of seeking. The prospect has to construct that on their own.

**ANALOGY 2 — The Self-Made Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "Think about the most successful people you respect. Did they wait for someone to come and tell them what to do? Or did they go figure it out, even when nobody was holding their hand?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've been approaching your own growth?"

Why this lands: Activates their reference points for success. Successful people don't wait — they hunt. The prospect has been holding themselves to a standard their heroes didn't meet.

**ANALOGY 3 — The Employee vs. Owner Mindset** — **Grade: A for Type A**

> "There's a difference between an employee mindset and an owner mindset. The employee waits to be told what to do. The owner figures out what needs to be done. Which mindset have you been operating with regarding this problem?"

After they answer:

> "You're an owner in your business. What's been different about how you've owned the problem of your own growth?"

Why this lands: Activates the CEO identity. Prospects who own businesses don't tolerate employee-mindset behavior from themselves in business, but often operate with employee-mindset around personal growth. The contradiction is the lever.

**ANALOGY 4 — The Resourcefulness Reframe (extrapolated from Tristan)** — **Grade: A**

> "If your kid was struggling in school and you wanted to help them, would you wait for someone to come and tell you how? Or would you research, ask, hunt, find the answer?"

After they answer:

> "What's been different about how you've handled your own struggles?"

Why this lands: Reveals the double standard — they'd be relentless for someone they love but passive for themselves. The prospect has to confront that they don't apply to themselves what they'd apply for their kids.

**Transition language after Path A reframe:**

> "Okay. So 'I didn't know' was really 'I wasn't looking.' Setting that aside, what else has been keeping you from getting where you wanted to go?"

### PATH B — Genuine specific information that wasn't accessible

**Rare but possible.** Some prospects have a specific information gap that wasn't easily addressable — niche expertise, specialized knowledge, something that required a specific advisor. This is real.

**How to recognize:**

- Can articulate the specific information they needed
- The information was genuinely specialized (not "how do I grow my business" but something narrow)
- They did try to find it, but the right source wasn't accessible
- Calm, not defensive

**This is closer to qualification than reframe.** Provide what they need to evaluate.

**The move:**

> "Fair. What specifically did you need to know that wasn't accessible? And does this offering provide that information?"

Address the specific gap. If your offer fills it, articulate how. If it doesn't, that's useful information for both of you.

### PATH C — Conditioned passivity from work/school environments

**Common with prospects in corporate backgrounds or who recently left employment.** Years of being told what to do by bosses, teachers, structures. The "I didn't know what to do" pattern is conditioned passivity.

**How to recognize:**

- Long history in structured environments (corporate, military, academic)
- R question surfaces clear conditioning origin
- Pattern is pervasive (not just this domain)
- Often genuine frustration with their own passivity once it's named

**Follow-up questions to confirm:**

- "How long were you in [structured environment]?"
- "What was the pattern there — were you rewarded for waiting to be told, or for going and figuring things out?"
- "When you left that environment, did the pattern come with you?"

**Reframe questions (participatory):**

- "You spent [X years] in an environment that rewarded waiting to be told. That conditioning is real. AND you're not in that environment anymore. The pattern that protected you there is limiting you now. What would it look like to update?"

- "If you'd been raised hunting for your own answers — instead of waiting to be assigned them — what would your life look like right now?"

- "What if 'I didn't know what to do' isn't actually true — it's just the inherited pattern of waiting for instruction?"

**Participatory analogies:**

**ANALOGY 1 — The Corporate Conditioning Reframe** — **Grade: A**

> "In corporate environments, you got rewarded for waiting for direction and executing well. That conditioning makes sense in that context — but it's terrible for entrepreneurship or for personal growth. You can't be your own boss with an employee mindset. Where in your life are you still operating like an employee?"

After they answer:

> "What would the owner-mindset version of you do about this?"

Why this lands: Names the structural origin of the pattern without blaming the prospect. The conditioning was adaptive in one environment and maladaptive in another. Activates the update as the move.

**Transition language after Path C reframe:**

> "So the 'I didn't know' was conditioned passivity from environments where waiting was rewarded. Now you're in environments where it's costly. Setting that aside, what else has been in the way?"

## SWEEP TRANSITION (TO NEXT EXTERNAL)

Default language after any path:

> "Aside from the 'didn't know what to do' piece, what else has been keeping you stuck?"

Variants:

- "Beyond not having a clear path — what else?"
- "Setting that aside as something you can actually solve through resourcefulness — what else?"

## SIM BEHAVIOR NOTES

### When the prospect says any Layer 1 I DIDN'T KNOW phrase, the sim should:

1. Load this file as active reframe context
2. **Flag this as an externalization excuse** — the reframe is specifically about converting external responsibility to internal
3. Present SCRIPT options with S, T, P, and the responsibility question highlighted
4. Deploy the "nice versus kind" entry before the reframe — this softens the challenge
5. Based on response, diagnose path (A passive waiting, B genuine gap, C conditioned passivity)
6. Load matched reframe card
7. Closer deploys reframe with arm-around-shoulder tone
8. Check for concession
9. Sweep transition

### Type A prospect variation

When playing Type A, the sim's responses sound like:

- "I'm normally pretty good at finding answers, but for this specific problem, I haven't found a clear path forward."
- "I've tried various resources but none of them addressed my specific situation."
- "I would have done something if I'd known what the right move was."

Type A often presents this as a specific information problem (looks like Path B but is usually Path A in disguise). The Google Reframe usually cracks it open. The Owner vs Employee Reframe activates their identity.

### Type B prospect variation

When playing Type B, the sim's responses sound like:

- "Nobody ever showed me how."
- "I've been waiting for the right person to come along."
- "I don't have mentors like other people do."

Type B presents more openly as Path A passive waiting. The Resourcefulness Reframe lands well — they would hunt for their kids but not themselves. The Self-Made Reframe also works.

### Voice tone notes — CRITICAL

- **Deploy "nice versus kind" before the challenge** — this is non-negotiable for this excuse
- Don't shame the prospect for not knowing — name the pattern with warmth
- Use "us" and "we" language where possible ("we all do this," "we get used to waiting")
- After participatory analogies, let silence sit — the realization "I was waiting" is uncomfortable
- The arm-around-the-shoulder positioning makes the reframe land

### Friction budget notes

I DIDN'T KNOW can take moderate budget because the challenge directly attacks responsibility. The nice-versus-kind framing reduces friction. Don't pile reframes — the Google Reframe is usually sufficient. Get it to land and sweep.

### Special: when the prospect references AI / Google specifically

> "I've Googled it but the answers are all generic."

The move:

> "Fair. What specifically were you searching for? Because 'how do I grow my business' returns generic answers. 'How do I overcome the specific identity pattern that's been preventing me from hiring senior people for the last two years' returns much more specific results. Were you asking the generic question or the specific one?"

Reveals whether they were genuinely seeking or surface-searching. Most have been doing the latter.

### Adjacent: "I just need someone to show me the path"

Variant of the same excuse, framed as a request rather than past justification. The move:

> "Sure. AND — and this matters — even if I show you the path, you're the one who has to walk it. If you've been waiting for someone to show you the path, you may not just be missing information. You may be missing the muscle of walking the path even when nobody's holding your hand. Which one is actually the gap?"

This converts the deflection into the actual question: is it information or is it agency?

### Special: when the responsibility question lands hard

The responsibility question ("whose responsibility was it?") can produce a somatic shift. If it does, the prospect may name the externalization themselves: "I guess it was mine." If this happens:

> "Stay with that for a second. What does it mean that you've been treating fixing your life as someone else's job to give you the manual on?"

Then hold silence. This is often where the deeper fear surfaces — usually fear of being responsible for outcomes if they actually try.
